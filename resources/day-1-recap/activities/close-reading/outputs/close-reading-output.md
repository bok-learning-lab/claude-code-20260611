# Close Reading: Hamlet 3.1.64–96, "To be, or not to be"

*Hamlet*, Act 3, Scene 1, lines 64–96 (First Folio text)

---

## The Passage

> To be, or not to be, that is the question,
> Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer
> The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
> Or to take arms against a sea of troubles,
> And by opposing end them? To die: to sleep;
> No more; and by a sleep to say we end
> The heart-ache and the thousand natural shocks
> That flesh is heir to, 'tis a consummation
> Devoutly to be wish'd. To die, to sleep;
> To sleep: perchance to dream: ay, there's the rub;
> For in that sleep of death what dreams may come
> When we have shuffled off this mortal coil,
> Must give us pause: there's the respect
> That makes calamity of so long life;
> For who would bear the whips and scorns of time,
> The oppressor's wrong, the proud man's contumely,
> The pangs of despised love, the law's delay,
> The insolence of office and the spurns
> That patient merit of the unworthy takes,
> When he himself might his quietus make
> With a bare bodkin? who would fardels bear,
> To grunt and sweat under a weary life,
> But that the dread of something after death,
> The undiscover'd country from whose bourn
> No traveller returns, puzzles the will
> And makes us rather bear those ills we have
> Than fly to others that we know not of?
> Thus conscience does make cowards of us all;
> And thus the native hue of resolution
> Is sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought,
> And enterprises of great pitch and moment
> With this regard their currents turn awry,
> And lose the name of action.

## Close Reading

**Thesis:** The famous "To be" soliloquy is not primarily a meditation on suicide but a philosophical demonstration of the very paralysis it describes. Its argument performs its conclusion: the soliloquy begins as deliberation, moves through a rhetorical catalog of life's injuries, and folds back on itself at the word "conscience" — not advancing toward resolution but enacting, in real time, the mechanism by which thought arrests action.

---

### Syntax as Argument: The Unfinished Sentence

The soliloquy opens in a posture of logical rigor: "To be, or not to be, that is the question." The line announces itself as a formal dilemma — an either/or, the basic structure of argumentation. But the promised argument never arrives. The question is never answered. Instead, the syntax that follows enacts a different kind of motion: lists, appositives, digressions, hedges, and interruptions. By 3.1.91, "Thus conscience does make cowards of us all," the grammatical subject has shifted from "I" to "us all," and the speaker has dissolved into a universal we. Hamlet cannot finish a thought about himself because he cannot disentangle himself from the thought.

This formal enactment is what makes the soliloquy philosophically serious rather than merely rhetorical. It is not that Hamlet *says* thought prevents action; it is that his saying it prevents action.

---

### Prosody: Meter as Symptom

The passage is in blank verse — unrhymed iambic pentameter — but the meter is conspicuously irregular in ways that track the argument's instability. The opening line, "To be, or not to be, that is the question," runs eleven syllables with a feminine ending on "question," unsettling the solidity of the line's own claim to definiteness. Several subsequent lines similarly defer closure. "No more; and by a sleep to say we end" (3.1.69) produces a mid-line caesura after "No more" that isolates the negation: it sounds, for a beat, like a resolution before the line extends. "Ay, there's the rub" (3.1.73) introduces a colloquial interjection into the philosophical register — an affective exclamation in the middle of would-be logic.

The most metrically loaded moment is the inventory at 3.1.78–82:

> For who would bear the **whips** and **scorns** of time,  
> The oppressor's **wrong**, the proud man's **contumely**,  
> The pangs of despised love, the law's **delay**,  
> The **insolence** of office and the **spurns**  
> That patient merit of the unworthy takes...

The stressed monosyllables — *whips*, *scorns*, *wrong*, *spurns* — fall as percussive blows, mimicking the very injuries cataloged. The unusual word "contumely" (contemptuous insult) is notably four syllables and metrically excessive, as if the language strains under the weight of accumulated humiliation.

---

### The Problem of "Conscience"

The pivot of the soliloquy is 3.1.91: "Thus conscience does make cowards of us all." The word *conscience* demands careful attention. In modern English, "conscience" means moral consciousness, the inner sense of right and wrong. In early modern usage, it also carried the sense of *consciousness* or *self-awareness* more broadly — Latin *conscientia*, the faculty of knowing one's own mental states. Both meanings are operative here, and the ambiguity is load-bearing.

If conscience means *moral scruple*, then Hamlet is saying that ethical anxiety about the afterlife prevents people from acting on rational self-interest (suicide, revenge). If conscience means *consciousness itself*, then he is making a more radical claim: that reflective self-awareness is inherently paralyzing — that to be a thinking subject is already to be someone who cannot simply act. This reading collapses the distinction between Hamlet-the-neurotic and Hamlet-the-philosopher, and it anticipates, by two centuries, what Hegel and later Kierkegaard will call the dialectic of reflection: the idea that consciousness generates its own opposition to itself.

---

### The Undiscovered Country

The passage's most resonant image is the "undiscover'd country from whose bourn / No traveller returns" (3.1.87–88). The metaphor is colonial: death imagined as unmapped territory, like the New World, beyond the boundaries of known geography. "Bourn" means border or boundary. The image of *returning* sets up an impossible knowledge claim: we cannot know death's nature because no one survives it to report back. This is epistemologically airtight — and it is the reason the argument cannot proceed. The unknown cannot be weighed against the known. Uncertainty, not cowardice, is the bar on action.

Notice that this is *not* a specifically Christian fear of hell or divine judgment (though that reading is available). The dread is of *something* — indefinite, unspecified — "after death." The soliloquy is curiously secular in its anxiety, which distinguishes it from, say, Claudio's fear of death in *Measure for Measure* (3.1), where the imagery is explicitly theological. Hamlet's terror is philosophical before it is religious.

---

### The Collapse at the End

The soliloquy's final movement (3.1.91–96) shifts from argument to self-diagnosis. "The native hue of resolution / Is sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought" — the color metaphor is medical; resolution is imagined as healthy pigmentation, thought as the pallor of illness. "Sicklied o'er" is especially precise: it is not that thought destroys resolution but that it *overlays* it, discolors it without removing the underlying structure. The capacity for action is still there, sick under the surface.

"Enterprises of great pitch and moment / With this regard their currents turn awry / And lose the name of action" — "pitch" here is a falconry term: the height from which a hawk stoops. The image of the current "turning awry" suggests a river diverted, water that cannot reach its destination. Action loses not its substance but its *name* — its identity as action. It becomes something else: deferral, analysis, more thought.

This is the precise mechanism of Hamlet's tragedy. He does not lack will. He is one of the most willful characters in Shakespeare. What he lacks is the capacity to let will operate without reflecting on it — and that reflection is what he, alone among Shakespeare's tragic protagonists, cannot stop.

---

### Dramatic Irony: The Overheard Soliloquy

A final note on staging. The soliloquy is overheard: Polonius and Claudius are concealed behind an arras; Ophelia has been placed in the scene as a decoy. The audience knows this. So we watch Hamlet perform his most private thinking in what he believes is solitude, while we know he is being observed. This irony turns the soliloquy inside out. Hamlet's meditation on the impossibility of decisive action is itself being surveilled — instrumentalized as evidence for his enemies. Even his philosophy is not his own. The "undiscovered country" of his inner life is being mapped by Claudius before Hamlet can speak a word in his own defense.
