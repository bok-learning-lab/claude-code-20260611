### Q11 — Steak: Sear + Low Oven (10 pts)

**Model answer:**

During searing, the dominant mode of heat transfer to the steak surface is **conduction** — direct contact between the steak and the very hot cast-iron pan. The pan surface can exceed 250°C, which is well above the threshold for the Maillard reaction (~140°C), generating the flavor compounds and crust that define a well-seared steak. There is also some convection from any oil or butter in the pan, and minor radiation from the incandescent metal.

During oven finishing, the dominant mode is **convection** from hot circulating air, supplemented by radiation from the oven walls. The lower temperature (e.g. 120–150°C) means heat diffuses slowly inward through the steak — the thermal diffusion timescale τ = R²/α governs how long it takes the center to equilibrate — but without driving the surface far above the target internal temperature.

The combination works because **Maillard browning requires a very hot, dry surface**, which the pan provides, while **even interior doneness requires slow, gentle heat**, which a low oven provides. Cooking the steak entirely at high oven temperature achieves surface browning but also overcooks a thick band of exterior meat before the center is done. Separating the two functions — surface crust from searing, interior doneness from the oven — exploits the different physics of each heat source.

**Key points to award credit:** conduction in pan / convection in oven (2 pts); Maillard reaction requires high surface temperature (2 pts); thermal diffusion time controls interior doneness (2 pts); explanation of why combination is better than high-oven-only (4 pts).

### Q12 — Ganache: Why It Goes Grainy Overnight (10 pts)

**Model answer:**

Freshly made ganache is a **metastable oil-in-water emulsion**: tiny fat droplets (cocoa butter and cream fat) are suspended in an aqueous continuous phase (water from the cream), stabilized by lecithin (a phospholipid emulsifier naturally present in chocolate) adsorbed at the interface.

Over time, two interrelated processes destabilize it. First, **cocoa butter crystallization**: cocoa butter exists in multiple polymorphic crystal forms (Forms I–VI), and at room temperature the less stable, lower-melting forms slowly convert to more stable, higher-melting forms (especially Form V). This transition is a **diffusion-mediated process** — fatty acid chains must reorient and pack into tighter crystal lattices. As cocoa butter solidifies into larger crystals, fat is withdrawn from the emulsion structure, the smooth interface breaks down, and the texture becomes coarse and grainy.

Second, **emulsion instability**: as crystals grow, fat droplets can coalesce; the lecithin layer at the interface becomes less effective at preventing contact between droplets. The result is **partial phase separation** — pockets of solid fat and free liquid water — visible as the grainy, thickened texture.

**Key points to award credit:** ganache identified as emulsion (2 pts); cocoa butter crystallization named as mechanism (3 pts); diffusion link (slow molecular rearrangement / crystal growth) (2 pts); consequences for texture explained (2 pts); lecithin role mentioned (1 pt).

### Q13 — Honey vs. Water: Viscosity and Temperature (10 pts)

**Model answer:**

At 20°C, honey has a viscosity of roughly 10,000 mPa·s — about **10,000 times more viscous than water** (1 mPa·s). The molecular origin of viscosity is **intermolecular friction**: for a fluid to flow, layers of molecules must slide past each other, and the resistance to this sliding is viscosity. In water, molecules are small and the dominant intermolecular forces are relatively weak hydrogen bonds that break and reform easily — water flows readily. In honey, a concentrated solution of fructose, glucose, and longer oligosaccharides, the molecules are larger, more entangled, and form an extensive hydrogen-bonding network with each other and with surrounding water molecules. This network greatly resists shear, producing high viscosity.

Temperature reduces viscosity by supplying enough thermal energy (kT) to break intermolecular bonds and allow molecules to slide past each other. The relationship is approximately Arrhenius: η ∝ exp(Eₐ/RT), where Eₐ is an activation energy for molecular rearrangement. Because honey's viscosity is governed by a dense H-bonding network, its effective Eₐ is much higher than water's. Heating honey from 20°C to 80°C can reduce its viscosity from ~10,000 to ~100 mPa·s — a **100-fold drop**. Water over the same range drops from 1.0 to ~0.35 mPa·s — roughly a **3-fold drop**.

Honey therefore changes far more dramatically than water — both in absolute terms (reduction of thousands of mPa·s vs. fractions of mPa·s) and in relative terms (100x vs. 3x).

**Key points:** molecular origin of viscosity stated (2 pts); honey vs. water viscosity compared with structural explanation (3 pts); temperature mechanism explained (Arrhenius / bond breaking) (3 pts); comparison of magnitude of change (2 pts).

### Q14 — Pectin Gelation: Molecular Mechanism (10 pts)

**Model answer:**

Pectin is a linear polysaccharide (backbone of galacturonic acid units) found in fruit cell walls. In high-methoxyl pectin (the most common type), many of the carboxyl groups along the backbone are esterified with methyl groups. Gelation occurs when pectin chains form physical **cross-links** — non-covalent associations at multiple points along their length — creating a three-dimensional network that traps water and prevents flow.

At the molecular level, the cross-links form via hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic interactions between the methyl ester groups on adjacent chains. Three conditions favor this:

- **Low pH (<3.5):** protonates the remaining free carboxyl groups, neutralizing their negative charges. Without protonation, like charges repel adjacent chains and prevent them from approaching close enough to associate.
- **High sugar concentration (>55%):** sugar molecules are strongly hydrophilic and compete with pectin chains for water. Effectively dehydrating the pectin chains reduces the water layer around them, allowing direct chain–chain interactions.
- **Cooling:** reduces thermal motion. At high temperature, thermal energy prevents the weak non-covalent cross-links from persisting; as the jam cools, thermal fluctuations decrease and cross-links become stable. The gel sets.

**Diffusion plays two roles.** First, pectin chains must diffuse to encounter each other and form junction zones — the rate of gelation is limited by how quickly chains can find each other through Brownian motion. Second, once a gel forms, diffusion of ions and small molecules within the gel network is constrained by the mesh size of the cross-linked network — this is the basis for controlled-release applications in food science.

For low-methoxyl pectin (less common in home cooking): Ca²⁺ ions form ionic cross-links between deprotonated carboxyl groups, and gelation requires lower sugar concentrations.

**Key points:** cross-link formation described (2 pts); pH condition and mechanism (2 pts); sugar condition and mechanism (2 pts); temperature condition (1 pt); diffusion role explained (2 pts); any mention of low-methoxyl pectin / Ca²⁺ alternative (1 pt bonus or trade-off with other points).

### Q16 — Heat Transfer and Cooking Time (10 pts)

**Given:** α = 1.4 × 10⁻⁷ m²/s; formula τ = R²/α

---

**(a)** R = 4 cm = 0.04 m

$$\tau = \frac{R^2}{\alpha} = \frac{(0.04)^2}{1.4 \times 10^{-7}} = \frac{1.6 \times 10^{-3}}{1.4 \times 10^{-7}} = 11{,}429 \text{ s}$$

**Convert to minutes:** 11,429 / 60 ≈ **190 min** (~3.2 hours)

*Note for graders: τ = R²/α gives a characteristic (upper-bound) timescale; real baking times are shorter because full thermal equilibration isn't required. Accept the calculated value; a comment noting this context is worth bonus consideration.*

*(3 pts)*

---

**(b)** R = 1.5 cm = 0.015 m

$$\tau = \frac{(0.015)^2}{1.4 \times 10^{-7}} = \frac{2.25 \times 10^{-4}}{1.4 \times 10^{-7}} = 1{,}607 \text{ s} \approx \textbf{26.8 min}$$

**Scaling insight:** τ ∝ R² — if R is halved, cooking time drops by a factor of 4. Compared to the large potato (190 min), the baby potato cooks ~7× faster (190/27 ≈ 7). This is why cutting vegetables into smaller pieces dramatically reduces cooking time, and why size-uniformity matters for even cooking.

*(4 pts — 2 for calculation, 2 for scaling discussion)*

---

**(c)** Cutting in half reduces the effective radius from R = 4 cm to R' = 2 cm = 0.02 m:

$$\tau' = \frac{(0.02)^2}{1.4 \times 10^{-7}} = \frac{4 \times 10^{-4}}{1.4 \times 10^{-7}} = 2{,}857 \text{ s} \approx 47.6 \text{ min}$$

Compare: original τ = 190 min; new τ = 47.6 min.

Ratio: τ'/τ = (R'/R)² = (0.02/0.04)² = (1/2)² = **1/4**

**The chef is wrong.** Cutting the potato in half reduces cooking time by a factor of **4** (to 25% of original), not a factor of 2. Because τ ∝ R², halving the linear dimension reduces time by the square of that factor.

### Q17 — Viscosity (10 pts)

**Given:** η_oil(20°C) = 84 mPa·s; η_water(20°C) = 1 mPa·s; heating oil from 20→60°C reduces η by factor of 4.

---

**(a)**

$$\eta_{\text{oil}}(60°C) = \frac{84}{4} = \textbf{21 mPa·s}$$

*(2 pts)*

---

**(b)** Use **warm olive oil (60°C, η = 21 mPa·s)** to get smaller droplets.

**Explanation:** When the vinaigrette is shaken, mechanical shear stretches and fragments oil droplets. The size of the resulting droplets depends on the balance between the shear stress driving breakup and the Laplace pressure (2γ/r) resisting it. Lower-viscosity oil deforms more readily under applied shear — it offers less internal resistance to the elongation that precedes breakup — so the same agitation produces smaller droplets. Higher-viscosity cold oil resists deformation, and more energy is needed to reach the same droplet size.

*(4 pts — 1 for correct answer, 3 for physical explanation)*

---

**(c)** The vinaigrette will be **more stable** with Dijon mustard.

Dijon mustard contains mucilage (a polysaccharide) and other surface-active compounds that adsorb to the oil–water interface. This stabilizes the emulsion through two mechanisms:

1. **Lowering interfacial tension (γ):** surfactant molecules at the interface reduce the energetic cost of maintaining a large surface area between oil and water, slowing coalescence.
2. **Steric/mechanical barrier:** adsorbed particles and polymers form a physical layer around each droplet that prevents two droplets from merging even when they collide — a form of Pickering stabilization.

Together these mean that oil droplets persist for much longer without coalescing, and the vinaigrette remains emulsified after shaking for minutes to hours rather than seconds.

*(4 pts — 1 for correct answer, 2 for interfacial mechanism, 1 for mustard-specific explanation)*
