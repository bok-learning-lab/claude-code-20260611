Q11 — Steak: Sear + Low Oven

During searing, the dominant heat transfer mode is conduction — direct contact between steak and hot cast-iron. Pan surfaces exceed 250°C, well above the Maillard reaction threshold (~140°C), generating the crust and flavor compounds. Some convection from oil/butter in the pan also occurs.

During oven finishing, the dominant mode is convection from circulating hot air, supplemented by radiation from oven walls. Lower temperature means heat diffuses slowly inward (timescale τ = R²/α) without driving the surface far above the target internal temperature.

The combination works because Maillard browning requires a very hot, dry surface (the pan's job), while even interior doneness requires slow, gentle heat (the oven's job). High-oven-only cooking overcooks a thick exterior band before the center is done.

Conduction in pan / convection in oven (2 pts) · Maillard at high surface temp (2 pts) · τ = R²/α governs interior (2 pts) · Why combination beats high-oven-only (4 pts)

Q12 — Ganache Goes Grainy Overnight

Fresh ganache is a metastable oil-in-water emulsion: fat droplets (cocoa butter + cream fat) suspended in aqueous phase (water from cream), stabilized by lecithin adsorbed at the interface.

Overnight: cocoa butter crystallization — cocoa butter has polymorphic crystal forms (I–VI); at room temperature, less stable forms slowly convert to more stable ones (Form V). This is a diffusion-mediated process (fatty acid chains reorient into tighter lattices). As cocoa butter solidifies into larger crystals, fat is withdrawn from the emulsion, the interface breaks down → coarse, grainy texture.

Simultaneously, emulsion instability: growing crystals allow droplet coalescence; lecithin becomes less effective → partial phase separation of solid fat and free liquid water → thick, grainy appearance.

Ganache as emulsion (2 pts) · Cocoa butter crystallization named (3 pts) · Diffusion link / slow molecular rearrangement (2 pts) · Textural consequences (2 pts) · Lecithin role (1 pt)

Q13 — Honey vs. Water: Viscosity and Temperature

Honey (~10,000 mPa·s) is about 10,000× more viscous than water (1 mPa·s). Viscosity arises from intermolecular friction — resistance as molecular layers slide past each other. Water is small, mobile; honey is a concentrated fructose/glucose solution with extensive hydrogen bonding that resists shear.

Temperature supplies thermal energy (kT) to break intermolecular bonds; viscosity follows an Arrhenius-like relation: η ∝ exp(Eₐ/RT). Honey's dense H-bonding network has a much higher effective Eₐ than water.

Warming to 80°C: honey drops from ~10,000 to ~100 mPa·s (100× decrease); water drops from 1.0 to ~0.35 mPa·s (~3× decrease). Honey changes far more dramatically — both in absolute and relative terms.

Q14 — Pectin Gelation

Pectin (galacturonic acid backbone) gels when chains form physical cross-links — non-covalent associations creating a 3D network that traps water. For high-methoxyl pectin, three conditions favor this:

  • Low pH (<3.5): protonates carboxyl groups → removes electrostatic repulsion → chains can approach and associate via H-bonds and hydrophobic interactions.
  • High sugar (>55%): dehydrates pectin chains (sugar competes for water) → promotes direct chain–chain interactions.
  • Cooling: reduces thermal motion → weak cross-links become stable → gel sets.

Diffusion roles: (1) pectin chains must diffuse (Brownian motion) to encounter each other and form junction zones — rate of gelation is diffusion-limited; (2) once gelled, diffusion of small molecules is constrained by mesh size.

Q15 — Diffusion  (10 pts)

Given: D = 5 × 10−10 m2/s  |  formula: x = √(2Dt)

(a) t = 1 s (3 pts)

x = √(2 × 5×10−10 × 1) = √(1×10−9)
= 3.16 × 10−5 m
= 3.16 × 10−5 × 106 μm/m
= 31.6 μm

(b) x = 5 mm = 5×10−3 m; solve for t (3 pts)

t = x² / (2D) = (5×10−3)² / (2 × 5×10−10)
= 25×10−6 / 1×10−9
= 25,000 s ≈ 6.9 hours

(c) Target: x = 0.5 mm in t = 30 s (4 pts)

t needed = (5×10−4)² / (2 × 5×10−10) = 2.5×10−7 / 10−9 = 250 s ≈ 4 min
Or: in 30 s, dye diffuses x = √(2 × 5×10−10 × 30) = √(3×10−8) = 0.17 mm only
Not physically reasonable for this dye molecule — requires ~250 s, not 30 s. Award full credit for noting that Ca²⁺ ions (D ≈ 5–10× larger) make real spherification shell formation feasible in practice.

Q16 — Heat Transfer  (10 pts)

Given: α = 1.4 × 10−7 m2/s  |  formula: τ = R²/α

(a) R = 4 cm = 0.04 m (3 pts)

τ = (0.04)² / (1.4×10−7) = 1.6×10−3 / 1.4×10−7 = 11,429 s
≈ 190 min (~3.2 hours)
τ = R²/α is a characteristic (upper-bound) timescale; real baking times are shorter. Accept the calculated value; a comment on this context deserves recognition.

(b) R = 1.5 cm = 0.015 m (4 pts)

τ = (0.015)² / (1.4×10−7) = 2.25×10−4 / 1.4×10−7 = 1,607 s
≈ 26.8 min
Scaling: τ ∝ R² — halving R cuts time by 4×. Baby potato cooks ~7× faster (190/27). This is why uniform piece size matters for even cooking.

(c) R' = R/2 = 2 cm = 0.02 m (3 pts)

τ' = (0.02)² / (1.4×10−7) = 4×10−4 / 1.4×10−7 = 2,857 s ≈ 47.6 min
Ratio: τ'/τ = (R'/R)² = (1/2)² = 1/4
The chef is wrong. Cutting in half reduces cooking time by a factor of 4 (to 25%), not 2. Because τ ∝ R², halving the linear dimension reduces time by (1/2)² = 1/4.

Q17 — Viscosity  (10 pts)

(a) (2 pts)

ηoil(60°C) = 84 / 4 = 21 mPa·s

(b) Use warm oil (60°C, η = 21 mPa·s) for smaller droplets. (4 pts)

Under shear, droplets break when shear stress exceeds Laplace pressure (2γ/r). Lower-viscosity oil deforms more readily — less internal resistance to elongation → same agitation produces smaller droplets. Higher-viscosity cold oil resists deformation; more energy is needed for the same size.

(c) More stable with mustard. (4 pts)

Mustard's mucilage and surface-active compounds adsorb to the oil–water interface: (1) lower interfacial tension γ → reduces driving force for coalescence; (2) steric/mechanical barrier (Pickering stabilization) prevents droplets from merging on collision. Result: droplets persist for minutes to hours rather than seconds.
