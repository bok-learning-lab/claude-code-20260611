# Human Biology, Behavior, and Evolution

The concentration in Human Biology, Behavior, and Evolution (HBBE), situated in the
Department of Human Evolutionary Biology (HEB), allows students to explore all aspects of
human biology from an evolutionary perspective. HBBE engages students to develop
knowledge and skills to investigate questions about how and why humans are the way we are
and how the answers can help solve modern problems related to health, society, and
environment. HBBE concentrators explore human biology at every level, from genes to bodies
to people in their ecological contexts, to answer questions such as:
• What are humans adapted to eat, and how and why have recent changes in human
diet caused by farming and industrialization affected human health?
• Why are humans one of the few species that walks and runs upright? How much
physical activity did we evolve to do and to what extent and why does a habitual lack
of physical activity accelerate aging and increase the risk of some diseases?
• How do variations in gut microbiota caused by eating different diets affect the amount
of energy we get from the food we eat and other aspects of health?
• How did human societies expand from small hunter-gatherer bands to vast nation
states?
• Are culture and language unique to humans? And how do they evolve?
• What are the genetic bases for human traits?
• What makes the human brain and its function different from other species?
• How can an evolutionary perspective be used to address obesity and recent increases
in the prevalence of heart disease, cancers, type 2 diabetes, and other conditions?
• What has been the impact of environmental change on humans in the past and
present?
HBBE provides a general foundation in biology as part of the life sciences cluster of
concentrations and offers a rigorous education in human biology while encouraging
interdisciplinary work. We offer students two options: (1) the general degree in Human
Biology, Behavior, and Evolution and (2) HBBE with the Mind Brain Behavior track. All
students take LS 1A and LS 1B (or their equivalents) for a life science foundation, along with
HEB 10: The Biology of Human Health, and a research seminar for applied learning in the
field.
LEARNING OBJECTIVES
Human Biology, Behavior, and Evolution provides a general foundation in human and
organismic biology as part of the life sciences cluster of concentrations. All HBBE
concentrators receive a core introduction to human biology including physiology, anatomy,
human and nonhuman primate genetics, behavioral biology (including cultural evolution), and
paleontology. HBBE courses also explore interactions between genes and environments and
the co-evolution of genes and culture. We encourage our students to become involved in
research, and we offer many small, advanced courses for students to work intensively with
members of the faculty.
REQUIREMENTS
Human Biology, Behavior, and Evolution
Basic Requirements: 13 courses (52 credits)
1. Required courses:
a. LS 1A and LS 1B or equivalents.

b. HEB 10 (or HEB 97)
c. Three introductory-level HBBE courses (HEB 10-89, LS2, GENED 1027,
GENED 1056), selected from at least three subfields. Students should consult
the HBBE website for subfield designations.
d. Three intermediate- or advanced-level HBBE courses (HEB 91R, HEB 100-
199). At least one of these courses must be a research seminar (HEB 91R or
HEB 100-129). Pre-approved courses from other departments can be found on
the HBBE website.
e. Four additional courses in related fields, to be chosen from Integrative Biology,
Molecular and Cellular Biology, Neurobiology, Chemistry, Physical Sciences,
Math, Statistics, Computer Science, Archaeology, and approved courses from
Psychology, History of Science, and more.
2. Tutorials: None.
3. Thesis: None.
4. General examination: None.
5. Other information:
a. Non-letter Graded: Ordinarily, all courses must be taken for a letter grade
unless approved by a concentration advisor. First-Year Seminars and other
courses that are appropriate for concentration credit and that are graded
SAT/UNS—such as courses taken while studying abroad—may count toward
the concentration with the approval of a concentration advisor.
b. Languages: No requirement.\
For more information on courses, visit the HBBE website:
http://lifesciences.fas.harvard.edu/hbbe
Human Biology, Behavior, and Evolution
Honors Eligibility (Thesis) Requirements: 15 courses (60 credits)
1. Required courses: Same as Basic Requirements.
2. Tutorials: Thesis tutorials HEB 99A and HEB 99B taken in the fall and spring term,

Human Biology, Behavior, and Evolution
Honors (Non-Thesis) Requirements: 15 courses (60 credits)
1. Required courses: Same as Basic Requirements, plus two additional courses in
HBBE or related fields approved in advance by a concentration advisor. These
courses are ordinarily advanced lectures, seminars, or supervised reading courses on
a focused topic.
2. Tutorials: Same as Basic Requirements.
3. Thesis: None.
4. Other information: Same as Basic Requirements. Honors recommendations are

Human Biology, Behavior, and Evolution
Mind Brain Behavior Track
Requirements: 15 courses (60 credits)
The Mind Brain Behavior track within HEB (the MBB track) is geared toward students
interested in human cognition or psychology who want to understand the evolutionary
influences on our thoughts and behaviors. A senior thesis is required for this track.
1. Required courses:
a. LS 1B or equivalent.
b. HEB 10 (or HEB 97)
c. PSY 1 or GENED 1056: Human Nature.
d. Neuro 80: Neurobiology of Behavior, or equivalent.
e. PSY 16 or GENED 1027, plus two courses to be chosen from HEB.
f. Two additional courses in Mind Brain Behavior.
g. Three additional courses in related fields. Qualifying courses are the same as
for Basic Requirements.
2. Tutorials: All letter-graded:
a. One MBB-approved seminar course.
b. Senior year: HEB 99A and HEB 99B (in the fall and spring, respectively),
culminating in the submission of a senior thesis.
3. Thesis: Required. Thesis proposals are ordinarily submitted at the end of junior year.
The written thesis is submitted in March of senior year and is followed by an
(ungraded) oral presentation to the department in the HBBE Thesis Symposium.
4. General examination: None.
5. Other information: Same as Basic Requirements.
RESEARCH
We encourage our students to get involved in research. The department offers many small,
advanced courses for students to work collaboratively with members of the faculty.
Opportunities vary from primarily lab-based research (such as in behavioral endocrinology,
primate and human cognition, evolutionary genetics, physiology, anatomy, or nutrition) to
field-based work (such as studying nonhuman primates or participating in
paleoanthropological excavations). We also encourage our students, if it fits with their specific
interests, to seek outside research including clinical opportunities with co-advising from an
HEB faculty member. Our faculty members work closely with undergraduates on research
projects of all kinds, senior theses, research seminars, and tutorial classes. HEB students
also collaborate with faculty members in the other departments and other schools, such as
Harvard Medical School, in their research and theses.
