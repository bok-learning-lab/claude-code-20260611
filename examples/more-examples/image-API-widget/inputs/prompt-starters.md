# Prompt starters

*Example prompts in the aesthetic this widget is built around. The Studio's placeholder text is the first one. They are starter material — the point is to **adapt them to your own course's source images and historical lineages**, not to use them as-is.*

*Each starter is in the form: a prompt that names a source (Rule 1 — presence of source information), often paired with notes on how to use it dialogically (Rule 2). Adapt by replacing the named source with one from your own course's syllabus.*

---

## Starter 1 — Arcimboldo / Wunderkammer

```
a painting by Arcimboldo, fruit and vegetables forming a face,
Wunderkammer, 16th century
```

The Studio's default placeholder. Names a 16th-century Italian painter (Giuseppe Arcimboldo, court painter to the Habsburgs in Prague) whose composite-portrait paintings — faces made of fruit, vegetables, books, marine creatures — were collected in Rudolf II's *Wunderkammer*. Putting "Wunderkammer" in the prompt brings the cabinet-of-curiosities aesthetic with it: the period light, the dark backgrounds, the catalog-style framing.

Dialogical move: try the same prompt with different surrounding contexts — *"Wunderkammer, 16th century"* vs *"natural history illustration, 19th century"* vs *"art photography, 20th century"* — and watch what travels and what changes. The face stays composite; the *kind* of composite changes.

## Starter 2 — Film still, with a director's lineage named

```
a film still from a Werner Herzog documentary, jungle interior,
analog 16mm grain, conquistador in the foreground looking off-camera,
overcast light
```

Names a director (Herzog), a medium (16mm), a genre (documentary), and a subject lineage (his *Aguirre* / *Fitzcarraldo* preoccupations). The model has seen enough Herzog stills that "Werner Herzog documentary" pulls the visual register: low-angle, weathered, on-location, the kind of light you only get in the field.

Dialogical move: try replacing "Werner Herzog" with another director from the syllabus (Tarkovsky, Akerman, Varda, Reichardt) and notice which features of the image carry over with each name.

## Starter 3 — Book illustration with a specific edition cited

```
an illustration from a 1900 edition of Alice's Adventures in Wonderland,
ink and watercolor, John Tenniel style, the Caterpillar on the mushroom
```

Names the specific work, a specific edition's date, the medium (ink + watercolor), and the historical illustrator (Tenniel). The model knows the Tenniel illustrations as a coherent style, distinct from later Disney or Mervyn Peake versions.

Dialogical move: keep the subject ("the Caterpillar on the mushroom") constant and vary the illustrator/style: *Tenniel* → *Mervyn Peake* → *Yayoi Kusama* → *Saul Bass*. The image stays "Caterpillar on mushroom"; the implicit *century, medium, and visual logic* shifts.

## Starter 4 — Sculpture, naming the institution

```
a marble sculpture in the rotunda of the Vatican Museums, late Hellenistic,
contrapposto stance, drapery, soft museum light from above
```

Names the medium (marble), a specific institutional context (the Vatican Museums rotunda), a period (late Hellenistic), and the formal vocabulary (contrapposto, drapery). The institutional context matters because museum lighting, plinths, the wall behind — these are part of what the model has learned about "marble sculpture."

Dialogical move: try the same Hellenistic subject in different institutional contexts — *the British Museum* vs *the National Archaeological Museum of Athens* vs *a private collector's gallery in 1880*. The same sculpture; different histories of display.

## Starter 5 — Installation art, with year and venue

```
an installation by Olafur Eliasson, 2003, in the Tate Modern Turbine Hall,
a low orange sun, mist, mirrored ceiling, crowd of small silhouetted figures
```

Names the artist, the year (which is doing real work — *Weather Project* opened October 2003), the venue (Turbine Hall), and the formal elements. The model has seen *Weather Project* documentation often enough to pull the specific quality of that installation.

Dialogical move: keep the artist constant and try other works of theirs (*The waterfall*, *Riverbed*, *Your blind passenger*) by naming the year and venue. The artist's signature moves stay; the *occasion* changes.

## Adapting these to your course

The pattern across all five starters is the same:

1. **Name an artist or maker** (Arcimboldo, Herzog, Tenniel, "Hellenistic," Eliasson).
2. **Name a medium** (painting, 16mm film, ink and watercolor, marble, installation).
3. **Name a historical or institutional context** (Wunderkammer / 1900 edition / Vatican Museums / Tate Modern 2003).
4. **Name a subject or scene** (face of fruit, conquistador, Caterpillar on mushroom, contrapposto, mirrored ceiling).

For your own course: replace each of the four slots with material from your syllabus. A literature course on the Romantic period might generate from prompts like *"an illustration from an 1818 edition of Frankenstein, Theodore von Holst engraving style, the creature at the foot of Victor's bed."* A history-of-science course might generate from *"a botanical illustration in the style of Maria Sibylla Merian, Surinam expedition, 1705, butterflies emerging from a passion-fruit vine."* The interface and the rules don't change. The named sources do.

The point — Rule 1 — is that the *named source is doing the work*. A prompt that names no source is asking the model for a generic average. A prompt that names sources is asking the model to reach for a specific historical lineage. Make the source visible.
