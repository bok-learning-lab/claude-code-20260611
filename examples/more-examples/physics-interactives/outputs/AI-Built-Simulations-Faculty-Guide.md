# AI-Built Simulations for Learning

*A Faculty Guide Grounded in PhET and Active-Learning Research*

Prepared as a faculty-facing handout | May 21, 2026

## Central message

AI can accelerate the production of interactive simulations, but the pedagogical value comes from how the simulation is designed, tested, and used. The strongest faculty-facing claim is not that an AI-built simulation is automatically effective; it is that the simulation follows a research-based design lineage and is used in an active-learning activity that asks students to predict, manipulate, observe, explain, and revise.

## Executive summary

- Use PhET as a design lineage, not as a blanket proof. PhET research supports principles such as direct manipulation, visible models, intuitive interfaces, student questioning, and iterative refinement through learner observation.
- Use active-learning evidence to justify classroom use. A simulation should not simply be shown to students; it should be embedded in tasks that make students reason, discuss, test predictions, and explain results.
- Make AI assistance transparent but not central. Faculty should state that AI helped build the artifact while also taking responsibility for disciplinary accuracy, accessibility, student data, and revision.
- Evaluate the actual learning activity. Student explanation quality, misconception shifts, and observed usability problems are more meaningful than whether the simulation is technically impressive.

> **Copy-ready one-sentence rationale**: This simulation follows a research-based interactive-simulation design tradition: it makes an otherwise difficult relationship visible and manipulable, asks students to test predictions, and supports active sense-making rather than passive viewing.

## 1. Evidence base: what the sources support

The source stack is strongest when it separates two claims: PhET sources justify simulation design; active-learning sources justify how the simulation is used in class. This distinction keeps the writeup accurate and avoids overclaiming.

| Claim faculty may want to make | Best source cluster | Careful wording |
|---|---|---|
| The simulation is more than an animation or video. | Wieman, Adams, & Perkins; Dubson et al. | It is an interactive model designed to support exploration and reasoning. |
| The interface design matters for learning. | Adams et al., Part I and Part II; PhET Research page | Interface friction can pull attention away from the target concept, so controls, representations, and help should be intuitive and coherent. |
| The simulation helps with abstract or hard-to-visualize concepts. | McKagan et al. on quantum mechanics | Visual representations and immediate feedback can help students build mental models of processes they cannot directly observe. |
| The activity is student-centered and evidence-informed. | Deslauriers et al.; Wieman; Freeman et al. | Learning gains are associated with active, research-based instruction, especially when students practice reasoning and receive feedback. |
| The AI-built simulation is ready for classroom use. | Local evidence plus the design sources above | The simulation was reviewed for disciplinary accuracy, tested with learners, and revised based on observed use. |

**Faculty note:** Do not cite PhET to imply that every simulation, or every AI-generated simulation, improves learning. Cite PhET to justify design principles. Cite active-learning research to justify the classroom structure. Then provide local evidence that this particular simulation supports your students' learning goals.

## 2. Faculty-facing rationale

**Syllabus or assignment language**

In this course, interactive simulations are used as manipulable models, not as replacements for explanation, discussion, or lab work. You will use the simulation to make predictions, change variables, observe consequences, and explain the relationship between the model and the course concept. The goal is active sense-making: you should be asking why a change produced a result, not simply clicking through the interface.

**Report, grant, or teaching-statement language**

Our simulation design draws on the PhET tradition of research-based interactive simulations, especially its emphasis on direct manipulation, visual models, student exploration, and iterative refinement through observation of learners. The simulation is embedded in a guided active-learning activity so that students practice prediction, interpretation, and explanation rather than passively viewing a demonstration.

**AI-use disclosure language**

The simulation was developed with AI assistance and then reviewed and revised by the instructor for disciplinary accuracy, clarity, accessibility, and alignment with course learning goals. AI assistance accelerated prototype development; the instructional design, content validation, classroom use, and evaluation remain the responsibility of the instructor.

## 3. Design principles for AI-built teaching simulations

The design target is not simply interactivity. The design target is productive interaction: students should be able to take an action, see a meaningful consequence, and use that consequence to reason about the concept.

| Principle | What faculty should look for | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Begin with one learning relationship | Define the relationship students should understand, such as how changing one variable affects another, how a model behaves under constraints, or why a common intuition fails. | Avoid building a rich sandbox before defining what students should notice. |
| Make the invisible visible | Use animation, labels, graphs, or linked representations to expose mechanisms that students cannot directly observe. | For abstract domains, the visible model is not decoration; it is the learning support. |
| Couple action to feedback | When a student changes a parameter, the simulation should respond immediately and meaningfully. | Delayed or ambiguous feedback weakens the student's ability to infer cause and effect. |
| Keep the interface intuitive | Controls should be discoverable, labeled, and close to the objects they affect. The first useful action should be obvious. | If students spend their attention deciphering the interface, they are not reasoning about the concept. |
| Use multiple representations coherently | Connect visual motion, numerical values, equations, graphs, maps, timelines, or text in ways that point to the same underlying relationship. | Multiple representations help only when students can see how they correspond. |
| Limit extraneous detail | Remove controls, text, animations, and visual elements that do not serve the learning goal. | A cleaner simulation leaves more cognitive space for conceptual work. |
| Create a productive puzzle | Design for moments where students wonder, 'Why did that happen?' and can answer by experimenting. | Curiosity should be tied to the target concept, not to irrelevant game mechanics. |
| Build in explanation | Pair the simulation with prompts that require students to predict, test, explain, and connect their result to a formal concept. | The learning artifact is not only what students see; it is also what they articulate. |
| Revise from student use | Watch students use the simulation before relying on it. Note where they hesitate, what they ignore, and what explanations they produce. | A simulation becomes educationally stronger through iterative refinement, not through first-pass polish. |

## 4. Classroom use patterns

A simulation is most useful when students do something with it. The patterns below can be used in large lectures, sections, labs, or homework. Each one keeps the simulation tied to reasoning, not merely exposure.

- **Predict - observe - explain:** Students predict an outcome before touching the simulation, test the prediction, and then explain the result in writing or discussion.
- **Compare cases:** Students run two or three carefully chosen cases and identify which variable or condition explains the difference.
- **Find the boundary:** Students adjust parameters until a phenomenon appears, disappears, or changes regime, then explain the threshold.
- **Debug the model:** Students are told that a model has a limitation or a hidden assumption and must identify what the simulation can and cannot represent.
- **Connect representations:** Students match a visual event in the simulation to a graph, equation, text passage, diagram, historical source, or dataset.
- **Peer instruction with simulation evidence:** Students answer a conceptual question, test with the simulation, discuss with peers, and answer again.

> **Important distinction:** Showing a simulation is still a demonstration. Learning is more likely when students use the simulation to practice disciplinary reasoning: prediction, comparison, interpretation, argument, and explanation.

## 5. Copy-ready activity template

| Prompt | Student task |
|---|---|
| Learning goal | By the end of this activity, you should be able to explain [target relationship] and use the simulation to justify your reasoning. |
| Prediction | Before using the simulation, predict what will happen when [variable/condition] changes. Explain your reasoning in one or two sentences. |
| Exploration | Change only one thing at a time. Record what you changed, what you observed, and whether the result matched your prediction. |
| Explanation | Explain why the result occurred. Use at least one piece of evidence from the simulation and connect it to a course concept, equation, model, text, or dataset. |
| Boundary or exception | Find a case where your first explanation needs revision. What assumption changed? |
| Reflection | What did the simulation make easier to see? What did it leave out or simplify? |

## 6. Evaluation and revision plan

Faculty do not need a full research study to improve an AI-built simulation. A small, disciplined cycle of review and student observation can identify most early problems.

- **Disciplinary accuracy:** A content expert checks whether the model, labels, units, assumptions, and boundary cases are accurate enough for the intended learning goal.
- **Usability:** Students can begin useful exploration without a long demonstration; confusing controls or misleading visuals are revised.
- **Conceptual alignment:** Student explanations focus on the intended concept rather than on superficial features of the interface.
- **Learning evidence:** Students produce better predictions, explanations, diagrams, code comments, problem solutions, or transfer responses after using the simulation.
- **Equity and accessibility:** Students can use the simulation with varied devices, input methods, visual needs, language backgrounds, and prior preparation.

**Lightweight observation protocol:** Watch two to four students use the simulation for ten minutes. Do not intervene at first. Record where they pause, what they click first, what they ignore, what they say the model means, and whether they can explain the target relationship. Revise the simulation or activity prompt based on the patterns you observe.

## 7. AI-specific responsibilities

AI assistance changes the production process, not the instructor's responsibility for the learning environment.

- **Accuracy:** Treat AI-generated code, labels, examples, datasets, and explanations as draft material. Verify the underlying model and edge cases.
- **Transparency:** Disclose the role of AI when appropriate, especially in public writeups or student-facing materials.
- **Accessibility:** Check keyboard navigation, text labels, contrast, readability, motion, captions or alternatives for visual information, and compatibility with course devices.
- **Privacy:** Avoid unnecessary collection of student data. If the simulation logs activity, say what is collected, why, where it is stored, and who can see it.
- **Bias and representation:** Review examples, metaphors, images, language, and default scenarios for exclusionary or misleading assumptions.
- **Reproducibility:** Keep a revision log: prompt or code source, instructor edits, model assumptions, classroom changes, and known limitations.

## 8. One-page faculty checklist

### Before using the simulation
- [ ] The simulation has one clearly stated learning goal.
- [ ] The instructor has verified the model, assumptions, labels, and units.
- [ ] Students can identify what they are able to manipulate and what they are supposed to observe.
- [ ] The activity includes prediction and explanation, not just exploration.
- [ ] Accessibility and device constraints have been checked.

### During the activity
- [ ] Students make a prediction before manipulating the model.
- [ ] Students change one variable or condition at a time unless comparison is the goal.
- [ ] Students discuss or write what the observed change means.
- [ ] The instructor listens for misconceptions and interface confusion.
- [ ] The class connects simulation evidence to a formal representation or course concept.

### After the activity
- [ ] Students produce an explanation that can be assessed.
- [ ] The instructor records what confused students and what they learned quickly.
- [ ] The simulation or prompt is revised based on student use.
- [ ] The writeup distinguishes design rationale from evidence of effectiveness.

## 10. Limits and cautions

- **Simulations are models:** They simplify the world. Ask students what is represented, what is omitted, and where the model stops applying.
- **Sims are not always lab replacements:** A simulation can support conceptual understanding, but it may not teach instrument handling, measurement uncertainty from physical equipment, troubleshooting, or embodied lab practices.
- **Polish can hide errors:** A visually attractive AI-built simulation can still contain false assumptions, coding bugs, misleading labels, or inaccessible design choices.
- **Unguided use is not enough:** Most students need a reason to explore and a prompt that helps them convert observations into explanations.
- **Disciplinary judgment is essential:** Faculty expertise is needed to decide whether a simulation's simplifications are instructionally productive or misleading.
