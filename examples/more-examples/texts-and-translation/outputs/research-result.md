What the Machine Cannot Read: A Contextualizing Essay on LLM-Assisted Work with Non-English Primary Texts
You have just seen a demonstration of large-language-model–assisted work with Homer's Odyssey in fourteen translations across six languages and with Jagannātha Paṇḍitarāja's Rasagaṅgādhara, a seventeenth-century Sanskrit treatise on poetics. The temptation now is either to extrapolate enthusiastically from what worked or to dismiss the enterprise on the strength of what didn't. Neither response is adequate. The humanities have built, over two centuries, a methodological apparatus for close textual work that is among the discipline's most distinctive achievements. Any responsible account of what LLMs do to that apparatus needs to start by describing it carefully, then describe just as carefully which parts of the work LLMs can help with and which they cannot — and on what terms.
This essay does that, in three parts. The first sketches the methodological tradition the demo is operating inside, from Lachmann to Pollock. The second articulates what close textual work actually consists of as labor — distinguishing the parts of it that consume time from the parts that constitute the discipline. The third maps LLM affordances onto that labor distinction and names the hard problems the humanities specifically face. The frame throughout is that LLMs augment labor-bound scholarly tasks; they do not perform interpretive judgment, and they do not produce scholarship.
Part 1 — The Methodological Tradition
Close textual work in the modern humanities has its origins in the philological revolution of the early nineteenth century. Karl Lachmann's edition of the Greek New Testament (1831) is the conventional milestone, though the procedures he codified — comparing surviving manuscript witnesses, identifying shared errors, reconstructing a hypothetical archetype via the stemma codicum — were already present in the work of Bentley, Bengel, and Wolf. What Lachmann did was assemble them into a method robust enough to be taught and extended. By the end of the nineteenth century the stemmatic method had become the default discipline for editing classical and medieval texts, and the critical edition — the printed source-text with its apparatus criticus of variants — had become the form in which philological labor reached the rest of the field. The genealogical methods Lachmann inherited were already being supplemented by computational stemmatology in the 1950s, and the application of phylogenetic software from evolutionary biology to manuscript traditions is now an established sub-field.
Alongside the philological tradition runs the parallel history of translation theory. Friedrich Schleiermacher's 1813 address to the Royal Academy of Sciences in Berlin, "On the Different Methods of Translating," articulated a distinction that has organized the field ever since: the translator must either move the reader toward the author, preserving the foreignness of the source, or move the author toward the reader, naturalizing the source into the target language's idiom. Walter Benjamin's "The Task of the Translator" (1923), the introduction to his German Baudelaire, pushed the question into more difficult territory: translation, for Benjamin, is not a procedure for transmitting information but a "mode" through which the afterlife of the original is realized. Lawrence Venuti's The Translator's Invisibility (1995; revised 2008) returned to Schleiermacher and refigured the distinction in political terms — domestication conceals the labor of the translator and the foreignness of the source under a fluent, transparent English; foreignization makes both visible. The point is not bibliographic completeness — Antoine Berman, Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak, and Maria Tymoczko all warrant treatment in a longer piece — but to register that translation studies has had, for two centuries, a sophisticated apparatus for thinking about what translation does and what it is for. A side-by-side comparison of fourteen Odyssey translations is not a neutral data display; it is a comparison of fourteen positions on the Schleiermacher–Venuti axis, each shaped by what its translator took translation to be.
The close-reading tradition descends from a different lineage. New Criticism — Brooks, Wimsatt, Ransom — consolidated in mid-twentieth-century American departments a procedure for reading the literary text as a self-sufficient verbal artifact, attending to ambiguity, paradox, and structure. Erich Auerbach's Mimesis (1946), written in Istanbul exile without his German libraries, demonstrated what close reading could achieve when wedded to philological erudition and ranging across Greek, Latin, Old French, Italian, Spanish, German, and English — twenty chapters, each opening from a single passage. Auerbach's first chapter, "Odysseus' Scar," reading Homer against the aqedah in Genesis 22, remains a paradigmatic demonstration of what close reading does.
The Sanskrit philological tradition has its own deep history, predating European philology by more than two millennia. Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī (c. 5th–4th century BCE) is itself an astonishing achievement in linguistic analysis, and the commentarial tradition — bhāṣya, vārttika, ṭīkā — constitutes a model of textual scholarship as elaborate as anything in Europe. Nāgeśa Bhaṭṭa's commentary on Pāṇini and Mathurā Nāth Śāstrī's Gurumarmaprakāśikā on the Rasagaṅgādhara are not external aids to reading; they are integral to how the text is read. European Indology in the nineteenth century — Müller, Weber, Aufrecht, Oldenberg — built its work on this indigenous tradition, often without acknowledging what it borrowed and frequently subordinating the indigenous commentaries to its own racial and theological priors. Sheldon Pollock's body of work, including his framework essay "Future Philology? The Fate of a Soft Science in a Hard World" (2009), has been the most sustained recent effort to reckon with that inheritance: to make philology critical of its own history while preserving what it knows how to do. Pollock's editorial work on the Clay Sanskrit Library and the Murty Classical Library of India — projected at 500 volumes over a century, published by Harvard University Press in bilingual format — is its institutional expression.
The computational turn in literary studies emerged from a different direction. Franco Moretti's "Conjectures on World Literature" (2000) and the essays gathered in Distant Reading (2013) made the case for studying literature at scales close reading could not reach. Matthew Jockers' Macroanalysis (2013), Ted Underwood's Distant Horizons (2019), and the pamphlets of the Stanford Literary Lab developed the procedures. Reception has been mixed. Critics from Katherine Bode through Nan Z. Da have pressed on assumptions about evidence and corpus; the political stakes — articulated by Roopika Risam in New Digital Worlds (2018) and by Alan Liu in his recurring intervention on digital humanities and cultural critique — sharpen as the tools become more powerful. The Anglophone bias of distant reading has been a persistent limit; the work has been overwhelmingly on English-language corpora, and the methods that gained traction in English departments did not extend easily to non-Latin scripts, polytonic Greek, or the morphological complexity of Sanskrit.
The infrastructure that made non-English computational work possible has come instead from philological projects: the Perseus Digital Library at Tufts (founded 1987, now hosting 69.7 million words across Greek, Latin, and English translations through the Scaife Viewer); the Thesaurus Linguae Graecae at UC Irvine; the Göttingen Register of Electronic Texts in Indian Languages (GRETIL), a machine-readable corpus of Sanskrit, Pali, Prakrit, Tibetan, and other South Asian languages; SARIT at the NEH; the Digital Corpus of Sanskrit at Heidelberg; Sefaria's open digital library of Jewish texts. Each represents enormous editorial labor and contains editorial decisions that subsequent computational work then inherits, often invisibly.
This is the tradition the LLM enters. It takes textual labor seriously, treats editorial decisions as scholarship rather than as preparatory work, and has a sophisticated and self-critical understanding of what close reading and translation are for. The question is not whether the LLM can do philology — it cannot — but what it can do for the philologist.
Part 2 — What Close Textual Work Actually Involves
The labor of close textual work is heterogeneous, and a serious account of LLM use has to keep the distinctions sharp. There is work that is labor-intensive but conceptually well-defined; there is work that is interpretively irreducible; and there is a slippery middle.
The labor-intensive but conceptually well-defined work is what graduate students have always done. Collating multiple witnesses against a base text. Building a glossary of specialized terminology — Homeric formulae, the technical vocabulary of rasa-dhvani theory, the alaṃkāra-names whose definitions vary across authors. Transcribing manuscripts (the transcription itself, when from clearly legible printed sources rather than ambiguous paleographic hands). Aligning translations to source passages line by line. Indexing a corpus by character, theme, motif, or trope. Cross-referencing commentaries — locating, in Mathurā Nāth Śāstrī's commentary on the Rasagaṅgādhara, where a given verse is taken up. Producing first-pass translations of routine passages. None of this work is unimportant. Critical editions depend on it; teaching depends on it; subsequent interpretive work depends on it. But none is the discipline's defining intellectual activity.
The interpretively irreducible work is what scholars are paid to do. Deciding what a contested passage means. Weighing competing readings. Assessing a translator's solution to an untranslatable phrase. Identifying which figure of speech is operating in a Sanskrit verse where two or three alaṃkāras could be defended. Situating a passage in a literary tradition. Understanding what register, what irony, what voicing, what allusion is doing in a specific moment. Recognizing what is at stake in a translator's choice — that Emily Wilson's "Tell me about a complicated man" is a stance, that "complicated" for polytropos is a defensible reading among others (Fagles' "the man of twists and turns," Lattimore's "the man of many ways," Lombardo's "the cunning hero") and that the stakes of choosing among them have to do with what kind of figure Odysseus is taken to be. This is what makes humanistic scholarship recognizable as a form of knowledge.
The two corpora the demo used illustrate both ends of the spectrum. Consider the opening of the Odyssey. The Greek begins Ἄνδρα μοι ἔννεπε, Μοῦσα, πολύτροπον — "Of the man tell me, Muse, the [polytropon]." The first word is ándra, "man," and Daniel Mendelsohn has observed that the structural significance of the entire poem can be read out of that placement: the Odyssey begins with the man, where the Iliad begins with "wrath" (mēnin). The first adjective applied is polytropon — literally "of many turns," from poly- and tropos. Greek allows the word to mean both "much-wandering" and "much-turning of mind" — physically itinerant and mentally versatile, even, as Mendelsohn notes, morally slippery. Each translator has to pick a register. Chapman in 1614: "the man, O Muse, informe, that many a way / Wound with his wisedome." Pope in 1725: "The man for wisdom's various arts renown'd." Lattimore (1965): "the man of many ways." Fagles (1996): "the man of twists and turns." Wilson (2017): "a complicated man." Jaccottet in French (1955): "l'homme aux mille tours." Each is a position on a translation-theoretical question that is not resolved by any of them. Polytropos itself is the question; the translations are answers.
Jagannātha's Rasagaṅgādhara (composed in the mid-seventeenth century under the patronage of Shāh Jahān) is a treatise on the constitutive elements of kāvya — poetic literature — and its central concept is ramaṇīyatā, the quality of "captivating beauty" that distinguishes poetry from non-poetry. Jagannātha's opening kārikā defines kāvya as ramaṇīyārtha-pratipādakaḥ śabdaḥ — "word that conveys captivating meaning" — and the rest of the treatise unfolds the consequences through hundreds of illustrative verses, mostly Jagannātha's own composition. To analyze a verse in the Rasagaṅgādhara's framework, a contemporary scholar has to identify which alaṃkāra is at work — and the same verse can be read under multiple alaṃkāras depending on which aspect the scholar foregrounds. Is the verse displaying utprekṣā (poetic fancy)? Atiśayokti (hyperbole)? Rūpaka (metaphorical superimposition)? Jagannātha himself spends much of the Citra-mīmāṃsā-khaṇḍana refuting earlier rhetoricians' identifications and proposing his own. The decision is interpretive, not mechanical.
Between these poles lies the slippery middle: tasks that look mechanical but contain interpretive moves. Transliterating an ambiguous Sanskrit sandhi, where the same string can resolve into different lexical sequences. Rendering a Homeric epithet that has no English equivalent (rhododáktylos Ēṓs, "rosy-fingered Dawn," whose translation Sam Anderson in the New York Times praised Wilson for varying — "When early Dawn revealed her rose-red hands"; "her fingers bloomed"; "touched the sky with roses"). Choosing whether to render a Greek aorist with an English simple past or perfect, given the temporal nuance of the surrounding narrative. These middle-case tasks are where LLM use becomes most fraught. A first-pass translation that smooths an ambiguity into a confident reading has already done interpretive work — and has done it without showing its working.
Part 3 — Where LLMs Assist, and Where They Don't
With those distinctions in place, the affordances of LLMs for humanistic text work fall out in a usefully constrained way.
Comparative translation at scale. When a scholar wants to see what fourteen translators did with Odyssey 1.1–10, the LLM can assemble the side-by-side reliably — given that the translations are in its training corpus or are provided in context — with attribution to which translator made which choice. The scholar then reads the comparison and decides what those choices mean. The judgment is irreducibly the scholar's; the labor of pulling fourteen volumes off the shelf and lining them up is the LLM's. Emily Wilson's translation became a subject of public scholarly debate in part because reviewers compared its choices against the existing English tradition; the LLM makes that comparative work cheaper and faster for a scholar working on a single passage.
Glossary construction. Building a working glossary of Homeric formulaic epithets — polymētis Odysseus, podas ōkýs Achilles, boōpis Hera — or of rasa-dhvani technical terminology is mechanical labor at production scale. The LLM produces a first pass; the scholar edits. The result is a teaching tool or research aid that previously required a research assistant or did not get built at all. In the Sanskrit case, the affordance extends to commentary-aware glossary work: extracting technical vocabulary as used by a specific commentator across a corpus.
Candidate identification of figures of speech in alaṃkāra-rich texts. This is one of the more interesting affordances, because the validation is built into the corpus. The Rasagaṅgādhara itself defines each alaṃkāra and gives illustrative verses. An LLM can apply that taxonomy to other passages and produce candidate identifications, which the scholar accepts, rejects, or refines. The same text that defines the categories can audit the LLM's calls — a kind of internal consistency check that is harder to engineer for less codified literary traditions.
Indexing across long corpora. Twenty-four books of the Odyssey across multiple translations; the eight ānanas of the Rasagaṅgādhara. Building per-passage indexes — by character, by theme, by figure, by intertextual reference — is precisely the labor LLMs are good at. The scholar provides categories and validates output.
Non-Latin script handling. A meaningful affordance the older computational-humanities tradition often lacked. Frontier LLMs handle Devanāgarī, polytonic Greek, classical Chinese, Arabic, Hebrew, Tibetan, and Pali natively in their tokenizers, without OCR or transliteration loss. The constraint is that the model's competence varies dramatically across scripts and is generally worse for languages with less training data. Aralikatte and colleagues' study of cross-lingual zero-shot generalization for Sanskrit, Ancient Greek, and Latin in GPT-4o and LLaMA-3.1 (2025) found uneven but non-trivial performance, with retrieval-augmented setups outperforming closed-book substantially. Volk and colleagues' evaluation on Coptic translation (2024) found systematically weaker performance than on Ancient Greek for parallel passages. The pattern is what one would expect: the models are better at the well-represented languages.
Pedagogical handout generation. First-draft student-facing handouts — vocabulary glosses, grammar notes, discussion questions — for any passage in any corpus. The faculty member edits; the boilerplate is automated.
Source-language work in ethnography and area studies. A meaningful extension beyond classics. Interview transcripts in non-English languages can now be indexed, coded, and analyzed in the source language rather than only after a translation step that loses register, idiom, and code-switching information.
The pattern these affordances share is that the LLM helps with what is labor-bound. It does not help — and should not be allowed to be seen as helping — with what is judgment-bound. The validation work, the choosing among readings, the situating in a tradition, the recognizing of what is at stake — these remain the scholar's.
The affordances extend across adjacent humanistic fields with discipline-specific shape. In classics, they build on the Perseus tradition (morphological analysis, dictionary lookup, parallel-passage retrieval) while remaining downstream of its editorial labor. In Sanskrit and South Asian studies, GRETIL's corpus and the Murty Classical Library's bilingual editions become the substrate the LLM operates on; commentary-aware reading becomes practically feasible for scholars who need to engage Sanskrit material without being Sanskritists. In East Asian classical traditions — classical Chinese, kanbun, the Taishō Tripiṭaka, the Daozang — concerns about LLM training on poorly-edited digital editions sharpen. Wang and colleagues' Kanbun-LM project (ACL 2023) constructed the first machine-readable Classical-Chinese-to-Kanbun parallel corpus precisely because existing digital resources were inadequate. Yu and Tang's 2024 evaluation of GPT-4, ERNIE Bot, and Gemini on Buddhist Classical Chinese texts shows the models handle narrative passages reasonably but stumble on technical Buddhist terminology and the dense compression of classical syntax. In Arabic and Persian, challenges include unvocalized Arabic text (where the LLM has to infer vowels from context and frequently does so wrongly for poetry) and the multi-recensional Shahnameh tradition. In Hebrew Bible and Rabbinics, Sefaria provides an unusually well-developed digital substrate — open-licensed, hyperlinked across Tanakh, Mishnah, Talmud, midrash, and commentaries — making it one of the more promising sites for LLM-assisted reading, partly because the editorial decisions are transparent. In comparative literature, cross-language thematic indexing and reception studies become more feasible. In translation studies as a discipline, the question is what changes when the labor of translation comparison drops to near zero — what graduate programs train for, what publications count as contribution. In manuscript studies, LLM-assisted catalog generation and metadata extraction become useful adjuncts to (not substitutes for) hand-and-eye transcription. In lexicography, corpus-wide citation collection for new dictionary entries is a task the LLM does well, given a clean corpus.
The Hard Problems
The credibility of any account of LLM-assisted humanistic text work depends on what it says about the failure modes. There are at least six worth naming, and they are not the same hard problems the social sciences face.
The politics of training data. LLMs trained on existing translations, commentaries, and scholarly editions reproduce — and may launder — the editorial and ideological choices in those sources. The colonial-era European editions of South Asian texts (Müller, Whitney, Macdonell) are deeply embedded in the models' priors, in ways that are hard to audit and harder to undo. When an LLM produces a "first-pass translation" of a Sanskrit verse, it is producing a translation shaped by the assumptions of the philologists who edited the source texts it learned from. Adluri and Bagchee's Philology and Criticism (2018) makes the case that nineteenth-century German Indology baked specific racial and theological commitments into its editorial work; those commitments now propagate, automatically, through every LLM that learned to read Sanskrit on those editions.
Hallucinated citations. LLMs invent plausible-sounding bibliographic references. For philologically careful work, this is disqualifying without verification. The problem is no longer a curiosity: a Nature analysis in early 2026 estimated that tens of thousands of 2025 publications may contain LLM-invented citations, and a study of NeurIPS 2025 accepted papers (peer-reviewed by three to five experts each) identified approximately 100 fabricated citations in 53 of the 4,841 accepted papers. Statnews reported in May 2026 that the share of papers with fabricated references reached approximately one in 277 in early 2026, up from one in 2,828 in 2023. The implication for humanistic text work is that any LLM-mediated citation must be verified against the actual source.
The "good enough translation" risk. LLM-produced translations of canonical texts are often good enough for casual reading and not nearly good enough for scholarly use. The boundary is easy to lose track of, especially in pedagogical settings. A student who reads an LLM translation of Sappho 1 alongside an Anne Carson and a Diane Rayor translation may not be in a position to see what is wrong with the LLM's rendering — and the LLM's confident, fluent prose makes the wrongness harder to detect than the awkwardness of a less polished but more accurate scholarly translation would.
What counts as scholarship. If an LLM-assisted comparative-translation note can be produced in an afternoon, what does that mean for the academic-credit economy that built the field? Publication tempo, assessment criteria, the question of authorship — these are not merely administrative concerns. They go to what the discipline thinks it is rewarding when it tenures, promotes, and prizes a scholar. The field has had analogous reckonings before (the rise of the critical edition, the introduction of word processing, the JSTOR-and-Google-Scholar reorganization of citation practice). None was painless. None settled quickly.
The colonial inheritance. Philological work on non-European traditions has a history, and automating the work without engaging that history risks reproducing it at scale. Pollock's "philology of the future" — a critical practice that knows its own history — is one articulation of what reflective practice might look like; it requires the scholar to keep asking what the LLM's confidence has been built out of. The most LLM-resistant scholarly work, in the immediate term, may be the work that takes that question most seriously: editions, translations, and commentaries that are explicit about their genealogy and that the LLM cannot smoothly continue.
Access and corpus politics. Whose digital editions get used? Whose stay locked behind paywalls? Who pays for the API calls when faculty at well-resourced institutions can run LLM-assisted comparative work that faculty at less well-resourced institutions cannot? The Risam and Liu critique of digital humanities anticipated this in the 2010s; LLM-assisted text work raises the stakes. A two-tier humanities in which the scholars with API budgets work at one scale and the scholars without them work at another is not a humanities anyone should want.
· · ·
The demo two hours ago was useful because it was specific. It showed what an LLM could do with the Odyssey in fourteen translations and with Jagannātha. The contextualizing claim this essay has tried to defend is also specific: that the LLM's usefulness is bounded by the distinction between labor-bound and judgment-bound work, that the labor-bound side is where the affordance lives, and that the failure modes — hallucinated citations, laundered editorial choices, smoothed-over ambiguities, access asymmetries — are real, present, and the scholar's responsibility to manage. The discipline that produced Lachmann and Pollock, Auerbach and Venuti, is well-equipped to think carefully about a new tool that touches its work. What it should not do is mistake the tool for the work.
