# Figure analysis — *Odyssey* 6.1–20

Source: PerseusDL TEI-XML (tlg0012.tlg002.perseus-grc2), ed. Augustus Taber Murray

## Passage

```

 2  ὕπνῳ καὶ καμάτῳ ἀρημένος· αὐτὰρ Ἀθήνη
```

## Candidate figures

### 1. Simile (*eikasia*) — short simile, not extended

**Relevant text:** `ἡ δʼ ἀνέμου ὡς πνοιὴ ἐπέσσυτο δέμνια κούρης` (line 20)

**Rhetorical function:** Athena's approach to Nausicaa's bed is compared to a breath of wind — `ἀνέμου … πνοιή`, literally "a blowing of wind." The figure does two things at once: it renders the goddess's movement as silent and imperceptible (a wind does not knock or announce itself), and it preserves divine dignity by avoiding any suggestion of physical intrusion. This is a *short* simile, not the extended Homeric simile (*Homerisches Gleichnis*) that develops a full secondary scene — the comparison is compact, functional, and contained within the line. Worth noting precisely because the *extended* simile is the more famous Homeric device; this passage shows Homer also deploying the figure in compressed form.

**Confidence:** strong candidate

**How translators handled this** *(line 20 — line correspondence approximate for verse translations)*

- **Pope** (heroic couplets): "Light as the viewless air the warrior maid / Glides through the valves, and hovers round her head" — substitutes "light as the viewless air" for the wind simile; the vehicle shifts from breath to weightlessness, and the explicit `ὡς` comparison becomes an adjective.
- **Cowper** (blank verse): "Soft as a breathing air, she stole toward / The royal virgin's couch" — close rendering of `ἀνέμου … πνοιή`; "breathing air" preserves the wind vehicle and "soft" captures its imperceptibility.
- **Butler** (prose): "coming up to the girl's bedside like a breath of wind, she hovered over her head" — literal and close; the simile survives intact in plain prose.
- **Calbet** (French prose): "comme un souffle léger, s'approcha du lit de la jeune fille" — "like a light breath"; *léger* adds a nuance of lightness not present in the Greek but consistent with the figure's effect.
- **Segala Estalella** (Spanish prose): "Minerva se lanzó, como un soplo de viento, á la cama de la joven" — "like a breath of wind"; closest of the prose translations to the Greek wording.
- **Firmin-Didot** (Latin): "Illa vero venti veluti flatus accessit ad-cubile puellæ" — "she approached the girl's bed just like a blast of wind"; *veluti* for `ὡς`, *flatus* for `πνοιή` — word-for-word faithful.

### 2. Stock epithet (*epitheton ornans*) — πολύτλας δῖος Ὀδυσσεύς

**Relevant text:** `πολύτλας δῖος Ὀδυσσεὺς` (line 1)

**Rhetorical function:** "Much-enduring, godlike Odysseus" — a formulaic noun-epithet combination that recurs throughout the *Odyssey* and is metrically shaped to fill the line-ending. Its deployment here is quietly ironic: the opening line announces Odysseus with his full heroic title, and the next phrase immediately strips him back to exhaustion (`ὕπνῳ καὶ καμάτῳ ἀρημένος`, "overcome by sleep and toil"). The epithet asserts the hero's identity at the moment he is most inert.

**Confidence:** strong candidate

**How translators handled this** *(line 1 — line correspondence approximate for verse translations)*

- **Pope** (heroic couplets): "While thus the weary wanderer sunk to rest" — drops both epithets entirely; "weary wanderer" is a paraphrase of the situation, not a rendering of `πολύτλας δῖος`.
- **Cowper** (blank verse): "There then the noble suff'rer lay" — "noble sufferer" is a compressed translation of `πολύτλας` (much-enduring) and `δῖος` (noble/godlike) together.
- **Butler** (prose): "So here Ulysses slept, overcome by sleep and toil" — drops both epithets; renders the condition (`ὕπνῳ καὶ καμάτῳ ἀρημένος`, line 2) but not the title.
- **Calbet** (French prose): "le divin Ulysse" — preserves `δῖος` ("divine") but drops `πολύτλας`.
- **Segala Estalella** (Spanish prose): "el paciente y divinal Ulises" — "the patient and divine Ulysses"; uniquely preserves both epithets in translated form: *paciente* for πολύτλας, *divinal* for δῖος.
- **Firmin-Didot** (Latin): "audens divinus Ulysses" — "bold, divine Ulysses"; *audens* (daring) for πολύτλας is an unusual choice — the Latin shifts the epithet from endurance to courage.

### 3. Stock epithet (*epitheton ornans*) — γλαυκῶπις Ἀθήνη

**Relevant text:** `γλαυκῶπις Ἀθήνη` (line 13)

**Rhetorical function:** "Bright-eyed" or "owl-eyed" Athena — one of the most frequently recurring epithets in Homer, metrically fixed as a line-ending formula. Here it appears at the moment Athena enters Alcinous's palace on Odysseus's behalf. The epithet signals divine purposefulness; the word `γλαυκῶπις` carries connotations of sharp, penetrating vision, apt for the goddess of strategic intelligence arriving to engineer a human encounter.

**Confidence:** strong candidate

**How translators handled this** *(line 13 — line correspondence approximate for verse translations)*

- **Pope** (heroic couplets): "The martial maid" — replaces the epithet and divine name with a descriptive phrase; `γλαυκῶπις` (keen-eyed) becomes a martial characterisation rather than a visual one.
- **Cowper** (blank verse): "Minerva azure-eyed" — direct translation of γλαυκῶπις; "azure-eyed" captures the colour range of γλαυκός (blue-grey-green) and preserves the visual epithet.
- **Butler** (prose): "Minerva" — drops the epithet entirely; Roman name only.
- **Calbet** (French prose): "Minerve" — drops the epithet entirely.
- **Segala Estalella** (Spanish prose): "Minerva, la deidad de los brillantes ojos" — "the deity of brilliant eyes"; adds the epithet as an appositive phrase, preserving it at the cost of fluency.
- **Firmin-Didot** (Latin): "dea cæsiis-oculis Minerva" — "grey-eyed goddess Minerva"; *caesius* (grey-blue) is the standard Latin equivalent for γλαυκός; word-for-word faithful.

### 4. Repeated epithet — μεγαλήτωρ applied to both Odysseus and Alcinous

**Relevant text:** `νόστον Ὀδυσσῆι μεγαλήτορι μητιόωσα` (line 14) and `Ναυσικάα, θυγάτηρ μεγαλήτορος Ἀλκινόοιο` (line 17)

**Rhetorical function:** The adjective `μεγαλήτωρ` ("great-hearted") is applied first to Odysseus (line 14) and then to Alcinous (line 17), three lines apart. This may be purely formulaic — the word is metrically convenient and both men are kings — but the proximity is notable: Athena is thinking of Odysseus's return while entering the house of the man whose daughter will make that return possible. A scholar might ask whether the repetition is incidental or whether Homer is quietly linking the two men's greatness before they meet. Requires philological evaluation to distinguish formulaic habit from deliberate juxtaposition.

**Confidence:** possible candidate

**How translators handled this** *(lines 14 and 17)*

- **Pope** (heroic couplets): does not render μεγαλήτωρ for either Odysseus or Alcinous; the epithet disappears in both contexts.
- **Cowper** (blank verse): "Magnanimous Ulysses" (line 14) and "Alcinoüs" only (line 17) — preserves the epithet for Odysseus but drops it for Alcinous, breaking the juxtaposition.
- **Butler** (prose): "return of Ulysses" (line 14) and "King Alcinous" (line 17) — drops μεγαλήτωρ in both instances.
- **Calbet** (French prose): neither instance rendered; both men named without the epithet.
- **Segala Estalella** (Spanish prose): "el magnánimo Ulises" (line 14) and "el magnánimo Alcínoo" (line 17) — uniquely preserves μεγαλήτωρ for *both* men; the juxtaposition present in the Greek survives in Spanish.
- **Firmin-Didot** (Latin): "Ulyssi magnanimo" (line 14) and "magnanimi Alcinoi" (line 17) — preserves both instances; the juxtaposition survives in Latin as well.

### 5. Periphrasis — description of Nausicaa by parentage rather than name

**Relevant text:** `βῆ δʼ ἴμεν ἐς θάλαμον πολυδαίδαλον, ᾧ ἔνι κούρη / κοιμᾶτʼ ἀθανάτῃσι φυὴν καὶ εἶδος ὁμοίη` (lines 15–16), followed by `Ναυσικάα, θυγάτηρ μεγαλήτορος Ἀλκινόοιο` (line 17)

**Rhetorical function:** Nausicaa is introduced over three lines: first as "a girl" sleeping in the chamber (line 15–16), then compared to the immortals in form and appearance, and only then named — and named by her father's identity rather than by her own. The delay and the parentage formula (`θυγάτηρ μεγαλήτορος Ἀλκινόοιο`) is a standard Homeric introduction for a significant new character, but the two-line buildup before the name creates a small arrival effect. Her beauty and her lineage precede her name.

**Confidence:** possible candidate

**How translators handled this** *(lines 15–17)*

- **Pope** (heroic couplets): "A heaven of charms divine Nausicaa lay" — names Nausicaa immediately without the two-line buildup; the goddess-comparison becomes an adjective ("divine"), and the parentage formula is dropped.
- **Cowper** (blank verse): "the young Nausicaa, daughter of the King, reposed" — preserves the parentage formula ("daughter of the King") but names her in the same breath, compressing the three-line arrival into one clause.
- **Butler** (prose): "a girl who was as lovely as a goddess, Nausicaa, daughter to King Alcinous" — closest to the Greek structure; the comparison to immortals precedes the name, and the parentage follows.
- **Calbet** (French prose): "la divine Nausicaa, fille d'Alcinoüs" — names and parentage together; compresses the arrival but retains the lineage.
- **Segala Estalella** (Spanish prose): "Nausícaa, hija del magnánimo Alcínoo" — names with parentage; the goddess-comparison is rendered earlier in the sentence ("parecida á las inmortales"), preserving something of the Greek sequence.
- **Firmin-Didot** (Latin): "Nausicaa, filia magnanimi Alcinoi" — name then parentage; the immortal-comparison appears in the preceding line, maintaining the Greek order.

## Notes

- The simile at line 20 (`ἀνέμου ὡς πνοιὴ`) is worth comparing against the longer wind-similes elsewhere in Homer (e.g. the hawk similes in *Iliad* 22). The compressed form here may be pedagogically useful precisely because it sits alongside the *Odyssey*'s more developed similes elsewhere in the poem.
- Lines 9–10 have a notable asyndetic accumulation: three verbs in sequence — `ἔλασσε`, `ἐδείματο`, `ποίησε`, `ἐδάσσατʼ` — listing Nausithous's city-founding acts without connectives between the first two. A classicist should verify whether this qualifies as asyndeton or simply parataxis; not included as a numbered candidate given the uncertainty.

## What stood out

- **The wind simile splits cleanly by translation strategy.** Prose translators (Butler, Calbet, Segala Estalella, Firmin-Didot Latin) all keep the vehicle — wind or breath of wind. Verse translators diverge: Cowper stays close ("breathing air"), but Pope drops the wind entirely and substitutes "viewless air," shifting from a natural phenomenon to an abstract quality. This is a clean example of verse form driving a semantic decision.

- **Segala Estalella (Spanish) is the most faithful to the Greek epithets of any translator here** — and the only one who preserves the doubled μεγαλήτωρ for *both* Odysseus (line 14) and Alcinous (line 17). That juxtaposition, which may be doing structural work before the two men meet, survives in Spanish and in the Firmin-Didot Latin, but disappears in all three English translations and in French.

- **Firmin-Didot Latin mistranslates πολύτλας.** The epithet means "much-enduring" (long-suffering, patient under suffering) — the defining quality of Odysseus as a hero. Firmin-Didot renders it *audens* (bold, daring), which is a reasonable general heroic epithet but gets the specific meaning wrong. The Spanish *paciente* is actually closer.

- **Pope drops every epithet in this passage** — πολύτλας δῖος, γλαυκῶπις, both instances of μεγαλήτωρ — and substitutes atmospheric or narrative description instead. This is a consistent pattern in Pope rather than a local decision, and it means that any student reading Pope is reading a text where the formulaic epithet system has been systematically removed.
