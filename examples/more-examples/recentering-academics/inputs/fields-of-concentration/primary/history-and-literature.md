# History & Literature

Students in History & Literature—Harvard’s oldest concentration—learn to analyze cultural
objects in historical contexts and comprehend historical contexts through cultural forms. How
do films, novels, TV shows, fine art, and works of popular culture come alive when understood
as participants in larger social or political struggles? How are big, influential ideas—about self
and nation, identity and power, diaspora and language—negotiated through cultural
materials? And how can we shed light on the past by considering the objects, practices,
customs, and ideas of a particular time and place? To answer these questions, History &
Literature students adopt an interdisciplinary approach, drawing on historical and literary
methods of research and analysis.
As an interdisciplinary program, students can take courses from a wide variety of departments
across the Arts & Humanities and Social Sciences to fulfill History & Literature’s field
requirements. These requirements provide a structure to ensure that concentrators gain a
comprehensive and in-depth knowledge of their area of study. Students choose from one of
History & Literature’s eight different fields: American Studies, Ethnic Studies, European
Studies, Latin American Studies, Urban Studies, the Modern World, the World Before 1800,
and the Medieval World.
A hallmark of History & Literature is its tutorial structure, which provides an opportunity for
advisors and students with shared interests to work in small, tight-knit groups. The sophomore
tutorial introduces students to the interdisciplinary methods key to History & Literature. In the
first semester of the junior tutorial, small groups of students collaboratively design a syllabus
with the guidance of an instructor. In the second semester of junior tutorial, students focus on
researching and writing the junior essay. As an honors concentration, students in History &
Literature are expected to write a thesis as part of their senior tutorial. The thesis is a
capstone project of the student’s own design; senior tutorial provides one-on-one mentoring in
order to support students throughout the process.
LEARNING OBJECTIVES
Studying History & Literature prepares students to think in creative, flexible ways that can
translate into a wide variety of careers in arts and entertainment, law and public service,
education, media and journalism, business, medicine, and beyond. Students develop skills in
analyzing a wide variety of cultural and historical objects; reading works of theory and
scholarship; conducting original research and making strong arguments; and communicating
clearly and persuasively.
REQUIREMENTS
History & Literature
Concentration Requirements: 14 courses (56 credits)
At the end of sophomore year, each History & Literature concentrator selects a field of study
and submits a Field Worksheet. Revised worksheets are submitted in the junior and senior
years. A regularly updated list of courses in various fields that count toward the concentration
is available on the History & Literature website at https://histlit.fas.harvard.edu/courses-count.
Courses in the social sciences on subjects related to the student’s field may be accepted for
concentration credit by the Committee on Instruction by petition.
1. Required courses:

a. At least eight courses in the field of study, balanced between history and
literature. Specific period requirements exist in several fields of study. Details
on fields of study and their requirements are available on the History &
Literature website at http://histlit.fas.harvard.edu/fields. Generally, courses
must be letter-graded to count for concentration credit.
b. Language requirement: Students must take at least one course in which they
read sources in a language other than English. A list of ways to fulfill the
language requirement, including by earning a language citation, is available on
the History & Literature website at http://histlit.fas.harvard.edu/language-
requirement.
2. Tutorials:
a. Sophomore year: HIST-LIT 97 (one course). Required. Letter-graded.
b. Junior year: HIST-LIT 98 (two courses). Required. Letter-graded.
c. Senior year: HIST-LIT 99 (two courses). Required. Graded SAT/UNS.
3. Junior essay: A required 6,000-word research paper is the focus of the second
semester of the junior tutorial.
4. Senior thesis: A 10,000- to 20,000-word thesis is expected of all concentrators and is
required for concentration honors. A student enrolled in HIST-LIT 99 who does not
complete a thesis can receive credit for this course by completing a paper in the
relevant field.
5. Senior honors oral examination: A one-hour oral examination is required of all
concentrators, except those who do not submit a thesis by the deadline for
consideration for honors.
6. Other information:
a. Fields of study:
i. American Studies.
ii. Ethnic Studies.
iii. European Studies.
iv. Latin American Studies.
v. Urban Studies
vi. Modern World.
vii. World before 1800.
viii. Medieval World.
b. Subfields: In the junior year, concentrators may propose subfields, which
consist of two or three courses that would not otherwise count toward the
student’s field of study. Students will be examined on the subfield during the
senior oral exam.
c. Study abroad: History & Literature encourages study abroad. Generally,
students will take an additional course that counts for concentration credit to
replace the tutorial they miss while abroad. Students who successfully
complete the junior essay process while studying abroad in the junior spring
will receive a one-course reduction in the 14 total required concentration
courses.
d. Independent study: With the permission of the Director of Studies,
concentrators may be allowed to take HIST-LIT 91R for credit.
e. Joint concentration: History & Literature welcomes joint concentrations, but
must be the primary honors concentration in any joint Plan of Study, except
when the student plans to pursue a creative senior project through
departments like AFVS, TDM, Music, and English. Students may count one
semester of the other concentration’s Junior Tutorial to take the place of
History & Literature’s.
f. Double concentration: History & Literature’s flexible requirements and
interdisciplinary nature also make it an ideal choice for students considering a
double concentration. Because a thesis is required, most students choose to

designate it as the honors field.
ADVISING
History & Literature prides itself on the close advising and mentoring it provides to students.
Each year, a student’s tutorial leader also acts as that student’s academic advisor in order to
help assemble a Plan of Study that fits the student’s intellectual interests as they evolve. Each
student is also assigned a concentration advisor who will be a resource for the student
through every stage of the concentration. History & Literature’s personalized academic
advising gives students a flexible, individualized educational experience.
