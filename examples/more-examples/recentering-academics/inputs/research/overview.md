# Grading research overview: the full range and curbing inflation

A synthesis of the external evidence base on two linked questions: how to grade across the
full range (distinguishing satisfactory, good, and excellent) defensibly and consistently,
and which interventions to curb grade inflation actually work. It draws on four collected
sources, listed with links under [Sources](#sources) below. Complements the Harvard-specific
material in [../harvard-context/](../harvard-context/) and the Bok framework in
[../bok-advice/](../bok-advice/).

The honest headline: the full range is recovered not by *withholding* high grades but by
making explicit what each level of quality is and grading to it. Criterion-based standards
have the best track record; numeric mean caps work but are costly; and publishing grade data
to students can backfire.

---

## Part 1 — Validated practices for grading across the full range

### 1. Grade against explicit standards, not against the class (criterion-referenced)

Separate two things Sadler (2005) shows are routinely confused:

- **Criteria** — the dimensions you look at (argument, evidence, method, clarity).
- **Standards** — how good a piece has to be on each criterion to earn each grade.

Norm-referenced grading ("top X% get A's") creates a fixed pool students compete over.
Criterion-referenced grading ties the grade to the quality of the work, so the full range
emerges naturally: if much of the work is genuinely excellent it earns A's, but the standard
— not a curve — decides. Sadler's caution is that criteria-based grading only delivers when
both criteria *and* standards are made explicit; otherwise the real judgment stays tacit and
hidden from students. Write down, for each major assignment, what A-level, B-level, and
C-level work looks like before grading begins.

### 2. Use analytic, topic-specific rubrics with exemplars and rater training

This is the single best-supported practice. Jonsson and Svingby's (2007) review of 75
studies found scoring reliability improves most when rubrics are:

- **Analytic** — separate scales per criterion, rather than one holistic impression;
- **Topic-specific** — written for this assignment, not generic;
- **Paired with exemplars** — annotated samples of A, B, and C work that make the standard
  visible (Sadler's point that standards stay tacit until shown); and
- **Backed by rater training** — graders practice on common samples first.

Two cautions from the same review: rubrics improve *reliability* but do not guarantee
*validity* — a clear rubric for the wrong construct is still wrong — and the rubric must
target the skill the assignment is actually meant to assess.

### 3. Make the standard public to students in advance

Distributing the rubric and exemplars to students before they work is supported on two
fronts: it improves scoring consistency (Jonsson and Svingby) and it tends to support
learning rather than just measuring it. It also reframes a wider grade range as fair rather
than punitive — students can see what excellent work requires, so a B reads as accurate
feedback against a known standard, not as an arbitrary deflation.

### 4. Norm graders to the standard (rater training / calibration)

When multiple graders or TFs are involved, consistency across them is itself a reliability
issue (and a Bok assignment-design expectation). Jonsson and Svingby (2007) find that rater
training — graders practicing on common samples — is one of the conditions under which
rubrics most improve scoring consistency. Calibration meetings, where graders score shared
samples together and reconcile differences, operationalize this and double as faculty
development by forcing the standard to be made explicit. Pair calibration with the rubrics
and exemplars from practice 2; the point is to build a shared standard, not to add procedure.

### 5. Design assessments that produce gradeable differences

A full range is only possible if the assessment can elicit a range. Tasks with a low ceiling
(completion-style work, recall) compress everyone near the top; tasks that require analysis,
synthesis, transfer to a new case, or defended judgment spread performance out and give the
rubric something to discriminate. This connects directly to the Bok assignment-design
guidance in [../bok-advice/designing-assignments.md](../bok-advice/designing-assignments.md):
assess mastery rather than completion, and make end-of-term work cumulative.

## Part 2 — Interventions against grade inflation

### 1. Anchor grades to explicit standards (best-supported)

**Intervention:** Replace "how many A's should I give?" with published descriptions of what
A, B, and C work *is*, and grade against those — criterion-referenced rather than
norm-referenced grading (the practices in Part 1).

**Evidence:** Sadler (2005) and Jonsson and Svingby (2007), as above: explicit criteria,
standards, exemplars, and rater training are what make grading reliable and the full range
defensible.

**Why it travels better than a cap:** a standard restores the full range without creating a
fixed pool of A's that students compete over.

### 2. Department-level mean caps (works, but costly)

**Intervention:** Cap the *mean* grade in a class or department (Wellesley capped the mean
in intro/200-level courses with ≥10 students at 3.33, a B+).

**Evidence:** Butcher, McEwan, and Weerapana (2014) found the cap did reduce grade
compression at the top in the high-grading departments it bound on, with only a modest
effect on top honors. But it carried real costs: enrollments and majors shifted *away* from
the deflated departments, and student course-evaluation ratings there fell about 0.11 on a
4-point scale.

**How to use it:** a mean cap can restore spread, but it penalizes the departments and
individual faculty who comply, which is why such policies are hard to sustain. If used, it
should be adopted across departments together so rigor isn't punished unit-by-unit, and
paired with protection for faculty evaluations.

### 3. Publishing grade data — to whom matters

**Intervention:** Make grade distributions visible to pressure lenient grading.

**Evidence:** Cornell posted course *median* grades online in 1998, expecting transparency
to shame grades down. Bar, Kadiyali, and Zussman (2009) found the opposite: students used
the medians to seek out leniently graded courses, and the net effect *accelerated*
inflation.

**How to use it:** publishing grade data *to students* without changing standards rewards
easy graders and can make inflation worse. Distribution data is more safely used
internally — for faculty calibration and department review — than as a public course-shopping
signal. (Note: contextualizing transcripts by also reporting class median *alongside* a
student's grade is a distinct idea aimed at readers of the transcript, not at course
shoppers; it was not what Cornell's online posting tested.)

### 4. Calibration and moderation among graders

**Intervention:** Calibration meetings, where graders score shared samples together and
reconcile differences, to align everyone to a common standard.

**Evidence:** Jonsson and Svingby (2007) find rater training — practice on common samples —
is one of the conditions under which rubrics most improve scoring consistency. Calibration
operationalizes this and doubles as faculty development by surfacing what a standard
actually means.

**How to use it:** adopt calibration primarily for *shared standard-setting and TF/section
consistency* (which the Bok assignment-design guidance also calls for), paired with the
explicit rubrics and exemplars of intervention 1.

### Putting the interventions together

The interventions with the strongest and least double-edged evidence are the
*standards-based* ones (1 and 4): define what each grade level means, make it public with
exemplars, and norm graders to it. A department-level mean cap (2) can restore spread but
imposes real costs and is most defensible when adopted collectively and anchored to
standards rather than to headcounts. Transparency aimed at students (3) is the one to avoid
as a primary tool.
