# Recentering Academics — Syllabus Redesign: LING 5312 Language & Politics

- **Course:** LING 5312, Language & Politics (Fall 2024; Tues/Thurs 9:30–10:45)
- **Source syllabus:** [inputs/syllabus/sample-syllabus.docx](../inputs/syllabus/sample-syllabus.docx)
- **Framework:** Bok Center, [Recentering Academics](../inputs/bok-advice/recentering-academics.md) (overview + course policies, assignment design, class sessions)
- **Institutional context:** Harvard OUE, [Report on Grading and Workload](../inputs/harvard-context/harvard-grade-report.md) (Oct 2025)
- **Research evidence:** [grading-research overview](../inputs/research/overview.md) (Sadler 2005; Jonsson & Svingby 2007)
- **Date:** 2026-06-03

This syllabus already centers academics better than most: it is built on the student's
own empirical data (fieldnotes, an original research project), it grades across a full
A–F range, and it carries a genuinely thoughtful generative-AI policy. The single
biggest gap is a weighting problem — a quarter of the grade rides on Hypothes.is
annotations, the component most easily produced by an LLM — compounded by soft
attendance language and no in-class device policy. The redesign below sharpens those
edges without disturbing what already works.

(Note: this is used as a representative sample syllabus from another institution, so
references below to "Bok expectation" are the Recentering Academics standard applied to
it as an example, not a claim that the course is at Harvard.)

## Recommended revisions

### Pillar 1 — Meaningful and engaged classroom experience

**Attendance & participation language.** *Bok principle:* set clear expectations and make
presence matter; reward participation.

- **Currently:** "Please make an effort to be punctual and present. If you miss more than
  2 classes, please contact me regarding make-up work." (10% of grade)
- **Revise to:** "This is a discussion-and-workshop course; your presence is part of the
  work. Attendance and active participation count for [15]% of your grade. More than two
  unexcused absences will lower this component; if you must miss class, contact me in
  advance. Participation is assessed on the clearly communicated standard below [link to
  rubric], not merely on being present." Raising the weight and naming a standard follows
  the guidance to reward and clearly assess participation.

**Add an in-class device policy.** *Bok principle:* limit internet-enabled devices except
when directed; eliminate distractions so in-class activity matters.

- **Currently:** (not addressed)
- **Revise to add:** "Devices in class: because much of our class time is live data
  analysis and discussion, please keep laptops and phones closed except during designated
  workshop segments, when I'll ask you to open them for analysis. Bring readings on paper
  or as annotated PDFs you don't need to browse away from." This protects the
  lecture/discussion/workshop format the syllabus already names.

### Pillar 2 — Rigorous assessment and feedback

**Rebalance away from the most AI-exposed component.** *Bok principle:* assignments should
yield work that is reliable evidence of the student's own thinking, not AI usage, and
should assess skills rather than reward completion.

- **Currently:** "Hypothes.is annotations 25%" — open-text annotations of readings, the
  component an LLM can generate most convincingly.
- **Revise to:** lower annotations to ~15% and shift the freed weight to the fieldnotes
  and in-class analysis (work grounded in the student's own observation). Keep
  Hypothes.is for the engagement it creates, but grade it on specificity — annotations
  must quote and respond to the exact passage and connect it to a class concept — so that
  generic AI-generated commentary scores poorly. This also exercises the existing AI
  policy's "support, don't substitute" line on the assignment where it bites hardest.

**Make Project #2 cumulative on Project #1.** *Bok principle:* end-of-semester assessment
should be cumulative.

- **Currently:** "20 points for Project #1 (5–6 pages)... 30 points for Project #2 (10–12
  pages)" — two projects, sequential but independent.
- **Revise to:** require Project #2 to extend, deepen, or explicitly respond to Project #1
  (more data, a refined research question, or a methodological critique of the first
  attempt). A capstone that builds on a student's own earlier work is both more rigorous
  and harder to outsource than a fresh prompt.

**Attach an analytic rubric with exemplars to the major work and grade against an explicit
standard.** *Principles:* the Bok guidance to provide "clear directions, including rubrics
or grading expectations"; Harvard's ask that faculty "articulate their grading standards"
so students know that quality, not effort, is what grades measure; and the research that a
broad scale only becomes meaningful when the standard is made explicit — criterion-
referenced rather than curved (Sadler 2005), with analytic, topic-specific rubrics paired
with annotated exemplars and the strongest evidence behind them (Jonsson & Svingby 2007).

- **Currently:** point values are given, but no rubric is referenced for the projects or
  participation, so the published A–F scale rests on a tacit standard.
- **Revise to add:** an **analytic** rubric for the research projects (research question,
  data handling, analytic argument, engagement with the literature, clarity) — separate
  scales per criterion rather than one holistic score — and a short participation rubric,
  both distributed *with* the assignment. Pair the project rubric with one or two
  **annotated exemplars** showing what A-level vs. B-level work looks like on each
  criterion. This converts the already-broad A–F range from a number into a defensible
  standard: a B reads as accurate feedback against a known bar, not an arbitrary
  deflation.

## Drafted / strengthened generative-AI policy

The existing policy is good and should be kept as the spine. Strengthen it by binding it
to this course's actual assignments, following the Bok guidance that an AI policy works
best when aligned to specific learning objectives:

> **Generative AI in this course.** Learning to use AI tools well is becoming a
> professional skill, and you may use them to support your work — brainstorming,
> debugging a data script, getting a synonym — *as long as you disclose and document the
> use* (a chatlog or a short note describing how you used the tool). What you may not do
> is submit AI-generated text as your own writing or analysis; doing so is academic
> dishonesty.
>
> Applied to our assignments:
> - **Research projects and fieldnotes:** the analysis and writing must be yours. These
>   assess *your* ability to design research and interpret discourse data — the central
>   learning objectives of the course — so AI may help you brainstorm or check phrasing,
>   but not generate findings, interpretations, or prose. Document any use.
> - **Hypothes.is annotations:** these are your own first-pass reactions to the reading;
>   AI-generated annotations defeat their purpose and will be graded as non-responsive.
> - **Data work:** you may use AI to help write or debug code for organizing or querying
>   your data; note that you did so.
>
> Never cite an AI tool as a source of fact: find and cite the original source. If you're
> unsure whether a use is allowed, ask me before you do it.

## What to keep

These elements already center academics; don't revise them away:

- **The empirical research project with the student's own data** — the course's
  capstone is AI-resilient by design, because the work is grounded in data the student
  collects and analyzes.
- **Fieldnotes plus peer comments** — original observation plus visible engagement with
  classmates' work; active, cumulative, and hard to outsource.
- **In-class presentations** — oral defense of one's own work is among the most
  AI-resilient assessments available.
- **The full A–F grading scale** — already does what Harvard's grading report asks: a
  range that can distinguish satisfactory, good, and excellent, at a moment when 60.2% of
  Harvard grades are A's. Keep the scale; the only thing missing is an explicit standard
  (rubric + exemplars) to grade against, added above.
- **The clear late-work policy and the existing AI policy** — both are specific and
  enforceable; build on them rather than replacing them.
