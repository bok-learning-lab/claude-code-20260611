# Recentering Academics — Linguistics Concentration

- **Concentration:** Linguistics (primary field)
- **Source profile:** [inputs/fields-of-concentration/primary/linguistics.md](../inputs/fields-of-concentration/primary/linguistics.md)
- **Framework:** Bok Center, [Recentering Academics](../inputs/bok-advice/recentering-academics.md) (overview + course policies, assignment design, class sessions)
- **Institutional context:** Harvard OUE, [Report on Grading and Workload](../inputs/harvard-context/harvard-grade-report.md) (Oct 2025) and the [Subcommittee on Grading proposal](../inputs/harvard-context/proposal-grading-policies.md) (Feb 2026)
- **Research evidence:** [grading-research overview](../inputs/research/overview.md) (Sadler 2005; Jonsson & Svingby 2007; Butcher et al. 2014; Bar et al. 2009)
- **Date:** 2026-06-03

## What's at stake for this discipline

Linguistics is in an unusual position relative to generative AI: its object of study —
language — is the very thing large language models manipulate, and several of its core
undergraduate tasks (grammaticality judgments, glossing, parsing, rule-writing,
problem sets in phonology and syntax) are exactly the kind of short, structured,
text-based work an LLM will produce a fluent answer for. That fluent answer is also
frequently *wrong* in ways a beginner cannot detect, which makes the discipline's
signature skill — **constructing and defending a linguistic argument from data** — both
the thing most worth protecting and the thing most easily faked. At the same time, the
concentration is structurally well-defended: it is small (22 concentrators as of
December 2025), tutorial-heavy, thesis-oriented, and built around in-person
argumentation. The work below is less about adding scaffolding from scratch than about
naming what the AI era changes for assessment in the introductory and core method
courses, where the protective structure of tutorials hasn't yet kicked in.

There is a second pressure the discipline shares with the rest of the College, and it is
the one Harvard's October 2025 grading report puts first: grade compression. With A's now
60.2% of all College grades (up from 24% in 2005), grades have stopped distinguishing
satisfactory, good, and excellent work — the "information" and "distinction" functions
the report says grading has lost. Linguistics is comparatively well-positioned here,
because its signature task (a defended analysis of data) is exactly the kind of work that,
graded against an explicit standard, *produces* a real range rather than compressing
everyone at the top. The opportunity is to make that standard explicit — which is both
the research's best-supported lever (Sadler 2005; Jonsson & Svingby 2007) and what
Harvard is now asking every department to do.

## What this concentration already does well

These existing requirements already center academics in the way the Bok framework asks
for. They should be preserved and pointed to, not rebuilt:

- **A three-year tutorial spine.** LING 97 (sophomore spring, small-group), LING 98A/98B
  (junior), and LING 99A/99B (senior thesis) put students in small, in-person,
  argumentation-driven settings for three consecutive years. Small-group tutorials whose
  "specific topics change from year to year" are inherently resistant to outsourced work
  and embody the Bok call for sessions where "being present matters."
- **Core courses explicitly built around argumentation and method.** The profile states
  the required sequence (LING 102 syntax, 105 phonology, 106 semantics, plus a historical
  course) emphasizes "argumentation and methodology." That is precisely the
  reliable-evidence-of-own-thinking standard the assignment-design guidance demands —
  the courses are already named for the skill, not the content.
- **A required thesis and colloquium for honors candidates.** A prospectus, an advisor
  tutorial (LING 99B), and a public departmental colloquium presentation during Reading
  Period make the capstone cumulative and orally defended — a near-ideal AI-resilient
  assessment.
- **A genuine language requirement and (for honors) a second language.** This keeps
  students doing sustained, individually-demonstrated work that cannot be completed by
  prompting.

## Recommendations

### Pillar 1 — Meaningful and engaged classroom experience

- **Make the intro courses (LING 83, LING 101, First-Year Seminars) the place where the
  discipline's "you can't just generate this" lesson is taught explicitly.** These are
  the entry points named in the profile, and most concentrators "discover" linguistics
  here with no prior exposure. *Bok principle:* connect each session to the skills
  students need to learn, and design in-class activities with an active role so presence
  matters. *Concretely:* run in-class data-analysis workshops (a phonology dataset, a
  syntactic ambiguity, a reconstruction problem) where students build the analysis live,
  on paper or board, in small groups — the same elicitation-and-argument move the field
  runs on, done where it can be observed.
- **Adopt a department-wide expectation that core method courses (102/104/105/106/107/108)
  do real analysis in class, not only lecture.** The profile frames these as
  argumentation-and-methodology courses; the facilitation guidance asks that "in-class
  content depends on assignments and vice-versa." *Concretely:* pair each problem set
  with a short in-class segment where students extend or defend their own solution, so
  the out-of-class work is the price of admission to the in-class work.
- **Set a clear device policy for problem-solving sessions.** When the class activity is
  working a tree or a derivation, the Bok guidance to "limit or prohibit student use of
  internet-enabled devices except when explicitly directed" is well-matched: the skill is
  the by-hand analysis, and a laptop open to an LLM defeats the exercise. Frame it as
  protecting the workshop, not policing students.

### Pillar 2 — Rigorous assessment and feedback

- **Treat the problem set as the most AI-exposed assessment in the concentration and
  redesign accordingly.** Phonology, syntax, and morphology problem sets are short,
  structured, and exactly what an LLM answers fluently and often wrongly. *Bok principle:*
  assignments should "yield student work that is reliable evidence of their own thinking
  rather than AI usage" and "assess skills... rather than just rewarding completion."
  *Concretely:* (a) use novel or constructed-language datasets rather than textbook-standard
  ones; (b) require students to *show the analytic steps and justify each*, grading the
  argument not the final tree/rule; (c) move a meaningful fraction of problem-set credit
  to in-class or oral checkpoints where students explain a solution they submitted.
- **Make end-of-term assessment cumulative, building on the term's analytic work.** The
  assignment-design guidance asks that "end-of-semester assessments are cumulative." *In a
  method course:* a final analysis problem on a language not seen in class, or a take-home
  that requires synthesizing the term's phonological/syntactic machinery, assesses
  transfer in a way a single AI prompt cannot shortcut.
- **Grade the full range against an explicit standard, and norm it across tutorials and
  sections.** The profile's tutorials (LING 97, 98A) and any multi-section intro courses
  are graded by different instructors and tutorial leaders, and — like the rest of the
  College — are exposed to the compression the Harvard report documents. *Principles:* the
  Bok guidance to give TFs/tutorial leaders "clear grading rubrics" and "norm grades across
  sections"; Harvard's ask that faculty "articulate their grading standards" and reserve A
  for "extraordinary distinction"; and the research finding that the full range is
  recovered by making each quality level explicit and grading to it, not by withholding
  high grades. *Concretely:* (a) write a shared **analytic** rubric for linguistic-argument
  quality (data handling, rule formulation, counterexample treatment, clarity of
  derivation) — separate scales per criterion, not a single holistic impression (Jonsson &
  Svingby 2007); (b) pair it with **annotated A/B/C exemplars** of student analyses, since
  the standard stays tacit until it is shown (Sadler 2005); (c) grade against those
  criterion-referenced standards rather than a curve, so a genuinely excellent cohort earns
  A's but the standard, not a quota, decides; and (d) **calibrate** graders by having
  tutorial leaders and TFs score shared samples together before final grades — the single
  best-supported route to consistency across sections, and one that doubles as faculty
  development by forcing the standard into the open. One thing the department should *not*
  reach for is a numeric grade cap of its own: a cap is a collective instrument, which is
  why Harvard is pursuing the 20%+4 limit on A's at the FAS level rather than asking
  departments to self-cap. A lone unit that deflates unilaterally just pays the costs the
  evidence documents — enrollments and majors shift away from the capped unit and course
  evaluations fall (Butcher et al. 2014) — while every other department still looks easier,
  a real risk for a 22-student concentration that depends on drawing students in.
- **Hold "nontraditional" work to the same standard.** Linguistics assignments include
  fieldwork write-ups, elicitation reports, and corpus/data projects. The guidance is
  explicit that creative or nontraditional assignments be "held to the same intellectual
  standards." *Concretely:* a corpus project is graded on the soundness of its analysis
  and the student's own annotation decisions, not on volume of data collected.

## A discipline-specific generative-AI stance

Linguistics cannot adopt a blanket position, because the field's relationship to LLMs
varies sharply by task. An honest, calibrated stance:

- **Embrace as a tool** in computational and data-heavy work: scripting corpus queries,
  cleaning and formatting language data, building annotation pipelines, exploring
  patterns at scale. This is legitimate disciplinary practice and worth teaching well —
  including teaching students that an LLM's *judgments about* a language are not data.
- **Fence off to protect the assessed skill** in the core analytic exercises:
  by-hand derivations, grammaticality judgments students are meant to form themselves,
  reconstruction problems, and any assignment whose purpose is to build the student's
  own analytic intuition. Here AI use defeats the point, and the policy should say so and
  why.
- **Treat as object of study, not author** wherever the course examines language itself:
  an LLM is a fascinating source of *data and error* about syntax, semantics, and
  acquisition (the profile's own emphases, and a natural fit for the Linguistics/MBB
  track's interest in language and computation). Analyzing what models get wrong is
  excellent linguistics; letting them write the analysis is not.
- **Irrelevant** to the language requirement and to elicitation/fieldwork, where the
  whole point is individually demonstrated competence or first-hand data.

The department should publish this as a short, course-adaptable statement rather than a
single blanket rule, following the Bok guidance that a clear, learning-objective-aligned
AI policy (and the Bok Illustrated Rubric for syllabus AI statements) beats silence.

## Three things to do now

1. **Write a one-page department AI stance** structured as the four-way split above
   (embrace / fence off / object of study / irrelevant), and ask every instructor to
   adapt it into their syllabus. Lowest friction, highest clarity.
2. **Redesign the problem set in one core method course** (e.g. LING 105 Sounds of
   Language) as the pilot: novel dataset, graded on justified analytic steps, with one
   in-class or oral checkpoint per major set. Use it as the template for the others.
3. **Adopt a shared analytic linguistic-argument rubric with A/B/C exemplars**, and run
   one calibration session where tutorial leaders and TFs grade common samples together.
   This is the highest-leverage grading change in both the Harvard report and the research
   (Sadler 2005; Jonsson & Svingby 2007): it makes the standard explicit, restores a
   defensible full range, and keeps feedback and grades consistent across tutorials and
   multi-section core courses — reflecting mastery rather than completion.
