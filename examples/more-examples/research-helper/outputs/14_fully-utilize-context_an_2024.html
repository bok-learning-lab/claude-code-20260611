Make Your LLM Fully Utilize the Context

Shengnan An, Zexiong Ma, Zeqi Lin, Nanning Zheng, Jian-Guang Lou · 2024

Source: arXiv:2404.16811v2 (cs.CL); code: github.com/microsoft/FILM. IAIR Xi'an Jiaotong University, Microsoft, Peking University. Preprint (venue not stated).

A training method (IN2) builds synthetic QA data that forces a model to find and combine information placed anywhere in a long context, producing a 7B model (FILM-7B) that no longer "loses information in the middle."

Neutral Summary

The paper addresses the "lost-in-the-middle" problem: long-context LLMs can technically ingest tens of thousands of tokens but fail to reliably use information located in the middle of the window. The authors hypothesize this stems from insufficient explicit supervision during long-context training — general pre-training loss is dominated by nearby tokens, and supervised fine-tuning typically places the influential system message at the start, jointly introducing a positional bias toward the beginning and end. To counteract this, they introduce INformation-INtensive (IN2) training, a purely data-driven approach teaching the model that crucial information can appear at any position.

The method constructs a synthetic long-context QA dataset from the realnewslike subset of C4. Raw texts are split into ~128-token segments. GPT-4-Turbo generates two kinds of QA pairs: (1) fine-grained awareness questions answerable from a single segment, and (2) integration/reasoning (multi-hop) questions requiring two or more segments. Target segments are shuffled with randomly sampled segments to build contexts of 4K–32K tokens, length-balanced via reject sampling. The dataset totals ~1.1M fine-grained (~63%), 300K integration (~17%), 150K short-context QA (~9%), and 200K general instruction-tuning examples (~11%). They fine-tune Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 for one epoch (~300 GPU days) to produce FILM-7B.

The authors argue Needle-in-the-Haystack is inadequate (familiar document context, an easy forward-retrieval pattern resembling induction-head copying), and introduce VAL Probing — three context styles (document, Python code, structured database) and three retrieval patterns (forward, backward, bi-directional), each ~32K tokens. FILM-7B maintains robust performance across all positions: Avg/Gap of 85.9/13.9 across all tasks — comparable to or exceeding GPT-4-Turbo (79.0/32.1) and far above the Mistral backbone (47.3/56.2) and other open long-context models. On nine real-world LongBench tasks, FILM-7B improves over its backbone (e.g., NarrativeQA 23.5→26.9 F1) and reaches SOTA-level among ~7B open models (Avg 39.9). Short-context capability is preserved (MMLU 59.3→59.2).

Training-strategy analyses report two further findings: sliding-window attention severely harms long-distance retrieval (performance collapses when query–information distance exceeds the window), and higher information intensity benefits from a larger RoPE base θ (they suggest ~10× the backbone default). Stated scope/limitations: purely data-driven; synthetic data derived only from realnewslike C4 text and GPT-4-generated QA; evaluations capped at the 32K window; 10-gram overlap pre-filtering used to avoid contamination.

Key Findings

  • Lost-in-the-middle is attributed to an unintentional positional bias in general training data rather than to architecture alone, and is addressed purely by training-data design (IN2).
  • IN2 uses GPT-4-generated QA over shuffled 128-token segments in 4K–32K contexts, with single-segment awareness and multi-segment integration tasks.
  • FILM-7B shows flat, robust performance across all context positions on VAL Probing, versus a deep mid-context dip for the untrained backbone.
  • FILM-7B reaches Avg/Gap of 85.9/13.9 across all VAL Probing tasks — comparable to or better than GPT-4-Turbo (79.0/32.1).
  • Gains transfer to real-world tasks (LongBench Avg 39.9, SOTA-level among ~7B open models; NarrativeQA F1 23.5→26.9).
  • Short-context performance is essentially unchanged (e.g., MMLU 59.3→59.2) — no significant catastrophic forgetting.
  • Needle-in-the-Haystack overestimates long-context ability; VAL Probing's code/structured contexts and backward/bi-directional patterns are more challenging.
  • Sliding-window attention cannot capture long-distance information even with IN2; a larger RoPE θ (~10× default) helps under higher information intensity.

The Twist — Implications for teaching, learning & research with LLM harnesses

This paper is directly relevant to a foundational, often-invisible assumption people make when using LLM harnesses like Claude Code: that if you put a document, codebase, or set of readings "into context," the model will use all of it equally well. The paper's central, empirically grounded message is that this is not safe to assume. Even capable long-context models exhibit lost-in-the-middle behavior, and — importantly for educators — the authors show this is most acute precisely on the patterns that matter for real work: backward retrieval (finding a definition given a name that appears earlier), structured-data retrieval, and integration across scattered pieces, not the easy "answer follows the keyword" pattern. For an instructor or researcher pasting a long syllabus, a multi-file codebase, or a stack of papers into Claude Code and asking a synthesizing question, the honest takeaway is: place critical material deliberately, ask the model to integrate explicitly, and verify multi-source claims rather than trusting uniform coverage.

A second concrete bridge: the evidence that sliding-window attention greatly hurts long-distance retrieval, and that near-perfect Needle-in-the-Haystack scores can overstate true capability, gives instructors a defensible reason to teach skeptical evaluation of long-context tools. A useful classroom or research-methods lesson directly motivated by this paper is to have students run their own "VAL-style" probes — hide a fact in the middle of a long document and ask a tool to retrieve and combine it — so they learn experimentally that benchmark headline numbers and real reliability can diverge. The paper's data-construction recipe (single-fact awareness vs. multi-hop integration) is itself a clean template for designing such literacy exercises.

It is worth marking the limits of relevance honestly. This is a model-training paper about a 7B research model (FILM-7B), not about Claude or about agentic coding harnesses; it makes no claims about Claude Code, retrieval-augmented or tool-using agents, or pedagogy. Any statement that a specific frontier model used in Claude Code does or does not lose information in the middle is speculative with respect to this paper — it only evaluates FILM-7B, Mistral, LongAlign, InternLM2, and GPT-3.5/4-Turbo, and only up to a 32K window. The suggestion that the IN2 dataset format could be repurposed as a teaching tool for AI literacy is a reasonable but speculative extension the authors do not make.

The faithful bridge, then, is conceptual rather than prescriptive: this paper supplies rigorous evidence for a habit instructors and students should adopt when using any LLM harness for teaching, learning, or research — treat "it's in the context" as a hypothesis to test, not a guarantee; favor explicit instructions to integrate dispersed information; position the most important material thoughtfully; and remain especially cautious with code and structured data, where the paper shows retrieval is hardest and least uniform.
