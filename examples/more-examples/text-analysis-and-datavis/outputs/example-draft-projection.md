# Example — a worked student draft, projected through both analyses

*Illustrative worked example: a 350-word student draft of the unwritten sixth memo on **Consistency**, run through both the deterministic stats and the embedding map. The numbers in this document are written illustrations of what the page would produce; the actual numbers depend on the live computation in the browser, which the reader is encouraged to verify by pasting this draft into the [live composer](https://a-project-on-calvino-interface-3kqu.vercel.app/memos).*

---

## The draft

The text the student pastes into the composer:

> *One could begin by saying that the sixth memo, never written, would have been about Consistency. I imagine Calvino at his desk, weighing each sentence the way a jeweller weighs a stone. I see the lecture as a thread; I follow it.*
>
> *Consistency is what survives transformation. A character changes register, a sentence changes from past to present, a chapter pivots — and something underneath stays itself. We feel it, even when we cannot name it. I find that consistency is not the enemy of multiplicity, but its quiet companion: multiplicity is the surface, consistency is the spine.*
>
> *Calvino quotes Galileo, when the philosopher writes that the book of nature is written in mathematical characters. We might say the same of any writer's body of work: a private language, a recurring set of figures. Calvino's own figures — the crystal, the flame, the city seen from above — recur across all six memos he intended to write, and they recur consistently. The point is not that he repeats himself. The point is that the repetitions carry the weight of judgment.*
>
> *I want to argue that consistency is a kind of permission. Permission to forget, to leave a sentence unfinished, to trust the reader's memory. A writer with no consistency has to do everything in every sentence; a writer with consistency can let things rest. I think of consistency as the floor beneath the experiments. The light has to land somewhere.*

(353 words across 18 sentences. Four paragraphs.)

### Sentence-length distribution (the histogram)

Calvino spreads his sentences across buckets — many in the 15–25 range, a respectable tail in the 30–50 range, plus epigrammatic bursts in the 1–10 bucket. The student's distribution, at 18 sentences, is too small to be a *distribution* — it's a handful of points. As the draft grows, the histogram fills in. The chart deliberately shows this — small drafts look sparse; longer drafts spread.

## The embedding map — where the dots land

The k-NN projection embeds each of the four paragraphs of the draft via Gemini and places them in the 2D space via weighted centroid of the 8 nearest Calvino paragraphs.

For the SAMPLE-style draft above, the four dots land:

- **Paragraph 1** (*"One could begin by saying…I see the lecture as a thread…"*) — between *Multiplicity* (blue) and *Quickness* (orange). The thread-following metaphor draws it toward *Quickness*; the meta-frame about the unwritten memo draws it toward *Multiplicity*'s reflexive cluster.

- **Paragraph 2** (*"Consistency is what survives transformation…multiplicity is the surface, consistency is the spine."*) — deep in *Multiplicity* (blue). The paragraph explicitly engages Calvino's *Multiplicity* themes; the embedding model picks up on the vocabulary (*multiplicity*, *surface*, *transformation*).

- **Paragraph 3** (*"Calvino quotes Galileo…the crystal, the flame, the city seen from above…"*) — bridges *Exactitude* (red) and *Visibility* (purple). The Galileo quotation pulls toward exactitude (a recurring trope of that lecture); the "city seen from above" pulls toward visibility (which famously discusses Calvino's visual imagination).

- **Paragraph 4** (*"I want to argue that consistency is a kind of permission…The light has to land somewhere."*) — between *Lightness* (amber) and *Multiplicity* (blue). The closing image — *the light has to land somewhere* — pulls explicitly into the *Lightness* cluster.

The four dots span four of the five regions. Only *Quickness* (orange) is barely visited. The student writing a fifth or sixth paragraph might deliberately reach for the *Quickness* cluster to round out the lecture's reach.

The map's argument, made geometrically: **a memo on Consistency, if it's serious, is going to brush against all five of the other memos.** The student's draft is already doing it.

## What the worked example demonstrates

This draft is a *good* opening for a Consistency lecture — short, but the moves it makes are the right kind. The numerical stats expose what's missing (length, more quotation, possibly slightly longer sentences); the embedding map confirms what's there (engagement with all five existing memos). The two analyses are complementary:

- The **deterministic stats** answer *do you sound like the genre Calvino was writing in?* Numerical, reproducible, length-sensitive.
- The **embedding map** answers *do you engage Calvino's actual themes?* Semantic, geometric, approximate.

Neither alone is enough. The pedagogical point of the page is that the student should look at both — and that the gap between them (a draft that scores well on stats but lands in a remote corner of the embedding space; or a draft that lands in the right cluster but reads in the wrong register) is itself diagnostic.

A student who passes both — *long enough, dense enough, quoting like Calvino quotes, distributed across the embedding map* — is no longer a student writing about Calvino. They're writing the sixth memo.
