# The Broken Surface — lecture notes

*Working notes for a one-hour undergraduate lecture on late nineteenth-century painting
in France (c. 1872–1897). Plain text only — no slides built yet. Each `SHOW:` line marks
a work I want on screen at that moment; I'll find good images later. Every work should be
something Harvard actually owns, so students can walk over to the Fogg and test my claims
against the real canvas.*

**Throughline:** a generation took the picture apart — first light, then structure, then
feeling — and never quite put it back together. Start with Impressionism as a *quarrel
about finish*, move through Monet's serial obsession, fan out into the four Post-Impressionist
"exits," detour through Montmartre after dark, and close with the Anglo-American coda.

---

## Part One — The Quarrel with Finish

Open with the 1874 anecdote: a critic stands before a hazy Monet harbor and sneers
"*Impression*" — meaning unfinished, a sketch passed off as a painting. The hour is the
story of how that insult became a program.

Core point: Impressionism is an argument about *where a painting should stop*. The academic
picture is finished in the studio, brushwork polished out of sight. Monet does the opposite —
paints on the spot, in one sitting, chasing light that won't hold still, and lets you see
every decision.

- SHOW: Claude Monet, *View of the Seine, Lavacourt*, 1880. — Stand close, the water is
  separate loaded strokes; step back, it resolves into a river. That gap between touch and
  illusion is the whole argument. He trusts your eye to do the finishing he refused to do.

Then: light *is* the color, not a neutral medium that reveals it. Best shown in snow.

- SHOW: Camille Pissarro, *The Hermitage, Effect of Snow*, 1874. — Almost no white. Snow
  built from lavender, ochre, pale green, grey, set side by side so the eye mixes them into
  cold light. The creed in one field: shadow has color; color is relative to its neighbors.

Impressionism was never only landscape — equally a painting of *modern life*, caught as if
glimpsed in passing. Pair these two:

- SHOW: Edgar Degas, *Singer with a Glove*, c. 1878 (pastel). — Café-concert performer lit
  from below by footlights, mouth open mid-note, gloved hand thrust at us. Cropped like a
  photograph; a gesture no academy would dignify.
- SHOW: Pierre-Auguste Renoir, *Seated Bather*, c. 1883–84. — Renoir gets uneasy with pure
  dissolution and firms the contour back up. Impressionism beginning to doubt itself.

## Part Two — The Same Place, Again and Again

By the 1890s Monet presses his own logic to the limit. If a painting records light at one
moment, then to paint the *same* motif over and over is to paint time itself. The subject
thins to a pretext; what's left is atmosphere, surface, sensation.

- SHOW: Claude Monet, *Gorge of the Petit Ailly, Varengeville*, 1897. — One of a Normandy
  cliff series. The cleft of land is barely described; it's really about the weight of haze
  and warm light vibrating against cool. The motif is now an excuse for pure looking.

Transition / pull-quote idea: Impressionism won the argument about light — and in winning it,
exhausted it. The next generation asks: is that *all* a painting can be?

## Part Three — Four Ways Out

We call the answer *Post-Impressionism*, but the word hides how little they agreed. A shared
dissatisfaction, not a shared method. Four painters, four exits — **structure, science,
emotion, symbol.**

**Structure — Cézanne.** Keeps the bright Impressionist palette but wants the permanence the
academy had and Monet threw away. Builds form from planes of color; each stroke a small
architectural decision.

- SHOW: Paul Cézanne, *Still Life with Game Birds*, c. 1872–73. — Forms sit solidly — weighed,
  constructed, almost sculptural — next to Monet's dissolving river. He wanted "to make of
  Impressionism something solid and durable, like the art of the museums." This runs straight
  into Cubism a generation on.

**Science — Seurat.** Takes the insight about optical color mixing and makes it a discipline.
If the eye blends adjacent colors, place them deliberately — dot by dot, by law not feeling.

- SHOW: Georges Seurat, *Seated Figures*, study for *A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La
  Grande Jatte*, 1884–85. — A working study for one of the era's great machines. Spontaneity
  refrozen into method. Feels eternal and a little airless — which is the point.

**Emotion — Van Gogh.** Color and stroke aren't optical facts to record but feelings to
discharge. The brush carries a pulse. Pair these:

- SHOW: Vincent van Gogh, *Self-Portrait Dedicated to Paul Gauguin*, 1888. — A gift before
  their months together in Arles. Face carved from restless strokes, eyes slightly aslant,
  watchful. The paint is the man.
- SHOW: Vincent van Gogh, *Three Pairs of Shoes*, 1886–87. — Worn boots painted with the
  gravity of a portrait. Humble objects as carriers of a life — the charged ordinariness
  later thinkers wrote whole essays about.

**Symbol — Gauguin.** Abandons European optical truth: flattened shape, arbitrary color, a
hunger for the "primitive." Toward dream and myth — and, say it plainly, toward a colonial
fantasy of Tahiti the lecture must hold up for scrutiny, not just admire.

- SHOW: Paul Gauguin, *Poèmes Barbares*, 1896. — Painted in Tahiti. Color unhitched from
  observation, space compressed into decorative bands, a small idol against the figure.
  Beauty and ideology tangled; let students feel both the pull and the trouble.

## Part Four — Montmartre, and the Painting of the Night

Alongside the grand experiments ran a sharper, worldlier current — painters of the modern
city after dark. Toulouse-Lautrec made the cabarets and brothels of Montmartre his subject,
without sentiment.

- SHOW: Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec, *The Hangover (Suzanne Valadon)*, 1887–89. — A woman
  slumped at a café table by a half-empty bottle — the model Suzanne Valadon, herself a
  painter. Fast, unsparing drawing; a mood modern in a way that reaches into the twentieth
  century. No mythology, no flattery — a person and a bad morning.

## Part Five — The Anglo-American Coda

Close with two cosmopolitans working between Paris, London, and Boston — a reminder that
"late nineteenth-century painting" was never only French. Whistler pushes toward pure
aesthetic arrangement; Sargent toward bravura performance.

- SHOW: James McNeill Whistler, *Harmony in Grey and Peach Colour*, 1872–74. — The musical
  title is the manifesto: titled by color, not sitter. A painting is first an arrangement
  of tones — "art for art's sake." The road that ends in abstraction.
- SHOW: John Singer Sargent, *The Honorable Laura Lister*, 1896. — Absorbs the Impressionist
  touch but turns it to grand portraiture — the loaded, confident stroke that flatters and
  dazzles. Tradition modernized rather than overthrown.

## In Closing — What the Broken Surface Left Behind

We began with a complaint about a painting that looked unfinished; we end three decades later
with the surface deliberately, permanently open — to light, method, feeling, symbol. Each
painter loosened a different joint: Cézanne's planes → Cubism; Van Gogh's and Gauguin's color
→ the Fauves and Expressionists; Whistler's harmonies → abstraction.

Leave them with this: a movement is not a style you memorize but *an argument you can still
see being made*, stroke by stroke, if you stand close enough. Harvard owns every one of these
arguments. Go look.
