# Writers Named in Bob Dylan's Lyrics

A pass through 538 Dylan songs turned up **14 named writers** across **10 songs**
(some songs appear as multiple variant takes in the dataset). For each, below:
the song, the writer(s) named, the surrounding verse for context, and a stab at
why the writer is there.

Ordered by release year.

---

## "Song to Woody" — *Bob Dylan* (1962)

**Writers named:** Woody Guthrie · Lead Belly (Huddie Ledbetter)

> Hey, hey, Woody Guthrie, I wrote you a song
> 'Bout a funny, old world that's coming along.
> Seems sick and it's hungry, it's tired and it's torn.
> It looks like it's dying and it's hardly been born.

> Here's to Cisco, and Sonny, and Leadbelly too,
> And to all the good people that traveled with you.

**Why they're here:** This is the most literal case — a young Dylan's open
homage, modeled on Guthrie's own "Song to Woody"/"1913 Massacre" melody. Guthrie
is both songwriter and author (*Bound for Glory*); Lead Belly the great
folk-blues songster. Naming them is Dylan planting his flag in a lineage,
claiming the folk songwriter as a *writer* worth dedicating a song to.

## "As I Went Out One Morning" — *John Wesley Harding* (1967)

**Writer named:** Tom Paine

> As I went out one morning to breathe the air around Tom Paine's,
> I spied the fairest damsel that ever did walk in chains.
> I offered her my hand, she took me by the arm.
> I knew that very instant she meant to do me harm.

**Why he's here:** Thomas Paine — *Common Sense*, *The Rights of Man* — as the
patron saint of liberty. The song is widely read as Dylan's uneasy reckoning
with the 1963 Tom Paine Award (from the Emergency Civil Liberties Committee),
which he received and then derailed with a rambling, controversial acceptance
speech. The "damsel in chains" who means him harm reads as ideology or the
political left invoking liberty's name while shackling it — Paine becomes the
ground on which freedom is both promised and betrayed.

## "Desolation Row" — *Highway 61 Revisited* (1965)

**Writers named:** Ezra Pound · T.S. Eliot

> Praise be to Nero's Neptune, the Titanic sails at dawn,
> Everybody's shouting, "Which side are you on?"
> And Ezra Pound and T.S. Eliot fighting in the captain's tower
> While Calypso-singers laugh at them and fishermen hold flowers

**Why they're here:** The two titans of high modernist poetry — Eliot literally
edited Pound's hand in *The Waste Land* — reduced to squabbling in the tower of a
sinking ship while the folk singers and ordinary people below get on with living.
It's Dylan's sly verdict on elite literary culture: high modernism as a status
fight, irrelevant to the carnival of real life on Desolation Row. Coming from a
songwriter who'd soon be called a poet himself, it's pointed.

## "Ballad of a Thin Man" — *Highway 61 Revisited* (1965)

**Writer named:** F. Scott Fitzgerald

> Oh, you been with the professors and they've all liked your looks,
> With great lawyers you have discussed lepers and crooks,
> You been through all of F. Scott Fitzgerald's books,
> You're very well-read, it's well-known.
> But something is happening here and you don't know what it is, do you, Mr. Jones?

**Why he's here:** Fitzgerald is the credential, not the content. Mr. Jones has
*read* the books, hung with the professors — he has all the markers of cultured
literacy and still understands nothing. Fitzgerald (the great chronicler of
American social surfaces and their hollowness) is the perfect name to hang on a
man who's all surface: the reference indicts the bourgeois reader who consumes
literature as status while missing the thing happening right in front of him.

## "Stuck Inside of Mobile with the Memphis Blues Again" — *Blonde on Blonde* (1966)

**Writer named:** William Shakespeare

> Well, Shakespeare, he's in the alley with his pointed shoes and his bells
> Speaking to some French girl, who says she knows me well.
> And I would send a message to find out if she's talked,
> But the post office's been stolen and the mailbox is locked.

**Why he's here:** Shakespeare dragged off his pedestal and into the alley — in
pointed shoes and jester's bells, chatting up a French girl, as stranded and
absurd as everyone else in this surreal town. The greatest writer in English
gets no special exemption from the song's logic of comic dislocation; he's just
another hipster bard stuck in Mobile. Part of Dylan's running game of leveling
cultural giants down to street level.

## "You're Gonna Make Me Lonesome When You Go" — *Blood on the Tracks* (1975)

**Writers named:** Paul Verlaine · Arthur Rimbaud

> Situations have ended sad, relationships have all been bad -
> Mine've been like Verlaine's and Rimbaud's,
> But there's no way I can compare all them scenes to this affair,
> You're gonna make me lonesome when you go.

**Why they're here:** The doomed gay love affair of the two French Symbolist
poets — passionate, violent (Verlaine shot Rimbaud), and famously catastrophic —
as Dylan's shorthand for "my past relationships were train wrecks." Rimbaud was a
lifelong touchstone for Dylan ("I'm Rimbaud," he'd say). The reference flatters
the present love by contrast: even the most romantically literary disaster he can
name doesn't measure up to *this* affair.

## "Joey" — *Desire* (1976)

**Writers named:** Friedrich Nietzsche · Wilhelm Reich

> "Five to ten," said Joey. Judge says, "That's exactly what you get."
> He did ten years in Attica, reading Nietzsche and Wilhelm Reich,
> They threw him in the hole one time for trying to stop a strike,
> His closest friends were black men 'cause they seemed to understand
> What it's like to be in society with a shackle on your hand.

**Why they're here:** Two radical, anti-authoritarian thinkers — Nietzsche the
philosopher of will and the *Übermensch*, Reich the psychoanalyst of repression
(*The Mass Psychology of Fascism*), himself jailed in America. Citing them in
prison romanticizes mobster Joey Gallo as a self-educated rebel-intellectual, not
a thug: a man reading dangerous philosophy in his cell, befriending the
oppressed, a free mind behind bars. (The song's sympathetic portrait of a real
gangster drew lasting criticism.)

## "When You Gonna Wake Up" — *Slow Train Coming* (1979)

**Writer named:** Karl Marx

> Counterfeit philosophies have polluted all of your thoughts,
> Karl Marx has got you by the throat and Henry Kissinger's got you tied up into knots.
> When you gonna wake up, when you gonna wake up,
> When you gonna wake up, strengthen the things that remain?

**Why he's here:** From Dylan's born-again Christian period, Marx is named as a
false prophet — one of the "counterfeit philosophies" that have you by the throat.
Paired with Kissinger (realpolitik), Marx (materialist revolution) represents the
secular ideologies, left and right, that the song says have replaced God. Here the
writer is invoked to be *rejected*: the intellect to wake up *from*.

## "Highlands" — *Time Out of Mind* (1997)

**Writers named:** Erica Jong · Neil Young *(songwriter)*

> Well, she says, "You just don't seem like you do." I said, "You're way wrong."
> She says, "Which ones have you read then?" I say, "I've read Erica Jong."

> I'm listening to Neil Young, I gotta turn up the sound,
> Someone's always yelling, "Turn it down!"

**Why they're here:** In the long comic diner scene, a waitress needles the
narrator for not reading women authors, and he reaches for **Erica Jong**
(*Fear of Flying*) — the one name he can muster, a slightly desperate,
self-deprecating bid to prove he's not a philistine. **Neil Young** appears later
as the music the aging narrator turns up against the world telling him to turn it
down — a generational, who's-still-listening nod from one veteran songwriter to
another. (Young is included on the "songwriter/lyricist" criterion rather than as
a literary author.)

## "Last Thoughts on Woody Guthrie" — *The Bootleg Series Vols. 1–3* (rec. 1963, rel. 1991)

**Writer named:** Woody Guthrie

> There's this book coming out and they asked me to write … something about Woody …
> "What does Woody Guthrie mean to you in 25 words?" And I couldn't do it, I wrote
> out five pages … this is called "Last Thoughts on Woody Guthrie."

**Why he's here:** A spoken-word poem (delivered at Town Hall, 1963) that *is*
the tribute the prose blurb couldn't be — Dylan unable to compress his debt to
Guthrie into 25 words and spilling into five pages of free verse instead. Guthrie
the author/songwriter is the explicit occasion and addressee of the whole piece:
the mentor whose meaning overflows the assignment.

---

### A note on judgment calls

- **Neil Young** is the one borderline inclusion — counted as a *songwriter*. If
  you want literary writers only, drop him and the count is 13.
- Names checked and **excluded** as non-writers / false hits: Robert Frost
  ("windows filled with *frost*"), Pound (verb — "waves will *pound*"), Swift
  ("*swift* sword"), plus actors, outlaws, saints, and characters.
- **Kerouac** does **not** appear anywhere in the lyrics, despite an early note
  to the contrary.
