# End-of-class short-answer quiz

**Topic:** Grant, Behrends & Basl, "What we owe to decision-subjects" (2025).
**Format:** closed-book, on paper, ~10–15 minutes. Six short-answer items; one or two
sentences each unless noted. The aim is to check grasp of the *core argument*, not recall
of examples.

> **Instructor note.** A student-facing version (questions only) and an answer key follow.
> Print pages 1–2 for students; keep the key. Suggested weighting: Q1–Q3 are the load-bearing
> conceptual checks (2 pts each); Q4–Q6 apply the framework (1 pt each). Total 9 pts.

---

## Student version

**Name: ______________________**

Answer briefly in your own words. One or two sentences per item unless told otherwise.

**1.** State the paper's main thesis (the "Explainability Thesis") in one sentence. Your
sentence must include at least one of the authors' hedges (e.g. "in many contexts," "often,"
"prima facie") — and explain in a second sentence why that hedge matters.

**2.** The standard defense of the thesis appeals to *duties of transparency*. The authors
say that defense is too narrow and offer a different ground: *due consideration*. In two or
three sentences, explain the difference — and give one reason transparency alone can't carry
the thesis.

**3.** The authors split duties of consideration into **evidential** and **practical**.
What does each one constrain? Give one sentence for each.

**4.** A vendor says: "Our risk tool is more accurate than your judges, so using it is the
fair thing to do." Name one way, from the paper, that a *more accurate on average* system can
still fail a particular decision-subject. Be specific about the kind of failure.

**5.** The "Juror Substitution" thought experiment stipulates that the juror models are
perfectly accurate, fully transparent, and use the very same reasoning as the humans. Given
all that, what is the authors' remaining objection to using them? Name the duty involved.

**6.** Someone objects: "Human judges are black boxes too — we can't fully explain their
decisions either — so singling out AI is a double standard." Give the authors' main reply in
one or two sentences.

## Answer key (instructor)

Grade for the *idea*, not the wording. Section references are for your use, not required of
students.

**1. Thesis + hedge (2 pts).**
- *Thesis (1 pt):* In many contexts, decision-makers are morally obligated **not** to base
  decisions about how to treat people on the **outputs of black-box AI systems.**
- *Hedge (1 pt):* Any of "in many contexts / often / prima facie / potentially overridable,"
  *with* a reason: the claim is **not** "never use black boxes" — it is context-sensitive and
  can be outweighed; an absolute reading misstates the paper. (§1; thesis statement.)
- *Common error:* "AI is bad / unexplainable, so never use it." Dock the hedge point.

**2. Transparency vs. due consideration (2 pts).**
- *Difference (1 pt):* Duties of **transparency** are about being able to **disclose/explain**
  how a decision was made. **Due consideration** is about adopting **decision procedures that
  are appropriately responsive to the moral claims** subjects have on how they're treated — a
  constraint on *how the decision is made in the first place*, not just on what can be
  disclosed. It is **broader**.
- *Why transparency alone fails (1 pt):* any one of — many high-stakes decisions aren't
  governed by transparency duties at all (e.g. employers); there are cases where transparency
  duties are met yet a black box is fine (London's doctors); and a decision can be fully
  disclosed yet still wrong (the **coin-flipping bail judge** discloses everything and still
  fails the defendant). (§2.)
- *Look for:* the recognition that you can satisfy transparency and still fail the subject.

**3. Evidential vs. practical (2 pts, 1 each).**
- **Evidential** consideration constrains **fact-finding** — how we gather and evaluate
  evidence to form beliefs about decision-subjects.
- **Practical** consideration constrains **decision-making** — how we decide to treat them
  given those beliefs. (§3.)
- *Common error:* describing both as "being fair." Push for the fact-finding / decision-making
  split.

**4. Accuracy isn't sufficient (1 pt).** Accept either:
- **Ignoring readily available evidence** (§5.2): the system can be insensitive to evidence a
  human wouldn't overlook (the brain-tumor defendant) — a *procedural* failure to that person.
- **Morally inadmissible evidence** (§5.3): the system may base predictions on features it
  ought to set aside (race/gender, redundantly encoded via proxies — the "women's" résumé
  tool).
- *Also acceptable:* field **degradation / overfitting** (§5.1) — lab accuracy may not hold in
  deployment. *Full credit requires naming a specific failure, not just "it could be biased."*
- *Bonus, not required:* gesturing at the substantive/procedural distinction — a procedure fair
  on average can still wrong an individual.

**5. Juror models (1 pt).** The remaining objection is the duty of **agential
consideration** (§7.2–7.3): certain decisions (criminal punishment) must be made by a
**full-blown moral agent who takes responsibility** and thereby respects the defendant as a
fellow member of the moral/political community. A model can do none of this — no one *owns*
the verdict. (Credit "agential consideration" or a clear description of it; the point is that
the wrong is *not* about accuracy, transparency, or the content of the rules.)

**6. Double standard reply (1 pt).** The authors **concede the worry partly generalizes** to
humans — but it doesn't sink the thesis, because there is a **third option: interpretable
models**, which are less prone to the relevant failures and whose rules can be inspected.
(Strong answers also note humans have **agential capacities** that machines lack, §7, so where
agential consideration is owed the asymmetry is principled.) (§6.)
- *Common error:* "they just deny humans are black boxes." Dock — the authors *grant* the
  premise and route around it.

---

### Quick scoring band (optional)

- **8–9:** has the core argument, including the transparency → due-consideration move.
- **5–7:** grasps the duties but blurs transparency vs. due consideration, or treats the
  thesis as absolute.
- **≤4:** reproduces "black boxes are bad" without the grounding the paper actually gives.
