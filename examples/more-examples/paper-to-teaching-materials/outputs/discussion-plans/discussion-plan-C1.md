# Discussion plan — pretrial recidivism scorer (C1)

*Produced by `/discussion-plan --case C1 --length 45`. Validated: anticipates
[S1](../student-arguments/S1-accuracy-is-all.md) and routes to §5.2.*

**Case:** [C1 — RiskPredict](../cases/C1-recidivism.md). **Move:** ignoring readily
available evidence (§5.2), against the backdrop of the argument from accuracy (§5.1).
**Time:** ~45 min.

---

## 1 · Warm-up (5 min) — surface the intuition

> "A vendor's risk tool predicts pretrial reoffending more accurately than your judges do
> unaided. Is there any reason a court shouldn't use it?"

Most students will say no — and many will reach for *accuracy* ("more accurate is more
fair") or *transparency* ("as long as we can explain it"). Get both on the board; they are
the two intuitions the rest of the session complicates.

## 2 · Core dilemma (10 min) — introduce the defeater

Hand out the brain-tumor detail: the high-scoring defendant's record traces to a condition
since corrected, evidence RiskPredict can't ingest. Now the tool that's accurate *on
average* is wrong about *this* person. Ask: "Has the court wronged him? If so, what exactly
is the wrong — and would it be cured by the vendor disclosing how the model works?"

## 3 · Positions to expect (12 min)

- **"Accuracy still wins on balance."** — the [S1](../student-arguments/S1-accuracy-is-all.md)
  position. Concede what's right (accuracy *does* bear on due consideration, §5.1), then
  press: averages don't license being insensitive to decisive individual evidence.
- **"Then just add that fact as a feature."** — push back: you can't anticipate every such
  fact; a human wouldn't have overlooked it; and a black box makes it hard to tell whether
  any given fact is even being used (§5.2).
- **"Disclosure fixes it."** — the transparency reflex. It doesn't: the evidence never
  entered the model. Use this to pry transparency apart from due consideration.

## 4 · Objection to steer toward (8 min)

Name it: ignoring readily available evidence that would benefit a particular subject is a
**procedural** failure of evidential consideration (§5.2) — distinct from average accuracy.
Introduce the **substantive vs. procedural** distinction here; it's the lever that lets
students say *how* an accurate-on-average system can still wrong an individual.

## 5 · The move that resolves it (7 min)

Due consideration is **broader than accuracy**: a procedure can be fair in the aggregate
and still fail a person by being unresponsive to evidence bearing on their case. That is
why the duty is owed to the *subject*, not satisfied by *population* statistics.

## Honest note (for the instructor)

The case shows a **procedural failure**, not that risk tools are useless or that the thesis
is "never use AI." Don't let the room conclude either. If discussion runs hot, the
interpretable third option (§6, cf. [C3](../cases/C3-interpretable-alt.md)) is the
constructive exit: a tool whose use of evidence you can actually inspect.
