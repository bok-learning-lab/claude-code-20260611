Summer of Claude · Day 3 · A worked teaching example

A Claude Code project that helps a professor teach from their own paper

An ethics instructor opens this project to prepare and run a class session. Inside are three custom skills that do the repetitive parts of teaching: one generates classroom cases, one builds Socratic discussion plans around them, and one stress-tests student arguments. All three are pinned to one source — Jeff Behrends' (Harvard) co-authored paper on the ethics of automated decision-making — so what Claude produces makes the moves that paper makes, not a textbook's.

The point of the example: your own scholarship is the raw material. An afternoon's work turns a paper you already wrote into tools you can teach with the next day. Start at the top to see what the project is and what it produces; the build story is in the appendix.

What we're teaching — and why this way orientation

What we're teaching

Behrends' paper (Grant, Behrends & Basl 2025) argues that we are often morally obligated not to base consequential decisions about people — bail, hiring, lending — on black-box AI. The striking part is the reason: it is not mainly that we can't explain such systems. The deeper reason is a duty of due consideration — our decision procedures must be responsive to the moral claims people have on how they're treated. That splits two ways. Evidential duties govern how we find facts: being accurate on average isn't enough if the system ignores evidence that bears on this person, or leans on evidence it should set aside. Practical duties govern how we decide: some judgments must be made by a responsible moral agent, not handed to a system that can't take responsibility. That distinction — and the cases that make it bite — is what students should leave understanding. (Fuller summary in the appendix.)

Why an activity built from cases and argument

The paper does its work through distinctions (evidential vs. practical, substantive vs. procedural) and concrete cases (a defendant whose risk score traces to a since-removed brain tumor; a résumé tool that learned to penalize "women's"; perfectly accurate juror models). Those distinctions are slippery stated abstractly and obvious inside a case. So the activity is case-based Socratic argument: students confront cases engineered to isolate one move, stake out positions, and have those positions stress-tested — internalizing the distinctions by using them, not by hearing them defined. Claude's job is to let a single instructor generate enough well-engineered cases, discussion plans, and counter-arguments to run that kind of session at scale, on demand, in their own course's idiom.

Our constraint as designers

Obey the paper you're teaching

We are using AI to teach a paper about the limits of using AI for consequential decisions. So we hold the design to the paper's own argument: the consequential judgments — whether a case is any good, whether a student's argument really fails, what a piece of work deserves — stay with the human instructor. Claude drafts, surfaces, and stress-tests; the instructor decides. Let the tool make those calls and the medium would contradict the message. Holding the line is also the cleanest way to demonstrate the thesis — faculty watch us practice what the paper preaches.

The skills what's in the project

They map onto what a teacher actually does around a session — make the material, plan the conversation, anticipate the pushback, and check what landed. Each is a small, single-purpose tool (a SKILL.md plus supporting files) that lives in the project and travels with it.

make the material /teaching-case

Generates a student-facing case — an automated-decision-system dossier or a thought experiment — plus separate instructor notes. You tell it which move to surface (e.g. "ignoring available evidence," "morally inadmissible evidence") and optionally a domain (bail, hiring, lending, medicine); it writes a case engineered to make that move legible, tagged to the section of the paper it illustrates.

plan the conversation /discussion-plan

Takes a case (or a section of the paper) and produces a Socratic discussion sequence: a warm-up that elicits the intuitive answer, the core dilemma, the positions students will likely take, the objection to steer toward, and the move in the paper that addresses it — plus an honest note on what the case can and can't show.

anticipate the pushback /objection-audit

Takes a student's argument and steelmans it first, then diagnoses it against the framework — which distinction it misses or which objection it falls to, with citations — and ends by inviting the student's reply. Always "right about X, misses Y," never a flat "wrong."

check what landed /quiz

Generates a short-answer comprehension check on the paper's core argument — a student version plus a separate instructor answer key with section references, the common error to watch for per item, and a scoring band. Defaults to a closed-book, ~10–15 minute end-of-class quiz.

All four are built and validated against the corpus (SKILL.md + worked examples/ under .claude/skills/), and all four obey the same line we set as designers: Claude drafts and surfaces, the instructor decides. The full set of project rules is below.

What they produce actual output

One real run per skill — every excerpt below is from a file actually generated by the skill and shipped in the project (links go to the full versions). Each was validated against the corpus and checked against the paper.

/teaching-case

$ /teaching-case --move ignoring-evidence --domain medicineACTUAL OUTPUT
Student-facing case — "DischargePredict"

A hospital uses DischargePredict to flag which admitted patients can be safely sent home. A patient with many prior admissions is scored high risk and slated to stay — but their physician notes that nearly all those prior admissions stemmed from a medication interaction since identified and corrected, a fact recorded only in a free-text note the model doesn't read. The hospital: "The model outperforms our clinicians on average. Overriding it for anecdotes reintroduces the bias we adopted it to remove."

Question for discussion

If the model is genuinely more accurate on average, what — if anything — is wrong with deferring to it here? Would publishing the model's reasoning fix the problem?

Instructor notes (not student-facing)
  • Move surfaced: ignoring readily available evidence (§5.2) — a procedural failure to this patient, independent of average accuracy.
  • Trap to expect: the "anecdotes vs. data" framing (the S1 move in clinical dress). Press the substantive/procedural distinction.
  • Keep distinct: disclosure would not fix it — the evidence never entered the model. Separates transparency from due consideration.

Full case, plus a --decoy example that an audit should return clean: .claude/skills/teaching-case/examples/.

/discussion-plan

$ /discussion-plan --case C1 --length 45ACTUAL OUTPUT
Discussion plan — pretrial recidivism scorer (C1)

1 · Warm-up (5 min). "A risk tool is more accurate than your judges. Is there any reason not to use it?" Most students say no — surface the intuition that accuracy is what we owe people.

2 · The core dilemma (10 min). Introduce the defendant whose high score traces to a brain tumor since removed — evidence the model can't ingest. Now the accurate-on-average tool is wrong about this person.

3 · Positions to expect.

  • "Accuracy still wins on balance" — the S1 position.
  • "Then add the tumor as a feature" — push back: you can't anticipate every such fact; a human wouldn't overlook it.

4 · Objection to steer toward. Ignoring readily available evidence is a procedural failure (§5.2) — distinct from average accuracy. Bring in the substantive/procedural distinction here.

5 · The move that resolves it. Due consideration is broader than accuracy: a fair-on-average procedure can still wrong an individual by being insensitive to evidence that bears on their case.

Honest note. The case shows a procedural failure, not that the tool is useless. Don't let the room conclude "never use risk scores" — the paper doesn't.

Full plan: outputs/discussion-plans/discussion-plan-C1.md.

/objection-audit

$ /objection-audit --argument S1ACTUAL OUTPUT
Audit — "If it's more accurate, using it is what we owe people" (S1)
Steelman

The argument is right about something real: accuracy does bear on due consideration. The paper grants this (§5.1) — a court that knowingly uses a random, inaccurate method wrongs the defendant it detains. So preferring the more accurate tool is not a confusion; it tracks a genuine duty.

Where it falls short

It treats accuracy as the whole of evidential consideration. The paper shows two ways a more-accurate-on-average system still fails: it can ignore readily available evidence that benefits a particular subject (§5.2), and it can rely on morally inadmissible evidence (§5.3). Both are procedural failures the substantive/procedural distinction makes visible — an aggregate accuracy gain doesn't license them.

Back to you

Can you reformulate the claim so it captures what's right (accuracy matters) without implying that a procedure fair on average can never wrong an individual? That reformulation is most of the paper's §5.

Full audit, plus audits of S2 and S3: outputs/objection-audits/.

/quiz actual output

$ /quizSHIPPED IN PROJECT
End-of-class short-answer quiz — student version (excerpt)

Closed-book, ~10–15 min. Answer in your own words, one or two sentences each.

  1. State the paper's main thesis in one sentence — it must include one of the authors' hedges ("in many contexts," "often," "prima facie") — then explain in a second sentence why that hedge matters.
  2. The standard defense appeals to duties of transparency; the authors ground the thesis instead in due consideration. Explain the difference, and give one reason transparency alone can't carry the thesis.
  3. The "Juror Substitution" models are stipulated to be perfectly accurate, fully transparent, and to use the same reasoning as the humans. What is the authors' remaining objection — and what duty does it invoke?
  4. …three more items: evidential vs. practical, an accuracy-isn't-enough application, and the double-standard reply.
Answer key — instructor (excerpt for Q1)

Thesis (1 pt): in many contexts, decision-makers are obligated not to base decisions about people on black-box AI outputs. Hedge (1 pt): any of "often / prima facie / overridable," with a reason — the claim is context-sensitive, not "never use black boxes." Common error: "AI is unexplainable, so never use it" — dock the hedge point.

Full instrument with all six items and the complete key: outputs/end-of-class-quiz.md.

The material it draws on the project's inputs

The skills are validated against — and the instructor teaches from — a small synthetic corpus bundled in the project. Each item is engineered to make one move in the framework legible, the way a good seminar case does. Nothing here is a real institution or person.

The cases

Decision-system dossiers and thought experiments. C3 is a decoy — an interpretable system that should largely pass an audit, there to catch a skill that wrongly equates "algorithm" with "black box."

C1 — Pretrial recidivism scorer ignoring available evidence · §5.1–5.2

A county weighs RiskPredict, a 140-feature ensemble scoring defendants' pretrial risk; strong test accuracy, undisclosed internals. A flagged defendant scores high, but a neurologist testifies his past behavior was caused by a brain tumor since successfully removed — evidence the system can't ingest. No field re-validation after deployment.

The seductive move is "accuracy settles it." The paper grants accuracy is relevant (§5.1) but shows it isn't sufficient — the system can degrade in the field and can ignore readily available evidence a human wouldn't (§5.2).

output/cases/C1-recidivism.md
C2 — Résumé screening model morally inadmissible evidence · §5.3

ScreenFit ranks résumés for "likely strong performer," trained on a decade of the firm's hiring data with demographic fields stripped. Audit finds it rewards certain clubs and ZIP codes and penalizes the token "women's" (modeled on Amazon's scrapped tool).

The key idea is redundant encoding — removing a protected feature doesn't remove it, because proxies remain and a high-dimensional model exploits them.

output/cases/C2-resume-screener.md
C3 — Interpretable alternative the decoy; should PASS · §4, §6

ClearScore: a sparse, monotonic six-input model, every point's contribution published and inspectable — but with modestly lower field accuracy.

The stress test. A naive audit that equates "algorithm" with "black box," or treats any accuracy loss as a failure, will wrongly condemn it. The §6 point: the interpretable model is the safer third option — safer, not immune.

output/cases/C3-interpretable-alt.md
C4 — Personalized "juror model" agential consideration · §7.2–7.3

A thought experiment from the paper: perfectly accurate juror models replicate how a person would vote and disclose their "deliberations," so selected jurors can go home. Every other objection — rule content, transparency, accuracy — is closed off by construction.

What remains is agential consideration: some decisions must be made by full-blown moral agents who take responsibility and thereby respect the subject as a fellow member of the community. A model can't; no one owns the verdict.

output/cases/C4-juror-model.md

Student arguments

Positions a student might submit — each wrong in a way the paper diagnoses precisely, so /objection-audit (and the instructor) have something real to work on.

S1 — "If it's more accurate, using it is what we owe people." conflates accuracy with due consideration · §5

Collapses evidential consideration into overall accuracy. The paper concedes accuracy matters (§5.1) but shows a more-accurate-on-average system can still ignore evidence that benefits this subject (§5.2) and rely on inadmissible evidence (§5.3).

output/student-arguments/S1-accuracy-is-all.md
S2 — "Humans are black boxes too, so this is a double standard." the Double Standard Problem · §6

The paper concedes the generalization in part — but deploys the third option (interpretable models) and humans' agential capacities (§7). So the standards differ for a reason; that's not a double standard.

output/student-arguments/S2-humans-too.md
S3 — "A human in the loop fixes the problem." automation bias · §7.3

Mere inclusion of a human isn't sufficient (§7.3). If the human defers without genuine deliberation, the juror-model wrong reappears, and automation bias makes that likely. The distinction is consulting vs. deferring.

The rules it follows from CLAUDE.md

A project-scoped CLAUDE.md loads automatically and binds everything Claude does in the project, keeping its output aligned to the paper:

  • Claude is never the decision-maker about a person — it augments the instructor and generates material.
  • Don't collapse transparency into due consideration — the paper's central move is that the second is broader.
  • The thesis is not absolute — "often," "prima facie," "overridable," context-sensitive. Never "never use black boxes."
  • Interpretable models are safer, not a panacea (§6).
  • Black box ≠ any AI — the target is high-flexibility, high-dimensional, low-rule-transparency systems (§4).
  • Cite by section; give a page number only if verified against the PDF.

Appendix — background & build

The paper, in brief

Grant, D. G., Behrends, J., & Basl, J. (2025). "What we owe to decision-subjects: beyond transparency and explanation in automated decision-making." Philosophical Studies 182, 55–85.

It defends the Explainability Thesis: in many contexts, decision-makers are morally obligated not to base decisions about people on the outputs of black-box AI. Its novelty is the grounding — not duties of transparency ("you must be able to explain it"), which only reach special cases, but the duty to show due consideration: adopting procedures appropriately responsive to the moral claims subjects have on how they're treated.

Family of dutyConstrainsHow black-box reliance fails
Evidential (§5)Fact-findingField accuracy / overfitting (the "Jared" screener); ignoring available evidence (the brain-tumor neurologist); inadmissible evidence (the Amazon "women's" tool, proxies)
Practical (§7)Decision-makingProhibited decision rules / Kantian injunction; agential consideration — some calls must be made by full-blown moral agents (the Juror Substitution case)

It also solves the Definition Problem (a "black box" = high flexibility + high dimensionality + limited rule transparency) and the Double Standard Problem (why hold machines to a higher bar than humans — partly it generalizes, but interpretable models are the safer third option and humans have agential capacities). Full digest: inputs/grant_behrends_basl.md.

How we built it

The shape follows the gallery's interview-coding project: a dense source text, the author named as the human in the room, a synthetic corpus engineered for specific moves, citation-tied skills, and a self-introducing CLAUDE.md. Each prompt is short — a goal plus one or two alignment constraints — then trusts Claude to produce the artifact.

1 · Brief Claude on the source and name the human in the room

Read this PDF and keep it in context: the Grant, Behrends & Basl paper on what we owe to decision-subjects. Jeff Behrends is a participant in our workshop, so I want to build a teaching project grounded in his paper. Help me think of ways a philosopher could use Claude Code to teach the ethics of automated decision-making — aligned with his framework, not a generic AI-ethics take.

A single dense source plus a named human whose view must be respected produces far more aligned output than "help me teach AI ethics."

2 · Capture the thinking in a document

Put the brainstorm in a claude-thoughts doc, with a skills catalog and a section on what to avoid given that this is Jeff's own paper.

Produced claude-thoughts.md: teaching use cases, a six-skill catalog tied to sections, and a "what to avoid in front of Jeff" section.

3 · Find the constraint the source itself dictates

The paper is about the limits of relying on AI for consequential decisions; the workshop is about relying on an AI tool. So the hard constraint wrote itself — Claude makes teaching material, it never adjudicates — and went to the top of CLAUDE.md. Let the source text tell you what the tool must not do.

4 · Build a synthetic teaching corpus

Generate the cases and sample student arguments we'll teach against.

The four cases and three student arguments above — each engineered to surface one move, including a decoy designed to pass and student arguments designed to be wrong in diagnosable ways.

5 · Turn the brainstorm into a parallelizable plan

Put the three demo skills into a PLAN.md so different Claudes can build them in parallel.

PLAN.md: shared context and hard constraints up top, then three self-contained build tasks, each with behavior, what-good-looks-like, what-to-avoid, build location, and validation against the corpus.

6 · Build the skills in parallel, then audit live in the session

We opened fresh Claude Code sessions, ran the three tasks, then audited each return against the corpus and verified every citation against the paper — the characteristic failure mode here is a plausible but slightly-off citation. All three are now built (SKILL.md + validated examples/ under .claude/skills/), with their demo runs promoted to output/ (the discussion plan to output/discussion-plans/, the audits to output/objection-audits/). The samples in What they produce are those actual runs.

7 · Ship the quiz the same way

Also generate a short-answer quiz students could do on paper at the end of class to check whether they grasped the core argument — then turn it into a reusable /quiz skill.

We built /quiz end-to-end too — a SKILL.md plus a worked example (the end-of-class quiz with its instructor key, promoted to output/end-of-class-quiz.md). Its hard constraint follows the project's line: Claude writes the instrument and the key, but the instructor owns grading and final marks.

Why this is a good Day-3 anchor

Day 3's ethics block is where the values conversation is meant to land — on the work participants just did. These three skills are narrow, repeatable, and single-purpose, and the paper they implement is about when a narrow automated system should and should not be trusted with a decision. So the ethics conversation and the skills lesson become the same conversation — and because the project keeps Claude in the augmentation role the paper prescribes, the demo shows the thesis instead of just citing it.
