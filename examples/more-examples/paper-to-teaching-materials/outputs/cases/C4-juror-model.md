# C4 — Personalized "juror model"

**Type:** thought experiment (drawn from the paper's §7.2 Juror Substitution case)
**Engineered to surface:** duties of agential consideration (§7.2–7.3) — the wrong
that survives even perfect accuracy and full transparency.

## The scenario

Computer scientists announce a method to build a **customized model of any individual
eligible for jury duty.** Trained on personalized data, a juror model predicts, with
near-perfect accuracy, how that person would vote in any given criminal case — by
implementing the very inference and decision rules the person would use. A court
adopts the **Juror Substitution Policy**: jurors are summoned and vetted as usual, but
once selected they may **go home**, and their model adjudicates in their place. The
models even make their "deliberations" available, satisfying any duty of transparency
one likes.

## The institution's argument for adopting it

"The verdict is identical to what the human jurors would have reached, the reasoning
is disclosed, and we save everyone's time. What is the objection?"

## What the scenario deliberately includes

- **Same inference and decision rules** as the human jurors. (Rules out an objection
  based on the *content* of the rules — §5, §7.1 don't reach it.)
- **Full transparency** of the model's deliberations. (Rules out a duties-of-
  transparency objection — §2.)
- **Perfect accuracy.** (Rules out the §5 evidential objections.)

## Notes for the instructor (not student-facing)

C4 is the case that isolates **agential consideration**. By construction every other
objection is closed off — content of the rules, transparency, accuracy. What remains
is the paper's §7.2–7.3 claim: some decisions (criminal punishment, lethal force)
must be made by **full-blown moral agents who take responsibility** and thereby show
the decision-subject respect as a fellow member of the moral and political community.
A model cannot do this; there is no one who *owns* the verdict. This is also the right
place to press the §7.3 point about human-in-the-loop: a human who merely rubber-stamps
the model's verdict reproduces the juror-model wrong (see [S3](../student-arguments/S3-human-in-the-loop.md)).
