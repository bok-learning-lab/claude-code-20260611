# Audit — "A human in the loop fixes the problem" (S3)

*Produced by `/objection-audit --argument S3`. Source:
[S3](../student-arguments/S3-human-in-the-loop.md).*

## Steelman

S3 identifies the right pressure point. The paper agrees that **agential consideration**
can be satisfied by a human who consults a system: a judge who genuinely examines a
defendant's circumstances is not rendered incapable of moral agency just because she also
reads a recommendation (§7.3). So "keep a human responsible for the call" is exactly the
right *shape* of solution — it is not naive, and a design that removed the human entirely
would be worse.

## Where it falls short

The argument slides from "a human is in the loop" to "agential consideration is satisfied,"
and the paper blocks that slide (§7.3). **Mere inclusion is not sufficient.** If the human
defers to the recommendation without genuine deliberation, "there is no meaningful
difference between a decision structure that includes the human and one that does not" — and
the juror-model wrong of [C4](../cases/C4-juror-model.md) reappears with an extra step. The
empirical literature on **automation bias** suggests this deference is common, and the risk
is *heightened* when the system is a black box the human can't interrogate. So the live
distinction is **consulting vs. deferring**, and S3 hasn't yet said which one its
human-in-the-loop is doing.

## Back to you

What institutional design would keep your human a genuine agent rather than a rubber stamp —
and how would you *tell the difference* from the outside? If your answer is "they have the
authority to override," ask what would make overriding real rather than nominal. That is the
question §7.3 leaves for the designer.
