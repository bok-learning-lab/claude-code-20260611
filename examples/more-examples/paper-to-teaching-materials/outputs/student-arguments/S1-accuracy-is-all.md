# S1 — "If it's more accurate, using it is what we owe people."

**Submitted position (student-facing):**

> We owe decision-subjects accurate decisions. A pretrial tool that predicts risk more
> accurately than a judge is therefore the *more* respectful choice, not the less. To
> reject a more accurate system because it is a "black box" is to prefer our own
> comfort over getting the answer right for the people affected. Accuracy is the whole
> of what due consideration requires in fact-finding.

**The flaw the paper names (instructor note):** S1 collapses **evidential
consideration** into **overall accuracy**. The paper concedes accuracy is relevant
(§5.1) but shows it is not sufficient: a system more accurate *on average* can still
(a) degrade in the field, (b) **ignore readily available evidence** that would benefit
this subject (§5.2, the brain-tumor case in [C1](../cases/C1-recidivism.md)), and (c)
rely on **morally inadmissible evidence** (§5.3). The substantive/procedural
distinction is the lever: average accuracy speaks to aggregate outcomes, but Sacco can
be wronged comparatively and noncomparatively even by an accurate system. A good audit
of S1 grants what is right (accuracy matters) before showing what it misses.
