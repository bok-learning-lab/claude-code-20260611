# S2 — "Humans are black boxes too, so this is a double standard."

**Submitted position (student-facing):**

> Psychology shows people have little reliable access to why they decide as they do.
> Judges and hiring managers are black boxes in exactly the sense the AI critics worry
> about. So if we let humans make these calls, consistency demands we let the machines
> do it too. Singling out AI is an objectionable double standard.

**The flaw the paper names (instructor note):** This is the **Double Standard Problem**
stated as an objection (§6). The paper's reply has two parts. First, it **concedes the
generalization in part** — there often *are* reasons of evidential consideration not to
rely on human fact-finders either. But that does not neutralize the thesis, because
there is a **third option**: **interpretable models** (§6), which are less prone to
overfitting, ignoring evidence, and exploiting inadmissible features, and whose rules
can be inspected. Second, humans (unlike both black boxes and interpretable models)
have **agential capacities** (§7), so in cases where agential consideration is owed the
asymmetry is principled, not arbitrary. So the standards differ for a reason; that is
not a double standard. A good audit of S2 resists the temptation to deny the premise
and instead deploys the third option.
