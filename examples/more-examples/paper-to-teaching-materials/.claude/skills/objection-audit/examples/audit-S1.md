# Audit — "If it's more accurate, using it is what we owe people" (S1)

*Produced by `/objection-audit --argument S1`. Source:
[S1](../student-arguments/S1-accuracy-is-all.md).*

## Steelman

The argument is right about something real, and the paper says so: accuracy **does** bear
on due consideration (§5.1). A court that knowingly estimates risk with a method it knows
to be inaccurate wrongs the defendant it then detains — that is the lesson of the
Sacco/Vanzetti–TEALEAVES case. So preferring the more accurate tool is not a confusion or a
mere preference; it tracks a genuine duty of evidential consideration. Any reply that
dismisses accuracy as irrelevant would be weaker than S1, not stronger.

## Where it falls short

S1 treats accuracy as the **whole** of evidential consideration. The paper shows two ways a
system that is more accurate *on average* still fails:

- It can **ignore readily available evidence** that bears on a particular subject (§5.2 —
  the brain-tumor defendant), a procedural failure to that person.
- It can rely on **morally inadmissible evidence** it was never meant to use (§5.3 —
  redundant encoding, proxies).

Both are visible only once you draw the **substantive/procedural** distinction: an aggregate
accuracy gain does not license a procedure that is unresponsive to decisive individual
evidence. So "more accurate, therefore what we owe them" skips the step where due
consideration is owed to the *subject*, not discharged by *population* statistics.

## Back to you

Try to restate the claim so it keeps what's right — accuracy matters — without implying
that a procedure fair on average can never wrong an individual. If you can't, what extra
premise would you need, and is it one the paper has already blocked in §5.2–5.3? That
reformulation is most of the paper's §5.
