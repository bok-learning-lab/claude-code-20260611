# Audit — "Humans are black boxes too, so this is a double standard" (S2)

*Produced by `/objection-audit --argument S2`. Source:
[S2](../student-arguments/S2-humans-too.md).*

## Steelman

This is a serious objection, and the paper takes it seriously as the **Double Standard
Problem** (§6). The empirical premise is well supported: introspection is unreliable, and
human decision-makers demonstrably import social-group bias they can neither report nor
fully control. If opacity and the risk of prohibited inference rules are what condemn
black-box systems, those features really are present in human experts too. A defender of
the thesis who simply *denied* this would be ignoring good evidence — so S2 is right that
consistency is on the table.

## Where it falls short

S2 assumes the only options are "trust humans" and "trust the machine," so that any reason
against the machine either sinks the thesis or convicts humans equally. The paper grants
the generalization **in part** — and then escapes the dilemma with a **third option:
interpretable models** (§6). These are less likely to implement a prohibited inference
rule, less prone to overfitting and data-quality failure, and — crucially — their rules can
be **inspected**, so when they do err it is detectable. Where an interpretable model is
available and roughly as accurate, it dominates both the black box and unaided human
judgment on evidential consideration. (And in the practical domain, humans have **agential
capacities** the machine lacks, §7, so where agential consideration is owed the asymmetry is
principled, not arbitrary.) So the standards differ for a reason; that is not a *double*
standard.

## Back to you

Suppose an interpretable model exists that matches the black box on accuracy. On your view,
is there still anything to the "double standard" charge — and if so, between which two
options, exactly? Naming the pair you think are being treated inconsistently is the fastest
way to test whether the objection survives §6.
