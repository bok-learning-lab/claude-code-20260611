# Recentering Academics — Syllabus Redesign: GENED 1074 The Ancient Greek Hero

- **Course:** GENED 1074, The Ancient Greek Hero: Mythology and Facing Death (Fall 2025). Course Head: Professor Gregory Nagy.
- **Source syllabus:** [inputs/syllabus/GenEd1074-Fall25-Syllabus.pdf](../inputs/syllabus/GenEd1074-Fall25-Syllabus.pdf).
- **Date:** 2026-06-04

GENED 1074's creative exercises (an epinikion, a vase-painting sketch, a lament) are a genuine, hard-to-counterfeit form of personal engagement. With respect to recentering academics, the course already does two things the Harvard report asks for: implements a defined full A–F percentage scale (criterion-referenced, not curved) and a cumulative, closed-book, in-person final. There is a gap in grade *differentiation*, and it is structural rather than a matter of policy language: by weight, roughly 60% of the grade sits on low-ceiling effort and reading-check components, and another 20% (the take-home midterm essay) is exposed to outside help and AI despite a blanket ban — so the course's ability to produce a broad distribution and identify A-level work of extraordinary distinction rests almost entirely on a **single 20% closed-book final.** A secondary gap is that a blanket prohibition on AI "at all stages" is standing in for AI-resilient *design*: on take-home creative and essay work the ban is unenforceable, and prohibition gives a false sense of security rather than making the work reliable evidence of the student's own thinking.

## 2. Recommended revisions

- **Add a device policy.**
  - *Currently:* none.
  - *Revise to:* "Laptops, tablets, and phones are not to be used during lecture or section except when an instructor directs their use for an activity." In a large lecture a device policy is a low-cost engagement gain ([course policies](../inputs/bok-advice/course-policies.md)).
- **State a participation standard distinct from attendance.**
  - *Currently:* the 15% "attendance and participation" section describes only absence limits.
  - *Revise to:* add a one-line descriptor of what counts as participation in section (e.g. preparation, contribution to discussion of the day's reading) so the 15% measures engagement on a stated standard, not just presence ([course policies](../inputs/bok-advice/course-policies.md)).
- **Relocate differentiation onto verifiable work.** The hardest, most differentiating work (the essay; interpretive analysis) currently cannot be fully verified as the student's own work. Move some of the differentiating weight to supervised settings rather than adding more hard take-home work ([full-range-task-design](../inputs/research/full-range-task-design.md); [discriminating-at-the-top](../inputs/research/discriminating-at-the-top.md)).
  - *Currently:* midterm essay 20%, take-home, self-designed prompt.
  - *Revise to:* keep the take-home essay but anchor the grade in a verifiable component built on it — e.g. an **in-section "defense" or short oral exam** on the student's own essay (a TF asks two questions about its argument and evidence), or an **in-class timed writing** that extends the essay's claim to a new passage. The student still does the reading and thinking at home; the grade rests on demonstrated understanding that AI cannot supply on their behalf. This also fits the course's collaborative ethos better than a prohibition does.
- **Rebalance weight away from low-ceiling components.**
  - *Currently:* creative exercises 35%, quizzes 10%, attendance 15% — ~60% on effort/low-ceiling work.
  - *Revise to:* keep the creative exercises but treat them as the **engagement** component and reduce their share of the *differentiating* grade, reallocating weight to the verifiable, differentiating work above (defended essay + final). Accept the reading quizzes as a reading-completion floor, not a differentiator.
- **Make the final do more of the differentiating, and confirm it resolves the top.**
  - *Currently:* "cumulative, closed-book, in-person … cover ALL readings."
  - *Revise to:* ensure the final reserves a portion of marks for **interpretive synthesis** items (comparing heroes across texts, applying a concept to an unseen passage) that only the strongest answers clear, not only identification/recall — and check after grading what fraction reach full marks; past ~15% at the maximum, the exam is not resolving your best students ([discriminating-at-the-top](../inputs/research/discriminating-at-the-top.md)). Given its verifiability, a modest increase in the final's weight is the most defensible way to restore range.
- **Publish standards and calibrate TF grading.**
  - *Currently:* TFs grade subjective creative and essay work; unclear in syllabus if there are rubrics, exemplars, or grade norming.
  - *Revise to:* write an analytic rubric for the essay and for the creative exercises whose criteria describe **proficiency** while leaving the top band **open** for genuine insight, depth of engagement, and originality — not a completion checklist (a creative exercise scored as "did the required elements" rewards compliance over the personal intellectual engagement the course is built on; [rubrics-differentiation](../inputs/research/rubrics-differentiation.md); the Bok [rubrics](../inputs/bok-advice/rubrics.md) and [non-traditional assignments](../inputs/bok-advice/assessing-non-traditional-assignments.md) pages both warn against this). Distribute annotated A/B exemplars, and hold a **calibration session** where TFs grade shared samples before final grades.

## 3. A tailored generative-AI policy

The current policy is strict but rests entirely on prohibition. Suggested syllabus language:

> **Generative AI in GENED 1074.** 
> - **You may not use generative-AI tools to produce any part of your creative exercises, essay, or exam answers,** including outlining, drafting, or "polishing." This work must be your own thinking in your own words. Violations are academic misconduct.
>
> If we have reason to believe submitted work was AI-generated, we may ask you to discuss it with us and, if warranted, refer the matter to the Honor Council.
