# Recentering Academics — Syllabus Redesign: CS 20 Discrete Mathematics for Computer Science

- **Course:** CS 20 / CSCI E-20, Discrete Mathematics for Computer Science. Course Head: Rebecca Nesson.
- **Source syllabus:** [inputs/syllabus/cs20-syllabus.md](../inputs/syllabus/cs20-syllabus.md).
- **Date:** 2026-06-10

CS 20 is taught in a flipped format in which reading and mini-lectures happen before class and class time "is spent on active problem-solving," with teaching fellows present to help, an in-class assignment on most days, and an explicit no-device policy in the classroom, so the engaged-classroom pillar is built into the structure rather than left to exhortation. With respect to grade differentiation, the course also already does much of what the Harvard report asks: it grades to criterion ("not graded on a curve. Grades are assigned based on performance and are not relative"), and it places roughly 48% of the grade (three unit quizzes at 8% plus a 24% final) on closed-book, in-person work that is both verifiable and high-ceiling. The remaining gap is one of where differentiation can come from: the 28% participation/in-class component is scored on a coarse "Extraordinary/Good/Acceptable/Missing" scale with the lowest four dropped, so it tends to function as engagement rather than differentiation, and the 24% problem sets are take-home with collaboration and AI explicitly allowed, so they serve as formative practice rather than as differentiators. That leaves the quizzes and final carrying the differentiating weight, which holds up only if those assessments reserve marks for the items that only the strongest students clear.

## 2. Recommended revisions

- **Confirm the quizzes and final reserve marks for items only the strongest clear.**
  - *Currently:* quizzes are "closed-book and administered during the class period" (60 minutes); the final is "a standard 3-hour exam."
  - *Revise to:* ensure a portion of marks on each quiz and on the final goes to harder proof, synthesis, or transfer items beyond the practiced templates, and after grading check what fraction of students reach full marks; past roughly 15% at the maximum the instrument is not resolving your strongest students ([discriminating-at-the-top](../inputs/research/discriminating-at-the-top.md)).
- **Give the in-class component a stated quality standard, or treat it frankly as engagement.**
  - *Currently:* in-class assignments are "graded on an Extraordinary/Good/Acceptable/Missing scale. Your lowest 4 scores will be dropped."
  - *Revise to:* state what distinguishes "Extraordinary" from "Good" and "Acceptable" so the 28% measures something beyond completion, or accept the component as an engagement floor and keep the differentiating weight on the quizzes and final ([course policies](../inputs/bok-advice/course-policies.md); [rubrics](../inputs/bok-advice/rubrics.md)).
- **Publish what each grade represents and calibrate proof grading across TFs.**
  - *Currently:* "not graded on a curve… Grades are assigned based on performance," with the calibration of final grades described in prose.
  - *Revise to:* write an analytic rubric for proofs whose criteria describe proficiency while leaving the top band open for genuine insight and elegance rather than a completion checklist, distribute annotated exemplars, and hold a TF calibration session on shared proofs before grades are set ([rubrics](../inputs/bok-advice/rubrics.md); [rubrics-differentiation](../inputs/research/rubrics-differentiation.md); Jonsson & Svingby 2007).
- **State an attendance expectation alongside the in-class mechanism.**
  - *Currently:* attendance is encouraged and rewarded indirectly through graded in-class assignments, but the syllabus does not state an attendance standard.
  - *Revise to:* add a one-line attendance expectation so the link between presence, the in-class assignments, and the participation grade is explicit to students ([course policies](../inputs/bok-advice/course-policies.md)).

## 3. A tailored generative-AI policy

The current policy is already AI-resilient by design and need only be stated crisply against the course's components. Suggested syllabus language:

> **Generative AI in CS 20.**
> - On problem sets and practice, you may use AI and other aids to help you learn and check your understanding. Whatever help you use, you must reach the point where you can produce and explain the work yourself; we do not award full credit to answers that do not demonstrate your own mastery of the content and techniques taught, regardless of correctness.
> - Unit quizzes and the final exam are closed-book and completed in class without AI or other aids; they are where your individual mastery is assessed.
>
> If we have reason to believe submitted work was authored by AI, we may ask you to meet with us to discuss it and, if warranted, refer the matter to the Honor Council.
