# Capstone Assignments

*Source: Bok Center for Teaching and Learning, <https://bokcenter.harvard.edu/capstone-assignments> (retrieved 2026-06-04).*

## Overview

A capstone assignment represents a culminating opportunity to assess what students can accomplish at course conclusion. The Derek Bok Center recommends considering authentic expertise first—"what does it look like when a CEO does that thing well?"—before adapting that mastery for classroom simulation.

Capstone assignments function as summative assessments, requiring students to synthesize substantial content and engage creatively with course material. Instructors should carefully match assessment verbs to learning sophistication levels. Lower-order tasks like "list" or "recognize" demand recall, while higher-order tasks such as "analyze," "apply," "evaluate," and "create" require students to "use [instruction] to perform a new action, with varying degrees of self-awareness."

## Key Design Principle

Assignment format matters significantly. Traditional exams work well for measuring content retention and reading comprehension. Term papers suit research synthesis goals. However, assignments connecting academic learning to real-world application—such as poster presentations, gallery installations, or community engagement projects—often prove more effective for demonstrating applied mastery.

## Common Capstone Genres

The center identifies several established formats:

- Response Papers
- Problem Sets
- Final Exams
- Podcasts
- Games
- Portfolios

These alternatives to traditional assessments can incorporate feedback from multiple sources including peers and audiences, extending learning beyond instructor evaluation alone.
