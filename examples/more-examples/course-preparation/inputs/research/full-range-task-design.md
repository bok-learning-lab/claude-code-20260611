# Designing tasks for the full range: low floor, high ceiling

How do you build an assignment that the strongest students can use to show distinction
while the modal and below-average student can still enter and make real progress? This is
[open question Q1](../director-advice/grading-for-the-full-range.md) in the director's
advice. It complements the synthesis in [overview.md](overview.md), which establishes that
a full range is only possible if the assessment can *elicit* a range; this file is about
the task-design mechanisms that do — and do not — produce that range.

The honest headline: the "low-floor, high-ceiling" idea is intuitive and widely repeated,
but the empirical base is thinner and more cautionary than its popularity suggests. The
best-evidenced mechanisms are (a) productive failure — hard problems *before* instruction —
for differentiating deep understanding, and (b) optional extension work for raising the
ceiling, with the strong caveat that optional work tends to be taken up by the students who
are already strong. Several mechanisms commonly cited in this space (desirable
difficulties; specifications grading's effect on the distribution) could not be confirmed
in this review and are flagged as gaps, not endorsements.

A note on method: the findings below were produced by a fan-out web search with three-vote
adversarial verification. Claims marked *confirmed* survived that bar; claims marked
*surfaced, not confirmed* were found in credible sources but did not clear independent
re-verification in this pass (often because verifier agents failed to return a vote, not
because a source contradicted them). Treat the latter as leads to check, not as findings.

---

## 1. "Low-threshold, high-ceiling" (LTHC) task design — a clear intent, weak direct evidence

**The concept (confirmed).** The canonical definition comes from NRICH (the University of
Cambridge mathematics-education project): a low threshold means "everyone has the
opportunity to be successful," and a high ceiling means "everyone has the opportunity to be
challenged." The design intent is explicitly to serve the full ability range *at once* —
"everyone has the right to succeed and everyone has the right to struggle." This is a
definitional, design-intent source, not evidence of effectiveness, and NRICH's own caution
matters: per John Mason, "it is not the task itself that is rich, but whether the task is
used richly." Richness lives in implementation, not in the task on the page.

**The evidence (weak, mixed).** Direct empirical evidence that LTHC/inquiry tasks raise
engagement across the ability range is thin. The strongest available implementation study
(Ouellet 2017, an MSc case study of a single Grade-10 mathematics class, no control group)
found that students' feelings about the inquiry *content itself* were predominantly
negative even though overall sentiment was roughly even — the favorite component was peer
mentoring, not the inquiry work. The author flags severe limits (convenience sample of
~10–21 students, violated statistical independence, qualitative coding the author calls
"one researcher's estimate"). This is high-school mathematics, not selective-university
work, which further limits transfer.

**What to take from it.** LTHC is a useful *design heuristic* — write tasks with an
accessible entry and no fixed ceiling — but it is not a proven motivational lever. Do not
present it to faculty as evidence-backed; present it as a design stance whose payoff
depends entirely on how the task is facilitated and how the ceiling is actually built.

## 2. Optional extension / challenge work — raises the ceiling, but rewards the already-strong

**It produces real learning, modestly (confirmed, medium).** Equiza-Goñi &
Rodríguez-Tejedo (2025, *REIRE*) found that optional extra-credit work had a statistically
significant positive effect on final-exam performance (measured *excluding* the
extra-credit points themselves) across three undergraduate course samples, robust to
controlling for midterm performance. The magnitude is modest and context-dependent (from
~0.27 to ~0.05 exam points per point of project grade), and the design is observational,
so read this as "optional challenge work is associated with genuine learning gains," not a
clean causal effect.

**The central caution: ability-based self-selection (confirmed, high).** The principal
documented risk is that optional/extension work is taken up disproportionately by students
who are already earning higher grades; lower-performing students tend not to take it up
(Harrison, Meister & LeFevre 2011, N=508; independently replicated in a 2024 introductory
physics study where 67% of high-grade students completed optional work versus 40% of
low-grade students). The implication for grade-distribution recovery is sharp and
double-edged: an opt-in ceiling can *widen* the distribution — which is what you want for
differentiation — but it does so by rewarding the already-strong rather than by stretching
the modal student. If the goal is also to engage the middle and bottom, a purely optional
high ceiling will not do it.

**Design consequence.** If you want a high ceiling that reaches beyond the top quartile,
the challenge has to live *inside a required task*, or the optional work must be scaffolded
and actively recruited, rather than offered as pure opt-in extra credit. (Whether such
designs successfully draw in modal students is itself an open question — see Gaps.)

## 3. Productive failure — hard tasks before instruction differentiate depth, not rote skill

**Best-evidenced mechanism here (confirmed, high).** Sinha & Kapur's (2021, *Review of
Educational Research*) meta-analysis of 53 studies / 166 comparisons found a moderate
benefit of problem-solving *before* instruction (PS-I) over instruction-first on
**conceptual knowledge and transfer** (Hedges' g = 0.36, 95% CI [0.20, 0.51]) but **no
benefit on procedural knowledge** (g = −0.03). The signal that matters for the full range:
having students attempt a genuinely hard problem before being taught the method builds the
kind of deep, transferable understanding that distinguishes the strongest work — and it
does so on exactly the dimension (conceptual/transfer) that rote, completion-style tasks
cannot discriminate.

**What it does *not* establish.** A proposed claim that the PS-I benefit grows with learner
level (which would directly support its use to differentiate ability) did *not* survive
verification. So PS-I is supported as a way to differentiate *conceptual depth from
procedural recall*, not yet as a way to spread *ability levels* within a cohort. Use it to
build assessments that have something deep to discriminate, not as a proven sorter.

## 4. Specifications / mastery grading — relevant, but its distributional effect is unconfirmed here

Specifications grading and mastery-based bundling (Nilson) structure higher tiers of
achievement by requiring students to clear bundles of pass/fail "specs" to earn higher
grades. This is directly relevant to the full-range question, but every specific claim this
review surfaced about its *effect on the distribution* — that it shifts toward more A's,
that it produces polarized/bimodal distributions, that it reduces score variance — failed
to clear verification (largely verifier abstentions, not refutations). That is an
unsatisfying result for a resource whose whole purpose is the distribution. **Treat the
distributional effect of specs grading as an open, high-priority gap** (see below), not as
either a recommendation or a warning. The mechanism is worth understanding; its effect on
crowding at the top is not yet established in this evidence base.

## What this means for assignment design

Tying the confirmed findings to the director's principles:

- **Build the ceiling into required work, not just optional add-ons.** Optional challenge
  reliably reaches the already-strong; it does not reach the middle. To distinguish levels
  of mastery across the whole cohort ([Principle 2](../director-advice/grading-for-the-full-range.md)),
  at least some required task must itself admit excellence — an open-ended part, a transfer
  problem, a question with no ceiling — rather than relying on opt-in extras.
- **Use problem-solving-before-instruction to create something worth grading.** PS-I builds
  conceptual depth and transfer, which are the dimensions on which the strongest work
  separates from the merely competent. A test made only of taught procedures has little
  depth to discriminate.
- **Don't oversell low-floor/high-ceiling as motivation.** It is a sound design stance but
  not a proven engagement lever; the payoff is in facilitation and in how the ceiling is
  constructed.
- **Watch optional work's effect on equity and spread.** Opt-in ceilings widen the
  distribution by rewarding students who were already ahead. That can serve differentiation
  and undercut access at the same time — be deliberate about which you are buying.

## Cautions and gaps

- **Transfer is assumed, not shown.** Almost all of this evidence is from K–12 mathematics
  or non-CS undergraduate courses. Application to selective-university CS/mathematics is an
  extrapolation.
- **High-priority gap — specifications/standards-based grading and the distribution.** Its
  effect on A-rates, bimodality, and score variance is central to this project and was not
  confirmable here. Re-research before using it either way.
- **Gap — desirable difficulties (Bjork) and aptitude-by-difficulty interactions.** Claims
  that current performance is an unreliable index of learning, that desirable difficulties
  require prerequisite knowledge or else harm weaker students, and that harder conditions
  help high-working-memory but hurt low-working-memory learners all surfaced but did *not*
  clear verification in this pass. These are well-known in the literature and worth a
  dedicated follow-up; they are not citable from this review.
- **Gap — two-part (core + extension) exams and tiered assignments specifically.** The
  confirmed evidence covers optional extra-credit uptake and learning gains, not the
  core-plus-extension exam structure named in Q1. The structure is plausible; direct
  evidence was not surfaced.
