# Rubrics that differentiate, not rubrics that bind

How can a rubric (or an alternative to it) sharpen judgment about quality and differentiate
student work — especially at the top — rather than functioning as an additive checklist
that rewards completeness over excellence and binds instructors and TFs into high grades
whenever every box is checked? This is
[open question Q3](../director-advice/grading-for-the-full-range.md) in the director's
advice, and it is the failure mode of the advice already in [overview.md](overview.md),
which recommends analytic, topic-specific rubrics. This file engages that failure mode
directly and what to do about it.

The honest headline: there is a substantial, peer-reviewed literature — led by Royce
Sadler — arguing that breaking holistic judgment into preset criteria has a *weaker*
theoretical foundation than is usually assumed, and that additive criterion scoring
mathematically compresses grades toward the middle while binding the top grade to
"outstanding on every box." The fix is not to abandon rubrics (which costs reliability,
audit trail, and appeal-defensibility) but to stop treating the rubric as an additive
contract: leave the top band open, recognize that different criteria discriminate at
different points of the range, and re-anchor grading to a holistic standard of excellence
maintained by calibration and exemplars.

A note on method and confidence: this synthesis was reconstructed from a fan-out web search
whose claim-extraction and three-vote adversarial verification largely completed (the run
was stopped during final write-up, not during research or verification). Of the verified
claims here, the large majority survived at high confidence; the quotes below are verbatim
from the cited sources. Treat this file's confidence as comparable to its companions, not
lower.

## 1. Sadler's indeterminacy critique — why preset criteria distort, and bind, the top grade

This is the theoretical core (Sadler 2009, *Indeterminacy in the use of preset criteria for
assessment and grading*; with his related work on holistic assessment and on calibration).

- **The headline finding.** "Breaking down holistic judgments into more manageable parts is
  seen as a way to increase openness for students and achieve more objectivity in grading.
  However, such approaches do not adequately represent the full complexity of
  multi-criterion qualitative judgments, and can lead to distorted grading decisions."
  Sadler identifies six anomalies in how assessors apply preset criteria and concludes that
  "explicit grading models do not have as strong a theoretical foundation as is commonly
  supposed, and that holistic appraisal merits further investigation."

- **Manifest words vs. latent criteria.** "The manifest expressions of assessment criteria
  (i.e., the words) cannot convey the full complexity of the latent criteria." A workable
  rubric (typically 6–12 criteria) is only a *sample* drawn from a much larger pool (Sadler
  reports a collection of 50+ for written work), and some evaluative qualities are tacit and
  "impossible to articulate, even in principle." "Fitting a work to the template so that the
  completed rubric is made to 'account for' the quality of the work as a whole is
  reductionist in principle … with validity lowered as a result."

- **The additive compression mechanism — directly the "binds graders" problem.** Additive,
  fully compensatory scoring "produces more middle-level aggregates, because many more
  combinations of sub-scores are possible there, whereas the highest (and lowest) aggregates
  require consistently high (or low) levels on all the components. (The Central Limit Theorem
  in statistics is essentially an expression of the same principle.)" The consequence Sadler
  draws is exactly the director's concern: "A work judged as 'brilliant' overall may not rate
  as outstanding on each criterion. This would be necessary, logically and arithmetically,
  for the work to be assigned the top grade. Conversely, another work that comes out well on
  each criterion may be judged as only mediocre overall." An all-boxes-checked rubric can
  thus bind the grade *upward* for competent-but-unexceptional work and *downward* for genuine
  brilliance.

- **Emergent criteria and the "contract" trap.** A criterion crucial to the judgment can
  emerge only during appraisal, yet "when the appraisal protocols are followed strictly, an
  'emergent' criterion cannot be admitted … To do so would breach the implicit contract with
  students, which is that only the preset criteria will be used in deciding the grade." The
  push toward transparency-via-preset-criteria is itself the source of the indeterminacy.

- **Conjunctive vs. disjunctive.** "Rubrics provide an example of a conjunctive rule that
  specifies a fixed set of criteria … with little or no flexibility," whereas "a particular
  level of quality can be easier to recognise holistically … than to define or deduce"
  through fixed criteria. Proficient assessors "run dual agendas simultaneously" (overall
  quality plus particular characteristics) and "notice aspects … worth noticing"; "less
  proficient assessors lean towards following rules."

## 2. The rubric-flattens-quality critique (Maja Wilson)

A second, independent line of critique comes from writing assessment. Wilson's *Rethinking
Rubrics in Writing Assessment* (Heinemann, 2006; winner of NCTE's 2007 James N. Britton
Award — i.e., not a fringe position) argues that rubrics "replace professional decision
making with an inauthentic pigeonholing that stamps standardization onto a notably
nonstandard process." Her core illustration is the "whole is so much more than the sum of
the parts" case — a moving student poem whose strengths established rubrics "failed to
recognize." She notes "many contemporary and professional writers would score poorly on
[rubrics]" and asks "whether the reductive standardized categories of rubrics will produce
standardized writers."

**The honest caution Wilson herself supplies:** she offers no clean replacement. "The problem
with developing our own rubrics is that they will not change the reductive nature of the
rubric itself." Abandoning rubrics buys no easy, reliability-preserving substitute — a point
that matters for a department with TFs and appeals.

## 3. Analytic vs. holistic — the empirical tradeoff is real but smaller than assumed

The critiques above are theoretical; the empirical comparisons are a necessary counterweight,
and they cut both ways.

- **Analytic rubrics do buy *some* consistency.** In a retrospective cohort of 325 student
  research manuscripts, analytic rubrics produced higher inter-examiner agreement than
  holistic marking (Pearson r = 0.36 vs 0.24 — both low), roughly halved the mean
  examiner-to-examiner disagreement, and sent 2.75× fewer cases to a third adjudicator
  (6.8% vs 18.7%). If the worry is gross disagreement and adjudication load, analytic rubrics
  help.

- **But the reliability gain is minimal, and it fails exactly at the top.** When pre-service
  teachers graded the same essays both ways (Lopera-Oquendo, Lipnevich & Mañez 2024), the
  analytic approach "did reduce the range of scores, albeit slightly … The improvement,
  however, was minimal," and the score range "remained alarmingly high for both methods."
  Critically for the director's question: raters "deviate[d] more from the expert criteria for
  the strong essay" while agreeing more on the weak essay — i.e., **both rubric and holistic
  grading struggle most to agree on, and differentiate, the best work.** And even with a
  rubric, raters "seemed to define their idiosyncratic criteria … the correlation between the
  rubric components and analytic and holistic scores were similar" — overall impression drove
  scoring regardless. Between-rater variability was driven by rater characteristics (subject
  expertise improved differentiation; extraversion was associated with higher grades), *not*
  by which method was used. Method choice mattered less than rater calibration.

- **Additive scoring does not reconstruct holistic judgment, and criteria discriminate
  unevenly.** Modeling holistic marks from analytic criteria (Tomas et al. 2019) showed the
  relationship is non-additive: "the conversion to percentage weightings of criteria is
  limited in revealing the complex interactions of criteria." And "some criteria may be
  better at predicting a pass from a fail, but not be good at predicting a fail from
  excellent" — a single additive rubric does not differentiate uniformly across the range,
  least of all at the top. Staff's *intended* weightings diverged sharply from those implied
  by their own holistic marks (critical thinking expected at ~25–30%, empirically ~13–16%):
  intended rubric design and actual quality judgments come apart.

## 4. Single-point rubrics — a concrete design move to keep the ceiling open

A single-point rubric "breaks down the components of an assessment" like an analytic rubric
"but … only lists the criteria for proficiency," leaving the "exceeds"/"advanced" region
**undefined and open-ended**. The rationale maps exactly onto Q3: "Instructors do not need to
anticipate all the ways a learner might excel … There are no limits on the ways that a
learner might succeed … Students may surprise us if we leave quality open-ended." Because it
carries far less prescriptive text, students are "more likely to engage" with it; the cost is
that it "might require a lot of writing on the instructor's part" to describe where a given
student excelled or fell short. (Evidence grade: the single-point-rubric case rests on
practitioner and formative-assessment sources rather than controlled trials — adopt it as a
reasoned design pattern, not a proven reliability intervention.)

## 5. Comparative judgement — an alternative for ranking the best work, with real limits

Comparative judgement (CJ) removes the rubric entirely: judges make repeated pairwise
"which is better" decisions, which a model scales into a rank order. (See also the
structural treatment in [discriminating-at-the-top.md](discriminating-at-the-top.md).)

- **The case for it.** Removing the rubric is "thought [to free] judges to make holistic …
  and more valid … judgments" — useful for "nebulous" constructs that "rubrics attempt to
  capture the letter of … but risk losing the spirit." Across 16 CJ exercises (1998–2015),
  Scale Separation Reliability ran .73–.99, "higher than is typically obtained through marking
  using a scoring rubric, at least for essay questions" (Steedle & Ferrara 2016). Eliciting
  *relative* judgments "cancel[s] out differences in examiner severity and leniency." Validity
  rests on "the individual holistic nature of decision-making and the fact that the final rank
  order is based on a shared consensus … of judges," and judges' written justifications mostly
  "related to the competence description."

- **The cautions, which are substantial.** (1) *Reliability is partly an artifact.* Adaptive
  CJ's headline reliability is inflated by the adaptivity itself: in one real-data essay study
  SSR was 0.97 adaptive vs 0.82 all-play-all vs 0.70 non-adaptive — and the non-adaptive scale
  "correlated slightly higher with external variables," so high SSR does not mean better
  discrimination of quality. (2) *Holistic ≠ construct-valid.* "Adding more comparisons does
  not guarantee that the comparisons are appropriate"; if judges aren't using construct-relevant
  criteria the validity claim fails (Bartholomew, Strimel et al. 2022). (3) *It can reward
  surface over substance.* In a high-stakes legal-exam study, "surface-level features (clarity,
  organization, readability) were often rewarded over deeper legal reasoning"; judgments as fast
  as 18–24 seconds "cannot realistically [assess multi-page papers] thoroughly." (4)
  *Audit/appeals.* CJ "provides limited audit trails for explaining how criteria are applied,
  complicating grade justification and defense." That study's bottom line: "we do not recommend
  ACJ in its current form as a stand-alone grading method for high-stakes, text-intensive
  examinations" — but it may serve a **complementary calibration or moderation role.**

## 6. Calibration of the standard, and inducting students into it

Both literatures converge on the constructive move. In holistic models, "validity and
reliability are achieved through intersubjective calibration: educators working together to
interpret authentic student work, compare judgments, and articulate the reasoning behind
their decisions." Sadler's program (from moderation to calibration) is to develop "calibrated
academics who are able to make grading judgements consistent with those which similarly
calibrated colleagues would make, but without constant engagement in moderation," and to
"induct students into the art of making appraisals" so they become "connoisseurs" of quality.
This is the same practice [overview.md](overview.md) already endorses (rater training,
exemplars), aimed here specifically at the *standard of excellence*. Caveat (a 2023
retrospective application): Sadler's ideas are "extremely influential from a conceptual
standpoint, though they remain difficult to deploy in practice," with "few explicit accounts …
of Sadlerian theory in practice."

## What this means for designing and using rubrics

- **Don't make the top grade conjunctive.** Requiring "outstanding on every criterion" to earn
  the top grade is the mechanism that both compresses toward the middle and binds graders into
  awarding high marks to box-checking work. Reserve the top grade for holistically excellent
  work, and say so.
- **Leave the ceiling open.** Use a single-point rubric, or leave the "exceeds" band
  deliberately undefined, so excellence is recognized rather than pre-enumerated — and so a
  complete-but-ordinary submission does not automatically reach the top.
- **Weight criteria that bite at the top.** A criterion that separates pass from fail will not
  separate good from excellent. Identify and weight the dimensions on which only the strongest
  work distinguishes itself; don't assume one additive rubric differentiates uniformly.
- **Spend the effort on calibration, not finer rubric text.** Exemplars of genuine A-level work
  and graders reconciling on shared samples do more for top-end differentiation than additional
  rubric rows — and they are what keep a loosened rubric defensible across TFs and on appeal.
- **Use comparative judgement as a complement, not a replacement.** It is well suited to
  rank-ordering open-ended work and surfacing the strongest pieces (e.g., to moderate or
  calibrate the A/A- line), but not as a stand-alone high-stakes grader, given construct-validity,
  audit-trail, and appeals limits.
- **Be honest about the tradeoff.** Loosening a rubric costs reliability, audit trail, and
  appeal-defensibility; analytic rubrics genuinely reduce gross disagreement and adjudication
  load. The recommendation is not "drop rubrics" but "stop treating the rubric as an additive
  contract and re-anchor it to a calibrated standard of excellence."

## Cautions and gaps

- **Most evidence is from writing/essay and text-based assessment** (plus medical research
  manuscripts). Transfer to problem-based STEM grading (e.g., proofs, problem sets) is plausible
  but not directly evidenced here.
- **Single-point rubrics are under-tested.** The rationale is strong and on-point, but the
  supporting sources are practitioner/formative rather than controlled comparisons of grade
  distributions.
- **The Bok Center's own rubric guidance agrees in spirit.** Its
  [rubrics page](../bok-advice/rubrics.md) explicitly warns rubrics should not be "a checklist
  of minimum requirements or 'paint by numbers' approach" — this research supplies the mechanism
  behind that warning.
