# Music

The concentration in Music equips students with a critical and practical understanding of
music by exploring a wide variety of musical styles, genres, and traditions. Department of
Music courses offer a solid foundation in music theory, analysis, composition, and criticism,
and provide an opportunity to develop acute listening skills through various cultural and
historical contexts. The concentration in Music is centered on an academic focus that
supports the intellectual development of musicians while also offering creative programs in
music performance, composition, and improvisation.
A wide range of introductory and advanced courses are available in music theory,
composition, historical musicology, creative practice and ethnomusicology. These courses
reflect the specialties of our academic faculty; classical, opera, jazz, and contemporary music,
music and politics, early music and historically informed performance, musical theater, music
and media, improvisation, hip-hop, music in film, American and European modernism, music
and ecology, music and language, new music of the 21st century, and the musical traditions
of Africa, Latin America, and Asia.
Students are encouraged to participate in faculty-led ensembles including orchestra,
choruses, and jazz orchestra. Chamber music courses focus on small-ensemble repertoire
and performance practice through faculty-led coaching and recitals.
Under advisement from the Director of Undergraduate Studies (DUS), students identify their
own path of study through the concentration, creating a mix of courses that best reflect their
musical interests and aspirations. Students may enter the concentration from any course
offered by the Department of Music.
The heart of the concentration is MUSIC 97 Sophomore Tutorial, a semester-long course that
introduces students from all musical backgrounds to the study of music. This required course
examines select musical repertoires drawn from diverse cultures and historical periods and
introduces a wide range of methodologies. Due to its foundational role, it is recommended
that MUSIC 97 be fulfilled as early in the concentration as possible, ideally at the time of
concentration declaration and before the completion of a student’s sophomore year.
After completing MUSIC 97, students continue with electives that invite engagement with the
arts at a deeper level, drawing on courses from a variety of areas of focus. MUSIC 97
provides a shared point of departure for course work in our three primary course categories:
History, Culture, Society
Music Theory
Creating Music
To foster breadth and depth in musical study, the concentration in Music requires that the
student complete at least one upper-level (100+) course in each of the three categories listed
above.
The department welcomes double and joint concentrations from all participating departments.
Students who wish to pursue a joint concentration should consult with the Director of
Undergraduate Studies (DUS) prior to concentration declaration to explore how best to pursue
their course interests in music, to review the role of primary vs. allied concentration, to
consider a thesis topic inclusive of both concentrations, and to review thesis timelines and
guidelines. Joint concentrators are required to fulfill a reduced number of course

requirements, as outlined below, including the completion of a senior thesis based on a topic
that engages both concentrations.
MUSIC 91 Supervised Reading and Research is an opportunity for advanced students to
pursue an area of musical interest in great depth. Open to Music concentrators only, students
are required to submit a detailed proposal to the DUS describing their anticipated project in
detail, including an estimated timeline for benchmarks and pathway to completion. MUSIC 91
may be taken for concentration credit as an elective with the approval of the DUS. Music 91
will be approved by the DUS based on appropriateness of the proposed work, faculty
availability, and with the understanding that all work must be fully self-directed by the student.
Music 91 may not be used for private vocal or instrumental instruction or individual lessons.
Students may not take MUSIC 91 more than once.
LEARNING OBJECTIVES
The concentration in Music encourages students to develop a critical and practical
understanding of music by exploring a wide variety of musical styles, genres, and traditions.
Department of Music courses offer a solid foundation in music theory and musical analysis, as
well as compositional studies in both acoustic and electronic formats. Performance-oriented
studies include a focus on large ensembles, chamber music, instrumental and vocal
traditions, contemporary music, western classical music, jazz, historical performance practice,
improvisation, and conducting. Musicology and ethnomusicology proseminars promote a
critical understanding of music in diverse cultural, regional, and historical contexts.

Requirements: 10 courses (40 credits)
1. Required courses:
a. MUSIC 97 Sophomore Tutorial (4 credits)
b. One upper-level course (100+) in each of the following three categories (12
credits total):
i. History, Culture, Society (4 credits)
ii. Music Theory (4 credits)
a. MUSIC 51B (after successful completion of MUSIC 51A or
equivalent.)
or
b. Music Theory (100+)
iii. Creating Music (4 credits)
a. For large ensemble credit options, see Other Information below.
2. Electives: Six courses (24 credits):
a. All Department of Music courses satisfy concentration elective requirements. In
addition, the following courses may count, with the approval of the DUS:
i. One First-Year Seminar taught by Department of Music faculty
members.
ii. One GENED course taught by Department of Music faculty members.
iii. With prior approval from the DUS, MUSIC 91 may be used to fulfill
elective credit.
3. Honors Track (optional):
a. MUSIC 91, Supervised Reading and Research
i. Taken during Spring semester of the student’s Junior year, to serve as
a pre-thesis creative/research workshop, with faculty advisement,

culminating in a robust and detailed honors thesis proposal.
ii. MUSIC 91 does not count toward the required 10 courses.
b. MUSIC 99, Senior Tutorial
i. Taken during Fall and Spring semesters of the student’s senior year.
ii. MUSIC 99 does not count toward the required 10 courses.
c. An honors thesis is required. For thesis timeline, guidelines, and requirements
see the department website at https://music.fas.harvard.edu/undergraduate-
thesis.
4. Other information:
a. Students participating in faculty-led ensembles (Harvard Radcliffe Orchestra,
Harvard-Radcliffe Collegium, Harvard Glee Club, Radcliffe Choral Society,
Harvard Jazz Orchestra) may take these courses for concentration credit in the
following ways:
i. MUSIC 110, 114, 115, 116, or 118 must be taken twice (4 credits total)
to count toward the Creating Music category or a single elective
requirement.
ii. Students may enroll in these courses an unlimited number of times, but
they cannot be counted more than twice toward concentration credit.
b. Music 180 New Music Ensemble, Music 186 Contemporary Chamber Music,
and Music 189 Chamber Music Performance may be taken an unlimited
number of times, but they cannot be counted more than twice (8 credits total)
toward concentration credit.
c. Courses taken outside the Department of Music will be considered for
concentration credit at the discretion of the DUS.
i. Courses proposed for concentration credit must not compete with
course offerings available in the Harvard Department of Music.
Students should schedule an advising session with the DUS to confirm
course eligibility prior to petitioning for transfer credit.
ii. Students who have taken courses through Harvard’s cross-registration
programs may be eligible to apply credits from cross-registered courses
toward a concentration in Music, at the discretion of the DUS.
iii. Courses must be taken for letter grade to count towards concentration
category requirements. Courses taken pass/fail or SAT/UNSAT will
count towards elective credit only, and only at the discretion of the
DUS. If permitted, students may not count more than 2 courses
pass/fail or SAT/UNS towards a concentration.
iv. Courses proposed from outside the Department of Music must be taken
for a minimum of 4 credits each. Courses taken for fewer than 4 credits
will be considered on a case-by-case basis, at the discretion of the
DUS. Courses taken for 1 credit only will not be considered.
d. Students participating in the joint studies program with Berklee College of
Music or the dual degree program with the New England Conservatory of
Music may transfer a maximum of 3 courses to Harvard for academic credit,
with a maximum of 2 courses counting towards a concentration in Music.
i. Courses proposed for transfer must not compete with course offerings
available in the Harvard Department of Music and are approved at the
discretion of the DUS. Students should meet with the DUS to confirm
course eligibility prior to petitioning for transfer credit.
ii. Private instruction and/or one-on-one instrumental, vocal, or
composition lessons are not eligible for transfer credit.
iii. Students may transfer a maximum of 2 courses per semester.
iv. One-credit courses are not eligible for transfer. Two-, three-, and four-
credit courses are eligible for transfer credit, at the discretion of the

Department of Music DUS.
v. Participation in Harvard’s joint programs of study with Berklee and NEC
cannot be used as a mechanism to reduce the undergraduate course
load at Harvard. Students are expected to meet Harvard’s requirements
for undergraduate progress. Details on the required rate of progress are
included in the Harvard Student Handbook.
vi. To receive Harvard credit students must complete and submit the
Proposal Form for Dual Programs of Study out of Residence. More
information is available on the Dual Programs of Study website
https://music.fas.harvard.edu/academics/undergraduate-study-in-music-

ADVISING
Advising in the Department of Music is carried out by the Director of Undergraduate Studies
(DUS) with support from the Undergraduate Program Coordinator (UPC). The DUS has
overall responsibility for the academic progress of undergraduates and is available by
appointment to review programs of study. All students are required to schedule an advising
appointment with the Director of Undergraduate Studies prior to the declaration of a
concentration or secondary in Music to develop a strategy for fulfillment of academic
requirements. All concentrators will continue to be advised by the DUS prior to course
registration.
More information on undergraduate advising in the Department of Music can be found by
visiting the department website at https://music.fas.harvard.edu/academics/. Students may
schedule advising sessions with either the DUS or UPC.
RESOURCES
The Eda Kuhn Loeb Music Library provides access to a comprehensive collection of books,
musical scores, and audio and video recordings in a wide variety of disciplines including
historical musicology, music theory, ethnomusicology, composition, and historically informed
performance practice. Library staff are available to provide support for research and access to
special collections.
The Shelemay Sound Media Lab allows students to experiment with sound production
equipment and techniques. Workshops are offered regularly on various audio engineering
topics, and a suite of professional field recording equipment is available for student use.
Equipment may be checked out with a valid Harvard ID through the Music Lab Equipment
Room.
Practice Rooms and Instrument Lockers are available to all Harvard students. 15
soundproof practice rooms are located in the Music Building and are equipped with either
upright or grand pianos. Practice rooms are available on a first-come, first-served basis, using
the sign-out process available in the Department of Music main office. A valid Harvard ID is
required for practice room access. A limited number of instrument lockers are available for
instrument storage. Lockers are available for the academic year only (including over winter
break). Interested students should contact the Department of Music for more information.
Prizes and Fellowships are available through a variety of undergraduate funding sources.
Travel fellowships are available to students who wish to pursue opportunities requiring travel
away from Harvard University. Composition prizes are available to students who wish to
create composed works of a wide variety. Interested students should contact the Department
of Music for more information.
The Office for the Arts Music Lesson Subsidy Program provides support for instrumental
and vocal instruction to qualified, full-time, enrolled Harvard College undergraduates currently
receiving some financial aid. It is intended for students who, without financial help, could not
make a sustained commitment to private music study. Students should contact the Office for
the Arts for more information.
