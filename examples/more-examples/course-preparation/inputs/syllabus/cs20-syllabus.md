## Course goals

CS 20 is a fun, collaborative introduction to discrete mathematics for computer science. The
course is designed to welcome students into the study of computer science and proof-based
mathematics. In it you will learn proof-based discrete mathematics in a highly collaborative,
active environment.

CS 20 teaches all the math not taught in the traditional calculus/linear algebra sequence that
is needed to take more advanced courses in theory of computation and/or algorithms. That is, CS
20 teaches discrete mathematics, logic, and basic probability, but does not teach calculus or
linear algebra. It also gives a good introduction to reading mathematical notation and writing
formal proofs. Many of the topics in CS 20 are not part of the typical secondary school math
curriculum. Prospective students may find it helpful to take our placement test to gauge whether
the course material is appropriate for you.

A principal objective of CS 20 is to develop mathematical maturity, by which we mean that CS 20
prepares you to think mathematically, to read and write mathematics, and apply mathematical
methods for problem-solving. It is these skills, even more than the specific topics covered, that
students will find they use again and again in future coursework.

### Learning and Learning Experience Objectives

- Develop mathematical maturity: the ability to read, understand, and apply novel mathematical
  definitions and proofs
- Become proficient at writing mathematical proofs including direct proofs, indirect proofs, and
  inductive proofs
- Master or become proficient in applying discrete math concepts drawn from logic, number
  theory, sets and functions, graphs and trees, state spaces, recursive definitions, recurrence
  relations and series, asymptotics, and discrete probability
- Become proficient at typesetting mathematical writing using LaTeX
- Build collaborative problem-solving skills while also forming collaborative relationships with
  peers
- Develop comfort with productive struggle. Become comfortable facing and sitting with problems
  you do not initially understand or know how to solve. Develop specific techniques to explore
  and make progress on problems when they feel mystifying or very difficult.

## Course format

The class is taught in a "flipped" format in which students are expected to do reading and watch
mini-lectures before class and the class time is spent on active problem-solving. Classes are a
mix of problem demonstrations, group problem solving, individual problem solving, and full class
discussion. Teaching fellows are present to help groups and individuals as they work.

## Typical enrollees

This course is primarily designed for students with little to no background in proof-based
mathematics and/or discrete mathematics. It is commonly taken by students interested in pursuing
computer science as the skills taught are foundational and essential for the courses that follow
such as CS1200, CS1210, and CS1240. The course should typically not be taken by students who
have already taken other courses in proof-based mathematics such as Math 22, Math 25, Math 101,
Math 153, Applied Math 106, Applied Math 107. It should also not be taken by students who have
already taken any of CS1200, CS1210, or CS1240. There can sometimes be exceptions to this when a
student has taken a prior course but does not feel that they adequately mastered the necessary
skills in that course.

CS20 is a "soft requirement" for all CS concentrators and a requirement for Extension Students in
the Computer Science program. For college students, this means that either you need to take it or
you need to attest to already knowing this foundational material. It is intended to be taken
before more advanced course work, such as CS1200, CS1210, or CS1240.

Historically, about 1/3 of CS concentrators take the course. If the material is already familiar
to you and doing the problems and proofs is time-consuming but not enlightening for you, then
this course is not for you. If the material is new to you or if writing proofs is new to you,
then taking CS20 may make your future coursework in CS easier and more effective. So how do you
know if CS20 is for you?

- Read [the information the CS concentration provides](https://csadvising.seas.harvard.edu/concentration/courses/#placement)
  about CS20 placement and take a look at (and try) the official self-assessment.
  - *Note that the self-assessment is pretty challenging and may be quite time consuming if you
    do the entire thing. Our educated guess is that if we gave the self-assessment as a final
    exam for CS20, the average score would be about a 75%. If you find most questions
    straightforward or doable with effort, then you should skip CS20. If you can already
    correctly complete 75% of the assessment, you may want to consider skipping CS20 and doing
    some self-review of some material (or you may choose to take CS20 to further strengthen your
    foundation). If the self-assessment has a lot of unfamiliar material and/or you can't do near
    to 75% of it correctly in a reasonable amount of time, CS20 is for you.*

## Assignments and grading

### Grading Breakdown

This semester we are trying something completely different. The assignments and grading breakdown
will be as follows:

- 3 unit quizzes - 8% each
- 3 problem sets - 8% each
- 1 final exam - 24%
- Participation/In-Class assignments - 28%

### Assignment of Final Grades

With the shift in type of assessments, we expect that student raw scores will be lower this
semester than in previous semesters. We intend to calibrate assignment of final grades
accordingly so that the change in the nature of the course work does not negatively affect
student grade outcomes.

- The final exam will be similar to that of prior semesters. We will use this to help us to
  normalize grades and neutralize the difference in raw scores.
- The course is not graded on a curve. Grades are assigned based on performance and are not
  relative. If performance of students is better than in prior years, grades will be higher.

### Homework

Homework is as follows:

- In preparation for each class, there will be a combination of readings and mini-lecture videos.
  These will be essential to your learning and success in class. In total, this should take ~3-5
  hours per week.
- In preparation for the unit quizzes, there are practice question banks for each course topic.
  You should spend an additional ~2-4 hours per week working practice questions and getting
  feedback.
- In weeks in which there are problem sets, instead of working practice questions you should
  devote your 2-4 hours to solving the problems on the problem set. You may work with others to
  solve the problems but you must write up the solutions entirely on your own and ensure that you
  really understand what you have submitted. A good test of understanding is if you can explain
  your solutions well. There will be practice problems available for these units as well if you
  want them.
- It is advisable to attend at least 1 hour of TF office hours each week to ask questions and get
  help on practice material.
- In total, it is reasonable to plan to spend 6-8 hours of out-of-class time on the class per
  week in addition to the 2.5-3.75 hours per week of in-class time.

### In-Class Assignments

On most days you will be asked to complete and turn in an in-class assignment. These will
contribute to your participation grade. They will be graded on an Extraordinary/Good/Acceptable/Missing
scale. Your lowest 4 scores will be dropped.

### Unit Quizzes and Final Exam

Quizzes will be closed-book and administered during the class period. Unit quizzes will start
promptly at the beginning of class and have a duration of 60 minutes. The final exam will be a
standard 3-hour exam.

## Reading list

We will be drawing reading materials from three sources:

- *An Essential Discrete Mathematics for Computer Science*, Harry Lewis and Rachel Zax. This book
  is available in the Files section of the course site for registered students. Professor Lewis,
  the lead author of this text, taught CS20 for many years. This book is designed for use in this
  course and courses like it. We are grateful to Harry and Rachel for allowing you to use
  pre-print copy with their permission.
- *Mathematics for Computer Science*, Eric Lehman, F Thomson Leighton, Albert R Meyer
- [*Building Blocks for Theoretical Computer Science*](http://mfleck.cs.illinois.edu/building-blocks/version-1.3/whole-book.pdf),
  Margaret Fleck

Every year there are a smattering of complaints from students that it is annoying and/or
confusing to have three different textbooks and yet I persist in having all three. That is because
they have different virtues and students have strongly different preferences between them:

- Lewis & Zax's *Essential Discrete Math* is concise while also providing quite a bit of depth
  and detail. It tracks very closely with what we study in the course and provides what you need
  to know and some extra depth. Some students find it to be difficult to understand because it
  goes briefly and quickly through concepts and examples.
- *Mathematics for Computer Science* is the book used by CS20's MIT counterpart 6.1200 (formerly
  6.042). It is extremely detailed. It is often a good option as a second reading when you want
  more depth or another explanation of a given concept. Students sometimes find its sheer size to
  be daunting.
- Fleck's *Building Blocks* is the most readable of the three books. Most students find it clear
  and easy to understand. It does not cover some of the topics in as much depth.

On the reading assignments I will indicate the reading options from each of the texts and a
default suggested reading (usually drawn from Lewis & Zax, which I think of as the compromise
option) for those who do not want to make their own choice.

A note on reading (that is general to all math courses). Reading these texts is not like reading a
novel or a newspaper or even some fairly involved English texts. To get value from the reading,
you should do it with a pad and pencil beside you and make sure you understand the definitions and
derivations presented there. Mathematical notation can be dense, so don't expect yourself to be
able read and understand it at first glance!

## Academic Integrity and AI Policy

All work you submit for this course should be your own and fully authored by you except in cases
in which the instructor explicitly directs otherwise. If the course staff believes work you have
submitted may have been authored by AI, you may be asked to meet with a member of instructional
staff to discuss your submitted work and you may be referred to the Honor Council. Use of aid from
instructional support staff, peers, and AI is allowed and even encouraged to aid in learning and
practice of course concepts. It is recommended that you use the learning resources provided by the
course as we will expect that your work reflects both the content and techniques covered in the
class and will not award full credit to answers that, regardless of correctness, do not
demonstrate mastery of the content and techniques taught in the course.
