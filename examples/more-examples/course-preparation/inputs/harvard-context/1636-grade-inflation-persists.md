*In* [Part I](https://news.1636forum.com/p/why-grade-inflation-persists-at-harvard-despite-everyone-knowing-about-it-part-i)*, we unpacked the incentives that keep Harvard’s grades inflated. In *[Part II](https://news.1636forum.com/p/part-ii-why-grade-inflation-persists-at-harvard-despite-everyone-knowing-about-it)*, we explored what it might take to break the cycle. In our final piece of the series, we turn to why Harvard is uniquely positioned to lead grade inflation reform, and why now is the time to act.*

**Part III: The Case for Harvard To Charge Ahead**

“Recentering academics will be a defining feature of the year,” Faculty of Arts and Sciences (FAS) Dean Hopi Hoekstra [told](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2025/10/8/faculty-meeting-oct/) the faculty this month. Dean of Undergraduate Education Amanda Claybaugh has echoed that message to [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/06/us/harvard-students-absenteeism.html?unlocked_article_code=1.sU8.esGX.FFCxKXNL5Y9a&smid=url-share) and [The Atlantic](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2025/08/harvard-college-grade-inflation/684021/?gift=9_RR-jJpOiwbyy_NHLGfUXWrxrbHech4hbZe1zwjIsY&utm_source=copy-link&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=share). For Harvard to name its own academic weaknesses so publicly is rare, and that honesty is itself a mark of leadership.

Fixing grade inflation means taking a deliberate leap: setting new expectations [across higher education](https://www.gradeinflation.com/), even before others follow. In a [system](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2025/10/20/editorial-harvard-grades-nyt/) where inflated grades remain the norm, the first institution to change course will always face uncertainty. But if any university can prove that higher standards strengthen, rather than weaken, academic excellence, it’s Harvard:

**Harvard’s professional schools shape postgraduate pipelines and outcomes.**When Princeton[tried](https://paw.princeton.edu/article/pause-progress-toward-meeting-grade-deflation-goal)to curb grade inflation in the 2000s, it did so without graduate programs in core fields like law, business, or medicine. Harvard, by contrast, can coordinate across the College and its Law, Business, Medical, and other schools to help its admissions offices interpret new grading distributions consistently.**Harvard’s prestige gives it cover to lead.**Unlike universities[reliant](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/01/06/us/college-rankings-us-news.html)on rankings like*U.S. News & World Report*, Harvard can absorb short-term risk to strengthen long-term rigor.Clear communication that recalibration is about academic integrity and excellence, not punishment, could even enhance Harvard’s reputation and applicant pool.**The risks to students may be smaller than many assume.**Princeton’s faculty[found](https://www.princeton.edu/sites/default/files/documents/2017/05/PU_Grading_Policy_Report_2014_Aug.pdf)little hard evidence that tougher grading significantly hurt student job prospects or graduate school outcomes, despite students’ widespread fears it would.**Harvard can learn from past attempts.**The takeaway from Princeton’s[experience](https://www.princeton.edu/sites/default/files/documents/2017/05/PU_Grading_Policy_Report_2014_Aug.pdf)a decade ago is not that we need to discount quotas or curves altogether, but to understand where rigid systems faltered and where more flexible approaches, tailored by department or even course, could succeed.**Peer institutions are watching.**When Princeton rolled back its deflation policy, some Harvard faculty like philosophy professor Edward Hall[warned](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/2014/10/9/princeton-grade-deflation-reversal/)that the reversal would make reform elsewhere harder — a reminder that momentum in one place can shift norms everywhere.**Harvard has already begun.**After the Classroom Social Compact Committee’s[report](https://www.fas.harvard.edu/sites/g/files/omnuum716/files/2025-04/CSCC-FINAL_Adopted-03.04.25-FINAL-ua_0.pdf), the FAS[voted](https://www.harvardmagazine.com/university-news/harvard-fas-academic-concerns-trump-policies)to update the College handbook to state that “students are expected to prioritize their coursework.” The College has[launched](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2025/08/harvard-college-grade-inflation/684021/?gift=9_RR-jJpOiwbyy_NHLGfUXWrxrbHech4hbZe1zwjIsY&utm_source=copy-link&utm_medium=social&utm_campaign=share)a committee on grading reforms and Claybaugh has encouraged professors to grade more rigorously on their own.

But as we discussed in [Part II](https://news.1636forum.com/p/part-ii-why-grade-inflation-persists-at-harvard-despite-everyone-knowing-about-it), powerful incentives still push against rigor, which is why the next step must be a clear plan that turns acknowledgement into action and keeps momentum when inertia sets in.

**Q: What ever happened to the Taylor Swift class that Harvard College ran?**

Offered in spring 2024, [English 183ts: Taylor Swift and Her World](https://english.fas.harvard.edu/english-183ts-taylor-swift-and-her-world) drew over 200 students (mostly non-humanities concentrators) who expected a syllabus out of their [wildest dreams](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IdneKLhsWOQ) centered on Swift herself. Instead, they found a more traditional English course pairing Swift’s lyrics with writers like Cather and Wordsworth. Students praised the professor’s enthusiasm, but the course gained a [reputation](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lWDpOG6s2l8) for disorganization and grading that was rigorous yet inconsistent. It has not returned since, but is remembered [all too well](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sRxrwjOtIag) in Harvard [folklore](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=u9xsgqHR1oc).

__District Judge Issues Final Order in Harvard’s Federal Funding Case __

The judgment, which formalizes Burroughs’

[earlier ruling](https://news.1636forum.com/p/special-edition-breaking-down-the-district-court-ruling-in-harvard-v-hhs), reflects language jointly submitted by Harvard and the government and confirms three outcomes:**The grant freezes and termination actions against Harvard are legally void.****The government is prohibited from reissuing those same freezes or terminations, or from taking similar actions**like blocking grants or contracts, if done in retaliation for speech or without following Title VI.**The court's ruling in Harvard’s favor on core constitutional and statutory claims is now final and can be appealed.**These claims include violations of the First Amendment and the Administrative Procedure Act.

__Brown, Penn, Dartmouth, USC, UVA Reject White House Compact on Federal Research Funding; All U.S. Universities Invited to Sign On__

[Brown](https://www.brown.edu/sites/default/files/brown-response-federal-compact-10-15-2025.pdf),[Penn](https://penntoday.upenn.edu/announcements/update-penns-response-compact-academic-excellence),[Dartmouth](https://president.dartmouth.edu/news/2025/10/dartmouths-feedback-compact), the[University of Southern California](https://we-are.usc.edu/2025/10/16/update-from-interim-president-kim/?utm_source=news.franklinsforum.org&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=issue-8-penn-refuses-the-compact-rejection-as-redirection&_bhlid=a9778672c61891ef0f77127e08dbf8df4d5ca7cf), the[University of Virginia](https://www.cavalierdaily.com/article/2025/10/uva-rejects-trump-administration-compact-for-academic-excellence), and the[University of Arizona](https://president.arizona.edu/news/update-compact-academic-excellence-higher-education)have joined[MIT](https://www.wsj.com/us-news/education/mit-trump-college-compact-8fefcea4?st=m6juTL&reflink=desktopwebshare_permalink)in rejecting the White House’s[Compact for Academic Excellence in Higher Education](https://www.washingtonexaminer.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/Compact-for-Academic-Excellence-in-Higher-Education-10.1.pdf). The Compact offers preferential access to federal research funding in exchange for commitments including ideological rebalancing, tuition freezes, and international enrollment caps.Each university expressed concern that the Compact

**could undermine academic freedom, institutional autonomy, and the principle that research funding should be awarded based on merit.**While rejecting the current proposal, all indicated openness to continued dialogue with the federal government on Compact feedback and alternative frameworks that preserve independence.[Franklin’s Forum](http://franklinsforum.org), a Penn alumni group similar to 1636 Forum,[applauded](https://news.franklinsforum.org/p/issue-8-penn-refuses-the-compact-rejection-as-redirection)the rejections and urged the group of universities declining the Compact to still unite around shared principles and craft a collective vision for the future of higher education.Of the

[9 universities](https://news.1636forum.com/p/a-carrot-or-a-stick-in-disguise-a-look-at-the-white-house-s-new-university-compact)originally offered the Compact, 7 have now declined. Of the[2 remaining](https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/20/us/politics/universities-funding-compact.html),**UT Austin has not released a decision, while Vanderbilt**[said](https://www.nashvillescene.com/news/pithinthewind/vanderbilt-compact-diermeier-statement/article_b9b37908-9a5a-4914-afd6-711f957ebacd.html)**it would “continue to share [its] point of view with the administration.”**Feedback is due today, October 20.Meanwhile,

**the White House has**[extended](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-13/trump-invites-all-colleges-to-join-compact-after-mit-s-rejection)**the Compact invitation to all U.S. universities**and[reportedly](https://www.wsj.com/us-news/education/trump-administration-meets-with-more-colleges-about-sweeping-compact-after-rejections-153bfca4?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=AWEtsqe5vAI_lj0H1WAKXM6M5mWQkAEdJtRSsAuqvfP6yssmHC9vVBTK0Pd5t0BXKEo%3D&gaa_ts=68f5335a&gaa_sig=foyRbzZ4yME4000lqFC0Fn9tb3yItTX-7Xe8zVVU4hP9bMPv4tvUpyjqc6L5zAkGdyyw8Bm5RY_Ltq7sP4EhjQ%3D%3D)recently met with others to gather input, including Arizona State and Washington University in St. Louis.
