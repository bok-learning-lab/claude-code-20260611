# Director's advice: structuring courses to grade the full range

Practitioner guidance from Rebecca Nesson (Executive Director, Bok Center for Teaching
and Learning; Lecturer on Computer Science, Harvard SEAS) on how to structure
assignments, course policies, and grading practices so that a course produces a broad
distribution of grades grounded in content knowledge and skill, and clearly identifies
the students who have done A-level work of extraordinary distinction.

This is a distinct layer from the others in `inputs/`. The `bok-advice/` files are
verbatim Bok Center guidance pages; the `harvard-context/` files are Harvard's
institutional data and proposals; the `research/` files are the external empirical
evidence. This file is the **practitioner's normative stance** — settled judgment about
what a well-designed course must do, plus the open questions where settled judgment runs
out and the research layer has to do the work.

When an evaluation draws on this file, treat the settled principles below as requirements
a course should meet, and treat the open questions as the things the
[research syntheses](../research/) are meant to answer concretely.

---

## Settled principles

These are non-negotiable design conditions. A course that violates any one of them
cannot produce a defensible full-range distribution, no matter how its rubrics are
written.

### 1. Enough of the grade must rest on work that can actually spread

A large enough proportion of the final grade must be based on assignments capable of
producing a broad distribution grounded in content knowledge and skill. If most of the
grade comes from components with a low ceiling — completion credit, participation,
effort-based or near-universally-passed work — then no rubric or standard applied to the
remaining sliver can recover the range. The high-ceiling, content-and-skill-based work
has to carry enough weight that the overall grade tracks demonstrated mastery.

### 2. Assessments must span a wide enough range of skills and skill levels

Assessments have to test a broad enough range of skills and skill levels that they can
distinguish levels of mastery, *including at the top end*. A course can have demanding
work and still fail to separate the very best students from the merely strong ones if
every task tops out at the same place. The instrument has to include challenges that only
the strongest work clears — otherwise excellence is invisible to the grade.

### 3. Vary the kinds of assessment

There have to be varied kinds of assessments so that the evaluation is actually measuring
knowledge of the content rather than skill at a particular format of assessment. A
student who is good at timed exams but not papers (or the reverse) should not be confused
with a student who has or lacks mastery of the material. Diversifying formats — exams,
problem sets, papers, projects, oral work — makes the grade a measure of the content, not
of test-taking or essay-writing technique in isolation.

## Open questions for the research layer

These are the places where the settled principles above are clear about the *goal* but
silent on the *mechanism*. The research syntheses in [../research/](../research/) are
written to answer them.

### Q1. How do we make assignments challenging enough for the very top end without being discouraging or unapproachable for the modal or below-average student?

Principle 2 demands a high ceiling; basic fairness and motivation demand an approachable
floor. The question is how to build single assignments — or assignment sequences — that
do both: that a struggling student can enter and make real progress on, while still
giving the strongest students room to demonstrate distinction.

### Q2. How can grading policies and rubrics be set up so that we can have high confidence the distribution will not end up crowded at the top end?

Principle 1 is necessary but not sufficient. Even with weight on the right components, a
course can still compress at the top if the tasks don't discriminate there or the grading
scheme washes out differences. The question is what design and psychometric properties
give *advance* confidence — before grades are in — that the distribution will spread
rather than pile up at A.

### Q3. How do we use rubrics to promote differentiation rather than as a box-checking mechanism that binds faculty and TFs into giving high grades because all the boxes are checked?

The research base already in `inputs/` recommends analytic rubrics, but an additive,
criterion-by-criterion rubric can reward completeness over quality and leave graders no
defensible way to withhold a high grade from work that checks every box without being
excellent. The question is how to design and use rubrics (or alternatives to them) so
they sharpen judgment about quality and differentiate at the top, rather than flattening
it.
