# Actionable highlights: grading the full range and identifying distinction

Top-line advice a department could act on, distilled from the three research syntheses in
[../research/](../research/). Each section ties back to its evidence base; for the full
argument and citations follow the links. This is the practitioner's short list — the
companion [grading-for-the-full-range.md](grading-for-the-full-range.md) holds the settled
principles and the open questions these answer.

---

## From task design — low floor, high ceiling
Source: [full-range-task-design.md](../research/full-range-task-design.md)

- **Build the ceiling into *required* work, not optional extras.** Optional and challenge
  work is reliably taken up by students who are already strong (in one study, ~67% of
  high-grade students versus ~40% of low-grade students), so it widens the gap by rewarding
  the already-ahead and never reaches the middle. To distinguish the top, at least one
  *required* task must itself admit excellence — an open-ended part, a transfer problem, a
  question with no ceiling. Optional challenge work isn't forbidden; it just shouldn't be
  where the graded differentiation lives. Four reasons the graded ceiling belongs in
  required work:
  1. *Self-selection conflates opting-in with ability* — the optional task measures who
     chose to opt in as much as who is excellent, producing false negatives at the top.
  2. *It only adds resolution at the very tip* — the broad middle is still measured by a
     low-ceiling instrument and stays compressed.
  3. *"Extra credit" usually inflates rather than spreads* — it adds points on top, pushing
     more students toward A instead of pulling the distribution apart.
  4. *Equity of opportunity* — the non-ability factors that drive opt-in (time, information,
     confidence) correlate with background; a required task gives everyone the same shot at
     the ceiling.
- **Use "hard problem before instruction" to create something worth grading.** Productive
  failure (meta-analysis, g = 0.36) builds conceptual depth and transfer — the dimensions on
  which the best work separates — while doing nothing for rote procedure. A test made only of
  taught procedures has little depth to discriminate.
- **Use "low-floor, high-ceiling" tasks to measure across the whole range with one
  instrument.** A low-floor, high-ceiling task has an accessible entry point that any student
  can begin and make real progress on (low floor), and no cap on how far the strongest
  students can take it (high ceiling) — for example, an open-ended problem a struggling
  student can partially solve while the best produce a general result. Because the same task
  is approachable yet uncapped, it spreads performance across the full range and differentiates
  at the top without confronting weaker students with an impossible floor — which is exactly
  what a required high-ceiling component needs to do.

## From measurement — discriminating at the top
Source: [discriminating-at-the-top.md](../research/discriminating-at-the-top.md)

- **Audit every component for a ceiling effect.** Practical rule: if well over ~15% of
  students hit the maximum on a component, that component is hiding your top students, not
  measuring them. A crowded top is not just unfair — it makes the grade a biased estimate of
  achievement.
- **Reserve part of every major assessment for genuinely hard, discriminating items.** Spread
  is a property you design in: too-easy items add zero differentiation; you need items located
  at high difficulty so the instrument has precision where distinction would show up.
- **Match method to the work.** For convergent work, vary item difficulty and check
  discrimination. For open-ended work that saturates rubrics, consider comparative judgement to
  rank-order quality — but adopt it for its structure, not its advertised reliability numbers
  (which are inflated by adaptivity).

## From rubric design — differentiate, don't bind
Source: [rubrics-differentiation.md](../research/rubrics-differentiation.md)

- **Stop making the top grade conjunctive.** Requiring "outstanding on every criterion" is the
  exact mechanism that both compresses grades toward the middle and binds graders into rewarding
  box-checking work. Reserve the top grade for holistically excellent work, and say so.
- **Leave the ceiling open.** Use a single-point rubric, or leave the "exceeds" band deliberately
  undefined, so excellence is recognized rather than pre-enumerated — and a complete-but-ordinary
  submission doesn't auto-qualify for the top.
- **Weight the criteria that bite at the top.** A criterion that separates pass from fail won't
  separate good from excellent; don't assume one additive rubric differentiates uniformly across
  the range.
- **Spend effort on calibration, not finer rubric text.** Exemplars of genuine A-level work plus
  graders reconciling on shared samples do more for top-end differentiation than more rubric rows —
  and they keep a loosened rubric defensible across TFs and on appeal.
- **Be honest about the tradeoff.** Loosening rubrics costs reliability and audit-trail/appeal-
  defensibility; analytic rubrics genuinely reduce gross disagreement. The recommendation is not
  "drop rubrics" but "stop treating the rubric as an additive contract and re-anchor it to a
  calibrated standard of excellence."

---

## The through-line

A broad distribution and a visible top come from three moves together:
1. Put weight on work that can actually spread on content and skill.
2. Deliberately build and measure difficulty at the high end.
3. Refuse to let an additive checklist substitute for a calibrated judgment of excellence.
