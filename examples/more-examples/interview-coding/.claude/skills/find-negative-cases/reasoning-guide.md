# Reasoning guide — what counts as a negative case

This is a short reference for the analyst working with `/find-negative-cases`. It collects the methodological principles the skill is built on.

## The Blee quote, in full

Deterding & Waters (2018, p. 731) treat this passage as the standard for what rigor in interview research looks like:

> "Data that diverge from the pattern are not discounted without a clear rationale to do so." (Blee 2009)

The skill operationalizes the requirement: if your claim has counter-evidence in the corpus, surface it; do not bury it; do not dismiss it by count.

## What is and is not a negative case

| Case type | Definition | Treatment in memo |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| **Negative case** | An in-scope respondent whose evidence actively contradicts the claim's prediction. | Quote verbatim. Write reasoning paragraph. Drives the implications section. |
| **Out-of-scope case** | A respondent who does not meet the claim's premise (e.g., did not attend college when the claim is about mothers who did). | List under Scope. Do **not** count as a negative case. |
| **Mixed case** | An in-scope respondent whose evidence supports the claim in some moments and contradicts it in others. | Flag explicitly. Do not force a side. Often the most theoretically generative. |
| **Silent case** | An in-scope respondent who simply did not address the topic. | Note as silent. Absence of evidence is not counter-evidence. |

## Common failure modes

1. **Treating low frequency as low importance.** One disconfirming case can be theoretically decisive — especially if it breaks a presumed mechanism. Waters explicitly cautions against dismissing by count (p. 731).
2. **Conflating dimensions.** Many claims about interview data conflate two analytically distinct dimensions (reasoning vs. framing, content vs. style, what the respondent says they do vs. what they would actually do). A negative case often surfaces because the claim collapsed dimensions that ought to be kept apart.
3. **Out-of-scope mistaken for negative.** A respondent who never went to college is not a negative case for a claim about mothers who attended community college — she is out of scope. Reporting her as a negative case inflates the count and makes the memo less useful.
4. **Paraphrasing as evidence.** A paraphrase is the analyst's gloss, not the respondent's words. A reviewer cannot audit a paraphrase. Use verbatim excerpts only.
5. **Stopping after the first finding.** The second negative case often shifts the recommendation from "keep with explicit treatment" to "narrow in scope" or "abandon."

## When to recommend abandon / narrow / keep

- **Abandon** when the negative cases outnumber or theoretically outweigh the supporting cases, or when a single negative case decisively breaks the proposed mechanism.
- **Narrow in scope** when there is a clear, articulable boundary that separates supporting from negative cases (e.g., "holds for mothers without a college-educated relative; does not hold for mothers with one"). The boundary must come from the data, not from convenience.
- **Keep with explicit treatment** when supporting cases dominate but at least one negative case is theoretically interesting enough to demand engagement in the write-up. The negative case becomes part of the eventual paper, not buried.

## Why N=4 makes this tractable

At N=200 it is genuinely impossible for one analyst to hold every disconfirming detail in mind while writing claims. The skill's value at production scale is exhaustive coverage. At workshop demo scale (N=4) it is just illustration of the move — but the move is the same.

## Citations referenced in the skill
