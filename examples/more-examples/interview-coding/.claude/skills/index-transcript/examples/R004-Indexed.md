## INDEX: college-meaning (lines 44–62 in R004-Transcript.md)

**I:** What does college mean to you?

**DENISE:** College means a different life. It means your kids don't worry about lights getting cut off. It means you can take your mom to dinner. It means if somebody gets sick, you don't have to choose between the prescription and the rent. College is — it's the thing that breaks the cycle. That's what it is. That is what it is for us.

**I:** That's a beautiful way to put it.

**DENISE:** It's the truth. I lived the other version. I know what that version costs. So when I say college is about a different life, I don't mean it in a — in an abstract way. I mean it in a specific way. Specific bills. Specific worries. Those go away when one person in the family gets a real degree in a real field.

**I:** Have you talked to Andre about what to study?

**DENISE:** Engineering. That's it. I don't — I don't want to be the mom who says "study what you love" because nobody in our family can afford to study what they love. Terrance didn't study what he loved. He studied what worked. And it gave the rest of us the freedom to — well, we still can't really do what we love either, but the next generation can. Maybe Andre's kids can do what they love. That's how this works. You build it up over generations.

**I:** What if Andre decides he wants something different?

**DENISE:** Then we'll have a conversation. But I'm not putting that option on the table right now because I've seen what happens to kids who go to college thinking they're going to find themselves and come out with eighty thousand in debt and a degree in — sociology or something. I love sociology, no offense, but you cannot pay rent with it. I've watched it happen to people I went to high school with. They went to college, they majored in whatever, they came out worse off than they would have been if they hadn't gone. Because now they have the debt and they don't have the leverage that an engineering degree gives you.

**I:** So how would you describe your view on college?

**DENISE:** I think college is sacred. I do. I think it's the most important thing my son can do. But sacred doesn't mean — sacred means you take it seriously. Sacred means you don't waste it. You go for a reason. You come out with a credential that opens a door. That's what I mean by sacred. I don't mean it in a — like, romantic way. I mean it in a — this is the thing that determines the rest of his life, and the rest of his children's lives, and we're not going to mess it up by treating it like a vacation.
