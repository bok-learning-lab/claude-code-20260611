# R002 — Marisol

## INDEX: background-education (lines 12–22 in R002-Transcript.md)

**I:** Thanks for meeting me here. Can we start with where you grew up, and what school was like for you?

**MARISOL:** Of course. I grew up partly in Mexico, partly here. We came when I was nine. My English was — terrible, the first two years. I cried every day. But I got there. I loved school once I could understand it. I loved reading especially. I would read these books from the library — that's actually why I wanted to meet here, I love libraries — and I would imagine I was somewhere else. Reading was my escape.

**I:** What about college?

**MARISOL:** [pause] My father didn't believe in college for girls. He said it was a waste, that I would just get married and have babies, so why pay for school. He was old-fashioned, you know, from another time. I fought with him about it. I cried about it. But he was the one paying the bills and the answer was no. I went to work right after high school. I worked at a flower shop, then I worked at a hotel, and now I work at a daycare which I love. But — I always think about what I would have done. What I would have studied. I think I would have been a teacher. Maybe English, because of how books saved me when we came here. Something like that.

**I:** Do you carry that with you?

**MARISOL:** Every day. Every single day. And that's why with Sofia — okay, you'll see. Let me tell you about Sofia.

## INDEX: hopes-after-hs (lines 28–30 in R002-Transcript.md)

**I:** What do you hope for Sofia after high school?

**MARISOL:** College. One hundred percent. Four-year college. I want her to have the experience. I want her to go somewhere and live in the dorms and meet people from different places and stay up late arguing about books and — all of it. The whole thing. I want her to find out who she is. Because I never got to do that, you know? I went from my father's house to my husband's house, and I love my husband but — there's a part of me I never met. I want her to meet that part of herself.

## INDEX: college-meaning (lines 32–50 in R002-Transcript.md)

**I:** What do you think college is for?

**MARISOL:** It's for becoming. That's what I think. It's for becoming the person you're going to be. Of course you also study something, you get a degree, that's important. But the real thing is the time. It's having four years where your job is to figure out what you think and who you are and what you care about. When else do you get that?

**I:** Have you talked to Sofia about specific schools?

**MARISOL:** Yes, we talk all the time. She wants to go away. Not too far — she's a good girl, she wants to be able to come home — but away. We've looked at state schools. She's interested in the one near the coast. She wants to study journalism, or maybe sociology, she's not sure. And I tell her — don't decide yet. Go and see. Take classes in different things. That's the whole point.

**I:** What about cost?

**MARISOL:** [sighs] That's the hard part. We don't have money saved. My husband works at the auto shop, I'm at the daycare, we make what we make. So she'll need financial aid, she'll need scholarships. We've been to two college fairs. There's a program at her school that helps with applications, she's signed up. She got a 1340 on the SAT which her counselor said is good. So we're hopeful.

**I:** Are you worried about loans?

**MARISOL:** A little. But — okay, this is going to sound naive to some people. I think you have to invest in your children. If she has to take some loans, she'll pay them back. She's smart, she'll figure it out. I'm not going to tell her not to go because of money. That's exactly what my father did to me. I'm not doing that.

**I:** What if she changes her major three times?

**MARISOL:** Good! Good. That means she's actually thinking. That means she's actually trying things. I changed my mind about who I was a hundred times when I was nineteen — except I had to do it while working at a hotel front desk. She gets to do it while taking a class on French literature or — I don't know — anthropology. That's a gift. That's the gift.

## INDEX: sources-of-advice (lines 52–58 in R002-Transcript.md)

**I:** Where do you get your ideas about college? You've been thinking about this a lot.

**MARISOL:** Mostly from books, honestly. And movies. [laughs] I know that sounds — but it's true. I read books about college life. I watched *Dead Poets Society* probably twenty times in my twenties. I have a romantic idea of it, maybe. But I think the romantic idea is the true idea, you know? Underneath everything practical, that's what college is supposed to be.

**I:** Has anyone in your family been to college?

**MARISOL:** My niece went, she's the first. She studied — she's a nurse now. She loved her time at school though, she talks about it like it was magic. So I see it through her too.

## INDEX: worries-barriers (lines 60–62 in R002-Transcript.md)

**I:** What worries you?

**MARISOL:** That she'll feel like she has to come home. That she'll worry about us too much and not let herself have the experience. I tell her all the time — don't worry about us, we're fine, you go. Go be young. Go be twenty.

## INDEX: reflections (lines 64–74 in R002-Transcript.md)

**I:** Anything you wish was different?

**MARISOL:** I wish college was free. [laughs] Obviously. But also — I wish there was less of this talk now about, what's your job going to be, what's the return on investment. That language makes me sad. Because that's not what college is. That's trade school. There's nothing wrong with trade school — but college is different. College is for the soul.

**I:** Is there anything I didn't ask?

**MARISOL:** Just — write down that I'm excited. I'm really excited. This is the thing I've been waiting for since she was born. To get her to this door and open it for her.

**MARISOL:** Thank *you*.
