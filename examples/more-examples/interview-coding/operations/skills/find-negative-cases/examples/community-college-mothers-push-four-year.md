# Negative-case audit — community-college mothers and four-year pushes

**Claim audited:** "Mothers who attended community college themselves push their children toward four-year schools."

**Corpus:** [inputs/transcripts/](../../../../inputs/transcripts/), N = 4.

**Date of audit:** 2026-05-21.

## Scope

The claim's scope condition is *mothers who attended community college themselves*. Mothers who did not attend any postsecondary school are out of scope — they cannot test the claim's mechanism.

- **R001 Tasha** — in scope. "I took two semesters at the community college after high school and then I got pregnant with Jamir and that was that." ([R001 L22](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R001-Transcript.md#L22)).
- **R002 Marisol** — out of scope. Did not attend college; her father refused to pay ([R002 L18](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R002-Transcript.md#L18)).
- **R003 Carla** — in scope. "After high school I went to the community college and got my associate's in medical billing." ([R003 L14](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R003-Transcript.md#L14)).
- **R004 Denise** — in scope. "I took some classes at the community college a few years after that, didn't finish, life got in the way." ([R004 L18](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L18)).

## Negative cases

### R001 Tasha

**Verbatim excerpts:**

> "All the time. Especially with Jamir coming up on ninth grade. So I'll tell you my honest opinion. For Jamir, I'm pushing trade school. HVAC, electrician, something like that. Because here's the thing — and people don't want to hear this — I know grown men who went to college, took out loans, and they're working at Best Buy. With debt." — [R001 L34](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R001-Transcript.md#L34)

> "For Jamir there's a program at the technical college I've been looking at. Two years, you come out certified, they do job placement. Ninety percent placement, that's what their website says. That's the thing — they have data. Colleges don't tell you their job placement rate, you notice that? Trade schools do." — [R001 L58](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R001-Transcript.md#L58)

> "Aaliyah's different. She might actually want a four-year. She talks about being a doctor sometimes, or a vet. She loves animals. If she wants to do that — okay. But she's gotta know what she's going for. I'm not paying for 'exploring.'" — [R001 L42](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R001-Transcript.md#L42)

**Reasoning:** Tasha attended community college herself but actively pushes her son away from four-year schooling toward HVAC or electrician training, and her support for a four-year path for her daughter is heavily conditional on a specific labor-market plan. Her reasoning is not "my own community-college experience taught me to want better for my kids"; it is "my own experience taught me that the four-year credential does not pay for itself, so we choose the path that does." The claim's implicit mechanism — that having attended community college motivates upward push toward a four-year — does not run here. The labor-market calculation and her cousin Marcus's story (L34, L38) override her own credential trajectory in shaping what she wants for Jamir.

## In-scope cases that support the claim

- **R003 Carla** — Pushes Marcus toward four-year college, weighing the in-state university against a regional private offering a scholarship ([R003 L46](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R003-Transcript.md#L46)). Her own community-college experience is explicitly part of why: she names the literature elective she did not get to take ([R003 L18](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R003-Transcript.md#L18)) as the version of college she wants Marcus to have.
- **R004 Denise** — Pushes Andre toward engineering at the state university; the path was set "since he was small" ([R004 L38](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L38)) and the family's information source is her brother Terrance, an engineering graduate ([R004 L66](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L66)).

## Implications for the theory

**Recommend: Narrow in scope, or abandon.**

In a corpus of three in-scope respondents the claim splits two-to-one in its favor, but the lone negative case (Tasha) does substantive damage to the proposed mechanism. The supporting cases (Carla, Denise) share a feature Tasha lacks: a model of what a four-year degree concretely enables — Carla's missed literature elective and Denise's brother's engineering-driven family ascent. The community-college experience alone does not predict the push toward four-year; what predicts it is the presence of a vivid model of what the four-year degree delivers, against which the mother's own truncated path is measured. A defensible reformulation: "Mothers who attended community college push their children toward four-year schools when they have a concrete model of what the four-year degree affords; in the absence of such a model, the labor-market calculation against bachelor's debt may override the credential-upgrade motive." That reformulation accommodates all three in-scope cases and is testable on a larger corpus. With N = 3 in-scope respondents, the count alone does not settle the matter — Waters cautions against dismissing disconfirming cases by frequency (p. 731), and Tasha's evidence is detailed and consistent across the interview.
