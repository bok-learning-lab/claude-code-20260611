# Negative-case audit — instrumental mothers and family/emotional framing

**Claim audited:** "Instrumental mothers reject family/emotional framing of college."

**Corpus:** [inputs/transcripts/](../../../../inputs/transcripts/), N = 4.

**Date of audit:** 2026-05-21.

## Scope

The claim is about *instrumental mothers* — respondents whose underlying reasoning about college is oriented to economic return, credential leverage, and risk management rather than to self-discovery or experience. Expressive mothers and clearly mixed cases are not the population the claim is about, so they are out of scope.

- **R001 Tasha** — in scope. Instrumental throughout: "College is for getting a job. That's what it is now." (L46).
- **R002 Marisol** — out of scope. Expressive: "It's for becoming the person you're going to be" (L34).
- **R003 Carla** — out of scope. Explicitly mixed; names the two voices in herself and refuses to choose between them (L34-38).
- **R004 Denise** — in scope. Reasoning is instrumental: "I don't want to be the mom who says 'study what you love' because nobody in our family can afford to study what they love. Terrance didn't study what he loved. He studied what worked" (L54).

## Negative cases

### R004 Denise

**Verbatim excerpts:**

> "College means a different life. It means your kids don't worry about lights getting cut off. It means you can take your mom to dinner. It means if somebody gets sick, you don't have to choose between the prescription and the rent. College is — it's the thing that breaks the cycle. That's what it is. That is what it is for us." — [R004 L46](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L46)

> "It changes everything. It changes everything to have one person in the family who has a real career. Because then everybody else has — has a floor under them. Like, if I lose my job tomorrow, I can call Terrance. I don't *want* to call Terrance, but I can. My mom doesn't worry about her bills anymore. That is what one degree did for us." — [R004 L34](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L34)

> "Andre is going to be the next Terrance. That's the plan. That's been the plan since he was small." — [R004 L38](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L38)

> "I think college is sacred. I do. I think it's the most important thing my son can do. But sacred doesn't mean — sacred means you take it seriously. Sacred means you don't waste it. You go for a reason. You come out with a credential that opens a door." — [R004 L62](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L62)

> "Just write down that I love my son and everything I'm doing is for him." — [R004 L78](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R004-Transcript.md#L78)

**Reasoning:** Denise's reasoning about college is instrumental in the strict sense — engineering credential, stable career, risk-managed, oriented to credentialed leverage in a labor market — but her *framing* of that reasoning is saturated with family and emotional vocabulary. She calls college "sacred," she names the cycle her brother's degree broke, she narrates the whole plan as a multi-generational family project. The claim assumes that instrumental reasoning travels with instrumental framing, but Denise demonstrates that the two can come apart: the analytic logic is about return on a credential while the rhetoric is about love, sacrifice, and family ascent. This is the construct-validity move Deterding & Waters describe (p. 731) — a respondent whose surface vocabulary would naively code as expressive while the underlying mechanism is instrumental.

## In-scope cases that support the claim

- **R001 Tasha** — Rejects emotional framing explicitly: "Instead it's all this 'follow your passion' stuff. Follow your passion does not pay rent. That should be on a poster somewhere" ([R001 L70](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R001-Transcript.md#L70)). Frames her own kids' future in terms of return on investment and labor-market math ([R001 L38](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R001-Transcript.md#L38), [R001 L46](../../../../inputs/transcripts/R001-Transcript.md#L46)). Her vocabulary tracks her reasoning.

## Implications for the theory

**Recommend: Narrow in scope.**

The claim conflates two dimensions that ought to be kept analytically separate: the *logic of reasoning* about what college is for (instrumental vs. expressive) and the *rhetorical framing* the respondent uses to describe that reasoning (transactional vs. family/emotional). Tasha demonstrates that the two can co-vary, but Denise demonstrates that they need not. A defensible reformulation: "Instrumental mothers whose family context does not feature a successful college-educated relative tend to reject family/emotional framing; instrumental mothers with such a relative may frame the same instrumental logic through family and emotional vocabulary." That reformulation is testable, has a stated boundary (the presence of a Terrance-figure), and treats Denise as theoretically informative rather than as an exception to be discounted. With only one negative case in a corpus of four, this finding should not be dismissed by count — Waters explicitly cautions against that move (p. 731); the disconfirming case is the point.
