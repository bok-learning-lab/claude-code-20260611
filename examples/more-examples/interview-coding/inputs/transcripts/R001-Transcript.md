# R001 — Tasha

**I:** Thanks again for sitting down with me. Can we start with you telling me a bit about where you grew up and what school was like for you?

**TASHA:** Sure. I'm from right here, born and raised. Westside. Went to the schools you'd expect. Nothing fancy. I was a decent student — not great, not terrible. I'll be honest, I was more interested in working. I had a job at McDonald's when I was sixteen and that was kind of my whole world.

**I:** What drew you to working?

**TASHA:** Money. [laughs] No, but really. My mom was a CNA and there wasn't ever extra. So as soon as I could get a check, I wanted that check. And I liked it. I liked having my own thing. School felt like — okay, you sit there, you do the work, and at the end of the day you don't have anything to show for it. At work I had a check.

**I:** Did you think about college back then?

**TASHA:** Yeah. My mom always said "you gotta go to college." Everybody says that. But she didn't — nobody in my family went, so it was kind of like, what does that even mean? I didn't know what I'd study, I didn't know how I'd pay. To be real, my grades weren't gonna get me a scholarship. I took two semesters at the community college after high school and then I got pregnant with Jamir and that was that.

**I:** Tell me about your kids.

**TASHA:** Jamir's fourteen, going into ninth grade. Aaliyah's eleven. They're night and day. Jamir is a sweet kid but school is not his thing. He's like me, honestly. He'd rather be doing stuff with his hands. He fixes everything in the house. Last month the toaster broke and I find him with it apart on the kitchen floor — I'm yelling, that's a fire hazard, baby — and he's got it fixed in twenty minutes. Aaliyah's the opposite. She reads, she draws, she's quiet. Her teachers love her.

**I:** How are they doing in school?

**TASHA:** Aaliyah's straight A's. Jamir passes. He's not failing, but he's not — he's bored. Last conference his teacher said he's smart but he doesn't apply himself, and I'm like, yeah, I know.

**I:** Have you started thinking about what they'll do after high school?

**TASHA:** All the time. Especially with Jamir coming up on ninth grade. So I'll tell you my honest opinion. For Jamir, I'm pushing trade school. HVAC, electrician, something like that. Because here's the thing — and people don't want to hear this — I know grown men who went to college, took out loans, and they're working at Best Buy. With debt. My cousin Marcus has a four-year degree in sociology, something like that, and he manages the Verizon store. He makes thirty-five thousand a year and owes sixty in loans. My other cousin DeShawn went to HVAC school for nine months, paid eight thousand, and he's clearing seventy. With overtime, more.

**I:** So you've done the math.

**TASHA:** Yeah. I look at it like, what's the return on the investment? Because I don't have college money sitting somewhere. Whatever we do is gonna be paid with loans or with work. You need a plan. You don't just go to college because someone said go to college. That's how you end up like Marcus.

**I:** What about Aaliyah?

**TASHA:** Aaliyah's different. She might actually want a four-year. She talks about being a doctor sometimes, or a vet. She loves animals. If she wants to do that — okay. But she's gotta know what she's going for. I'm not paying for "exploring."

**I:** When you say "exploring" —

**TASHA:** I mean — when she goes, she needs her major picked, she needs to know what job that major gets her, she needs to know what that job pays. There's no reason to take out fifty thousand dollars in loans to figure out who you are. You figure out who you are at home. College is for getting a job. That's what it is now. I see these kids on TV at the protests, at these private colleges, paying sixty thousand a year and they don't know what they want to do. Like — what is happening?

**I:** What do you think gives you that perspective?

**TASHA:** Life. [laughs] I don't have the luxury of looking at it any other way. If my kid takes on a hundred thousand in debt and can't get a job, that's my problem too. People with money can take risks. We can't take risks.

**I:** Have you talked to the kids about this?

**TASHA:** Jamir, yeah. He knows I want him doing a trade. He's okay with it. Aaliyah I haven't really pushed yet. But she'll know what I think when the time comes.

**I:** Have you looked at specific programs?

**TASHA:** For Jamir there's a program at the technical college I've been looking at. Two years, you come out certified, they do job placement. Ninety percent placement, that's what their website says. That's the thing — they have data. Colleges don't tell you their job placement rate, you notice that? Trade schools do. Because they know that's why you're there.

**I:** Where do you get information about all this?

**TASHA:** Online, mostly. I do a lot of Googling. There's a lady at Jamir's school, the counselor, I talked to her once but honestly she was pushing the four-year track and I was like, okay, you do that for the kids that's right for. I'm here for my kid. I do my own research. There's a Facebook group for moms in the district, that's actually helpful.

**I:** What worries you, looking ahead?

**TASHA:** Money. Always money. If Aaliyah does want to be a doctor — that's a long road, that's a lot of money, and I have no idea how we'd do it. I try not to think about it too hard or I won't sleep. We'll figure it out. We always do.

**I:** Is there anything you wish was different about how this all works?

**TASHA:** I wish college was honest about what it was. I wish they told you, here's what this degree gets you, here's what it costs, here's the math. Instead it's all this "follow your passion" stuff. Follow your passion does not pay rent. That should be on a poster somewhere.

**I:** [laughs] Anything I didn't ask that I should have?

**TASHA:** I think you got me. I'm an open book on this. I've thought about it a lot.
