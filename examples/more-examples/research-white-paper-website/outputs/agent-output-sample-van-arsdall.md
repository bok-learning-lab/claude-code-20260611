# Van Arsdall's translator's-choice notes — a philological inventory

*Compiled from the Author's Notes prefacing her translation and from the numbered per-chapter footnotes (C = … notation, where C is Cockayne) in [sources/old_english_herbarium.txt](../sources/old_english_herbarium.txt). May 2026.*

---

## Note on Van Arsdall's framing of the project

Two competing obligations structure every choice she makes:

1. **Faithfulness to the Old English** — what the OE word literally says.
2. **Transmitting information accurately to a modern reader** — what a modern, often Latin-less reader needs in order to use the text as Van Arsdall thinks the text was meant to be used: as a working aide-memoire for a practitioner, not a step-by-step manual.

Cockayne (1864–66) is her constant foil. His translation is "a more-or-less word-for-word rendition" whose lexical choices have aged into opacity. Van Arsdall's project is *modernizing* — accepting some loss of OE flavor (word order, repetition, filler particles) in exchange for usability. Almost every choice below sits on this axis.

A key methodological commitment, stated explicitly: *the Herbarium was not intended to be a step-by-step manual.* It assumed an apprenticed reader who already knew how to "unpack" terse instructions. Translation choices that flatten the apprenticeship-knowledge layer (e.g. converting *cnucian* into a chain of English near-synonyms) would falsely impose specificity that the OE does not carry.

## Part I — Translator's choices made in the Author's Notes (prefatory)

### Lexical / verbal

#### 1. *cnucian* (variant *cnocian*) → "pound" (retained)

OE *cnucian* means "to pound." Modern English has many nearby verbs: bruise, mash, crush, pulverize, chop. A translator could disambiguate based on context (a hard root vs. a leafy aerial part vs. a wet poultice would idiomatically require different verbs in modern English).

**Van Arsdall's choice:** keep "pound" almost everywhere.

**Tradeoff:** the OE uses *cnucian* repeatedly without disambiguation precisely *because* the text assumed an experienced reader who would supply the right action from context. To translate *cnucian* as "pulverize" in one chapter and "chop" in the next would be a false precision — the text is not making that distinction. Keeping "pound" preserves the textual evidence that the *Herbarium* is a memory aid for an already-trained leech, not a recipe book for a beginner. The cost is mild infelicity in modern English (one always pounds, which can sound mechanical).

#### 2. *wyllan* → "gently boil"

Literal meaning: "to boil." But modern herbal practice — confirmed in hands-on workshops Van Arsdall attended — never *truly* boils medicinal herbs; it simmers them, holding the liquid below a true rolling boil (one curandera said the water should be "smiling").

**Van Arsdall's choice:** "gently boil." Literal preserved (still recognizably *boil*), corrected for what a competent practitioner would actually do.

**Tradeoff:** strict philological literalism (just "boil") would produce a translation that, if followed by a modern reader, would destroy volatile actives — exactly the chemistry Madeleine's project depends on. Van Arsdall is making a *materially-aware* translation: she is translating the verb in a way that aligns with what the practitioner did in practice rather than with the bare lexical surface. The cost is a small interpretive overlay that a strict philologist could push back against (the OE does not literally say "gently"). The gain is alignment with the practice the text encodes.

This is a model for the same move the harvest-times project makes at a larger scale: surface ritual / surface lexicon vs. underlying material practice.

#### 3. *seoðan* (to seethe / simmer) → "simmer" or "seethe," used "nearly interchangeably"

Literal *seoðan* spans both gentle simmering and active infusion (seething). In practice the two produce nearly the same result — and a medieval practitioner would have learned the subtle difference observationally, not from a written specification.

**Tradeoff:** by treating *seoðan* and "simmer" as substitutable, Van Arsdall trades terminological precision for accuracy about how the text was actually used. The cost: a reader interested in lexical distinctions cannot reconstruct from the translation which verb the OE used in any given line. The gain: she does not pretend to a precision the OE does not carry.

### Pronouns and gender

#### 4. *man* + *him* → "them"

OE *man* meant "one / a person," and the following *him* was non-gendered in OE usage. Cockayne, working in the 1860s, translated as "a man" with "him/his" — making the text read as if its instructions and patients were male.

**Van Arsdall's choice:** "them" / "they" / "one" — bucking formal written English in favor of widespread spoken English.

**Tradeoff:** strict philological transparency (preserve "him" as in the manuscript) vs. removing a gender skew that is an artifact of nineteenth-century English, not of the OE source. This is also a *retrieval* move: Cockayne's apparent literalism imported a gender bias that wasn't in the OE. Van Arsdall's "them" is closer to the OE *man* than Cockayne's "a man" is. The cost is mildly informal English. The gain is representational accuracy plus modern readability.

### Medical register

#### 5. Latinate medical vocabulary → colloquial English

Modern medical English uses Latinate terms (fetus, menses, anus, diarrhea, urination, constipation, genitals). Van Arsdall replaces these with: *child, women's periods, rear, very runny stools, pass water, stopped up intestines, private parts.*

**Justification:** Latinate medical vocabulary entered English vernacular use *long after* the *Herbarium* was translated. To use modern Latinate terms is to anachronistically import a register the OE doesn't have. The OE register was vernacular and colloquial.

**Tradeoff:** modern medical readers will find the colloquialisms non-clinical. But the OE register was non-clinical — using Latinate English re-medicalizes the text in a way the original wasn't medicalized. Van Arsdall preferred to mirror the *register* of the OE rather than to elevate the text into a clinical idiom it never had. (She does pull back from the most colloquial — "shit," "piss" — settling at "very runny stools" and "pass water." This is a register choice, not a philological one: closer to OE in feel than to either modern medical English or modern crude English.)

### Word-level identifications she revised between editions

These are notes where Van Arsdall explicitly changed her mind between the first and second editions, and the *philological* tradeoff is between a confident specific identification and a more honest place-holder.

#### 6. *cild / cyld* → "child" for all of: living child, fetus, stillborn

OE has no separate word for *fetus*. *cild/cyld* covers all three. Chapters 63.1 and 165.4 use *tudder/tuddor* — once with "dead" modifier, once unmodified for an unborn child.

**Van Arsdall's choice:** translate *cild/cyld* as "child" everywhere; modify only when the OE does ("dead child").

**Tradeoff:** modern English readers expect *fetus* to be a separate category. Translating *cild* uniformly as "child" risks reading-comprehension friction — but it's the only way to preserve the OE's *non-distinction*, which is itself important data. A translation that introduces "fetus" where the OE only has "child" would be projecting modern obstetric categories onto a text that does not have them. A note explains the issue.

#### 7. *flewsa* → "flux" (with abortifacient notes omitted)

*flewsa* is "flow" — bodily flow of an unspecified kind. When the context is menstrual, it's clear; otherwise, it's not.

**Van Arsdall's choice:** retain "flux" rather than disambiguate. Also: she explicitly removed her first-edition notes flagging some remedies as possible abortifacients, on the grounds that "abortion vs. menstrual regulation vs. fertility concerns" deserve more careful study than a marginal note can give.

**Tradeoff:** the original text is ambiguous; preserving "flux" honors the ambiguity. Disambiguating in translation would freeze an interpretation that is not actually fixed in the OE. The abortifacient-note removal is a related move: rather than commit to a politically charged reading (these were abortifacient remedies) or its opposite (these were menstrual-regulation remedies) without rigorous textual support, she withholds judgment. Cost: readers lose a possible reading. Gain: she does not advance an interpretation she cannot fully defend; she points to Riddle (*Eve's Herbs*) and Green (*The Trotula*) for further work.

This is *intellectual conservatism in translation* — the inverse error from Cockayne, who was sometimes too willing to commit (e.g., his "tertian and quartan fevers," his Greek substitutions, his medicalizing turns).

#### 8. *nafola* → "navel" (revised from her first edition's "anus")

Earlier edition: she translated *nafola* (literally "navel") as *anus*, on the grounds that (a) many "worms about the navel" remedies use ingested herbs to expel worms, and (b) Latin *umbilicum* (navel) can refer to a knot of skin such as at the anus.

**Revision:** revert to literal "navel." She acknowledges the kinds of worms in the text are uncertain — possibly intestinal, possibly anal, possibly skin conditions like molluscum contagiosum or cutaneous larva migrans around the navel. Without archaeological evidence for the actual parasites of early-medieval England, no specific identification is supportable.

**Tradeoff:** her first-edition reading was *interpretive* (she read past the literal to a clinical hypothesis). The revision is *conservative* (let *nafola* mean *nafola* until evidence supports the move). This is a methodological lesson she states elsewhere: a translator who builds plausible interpretations into the translation itself is hard to correct later — readers cite the translation, not the OE — so the safer move is the literal one with a discursive note explaining the alternatives.

#### 9. *teter* → "skin conditions" (revised from "impetigo")

First edition: *impetigo*, a specific modern dermatological diagnosis. Cockayne: "tetter" — an archaic English word for skin infections generally. DeVriend: ringworm.

**Revision:** "skin conditions." Generic, because the actual condition denoted by *teter* is not really known.

**Tradeoff:** a specific identification (*impetigo*, *ringworm*) is more useful for a clinician trying to ask "did the AS leech treat impetigo with X?" — but it commits the text to a diagnosis the text does not actually make. "Skin conditions" is less informationally rich, more honest. Cockayne's "tetter" is in the same generic register but uses an English word now opaque to modern readers.

#### 10. *hreofl* → "bad skin condition" (often translated "leprosy")

DOE accepts "leprosy" as a real possibility. Literal sense: rough, scabby, scaly skin. Leprosy was widespread at the time, but many scaly/rough skin disorders aren't leprosy.

**Van Arsdall's choice:** "bad skin condition." Same logic as *teter*: don't commit to a modern clinical diagnosis the OE does not certify.

**Tradeoff:** the medical-history reader will want to know whether AS leeches were treating leprosy specifically. Van Arsdall is saying: the text says "rough scabby skin"; don't pretend it says "leprosy." The reader who needs the leprosy hypothesis can take it from the discursive note; the reader who would be misled by a too-confident translation is protected.

#### 11. *fotadle* → "foot disease" (revised from "gout")

"Gout" is a specific modern diagnosis requiring professional differentiation from other inflammatory foot conditions. *fotadle* literally means "foot disease."

**Revision:** "foot disease," awaiting evidence that the more specific gloss is warranted.

**Tradeoff:** identical to *teter* and *hreofl*. A pattern emerges: where Cockayne (and her own first edition) committed to specific modern diagnoses, the second edition retreats to literal generic glosses. *Retroactive diagnosis*, in Van Arsdall's view, is a translator's overreach.

(Same logic appears in note 144 for *dropa* in the marjoram chapter — translated as "gout" in many dictionaries, but DOE primarily means "drop," and the conditions for which the plant is recommended are dissimilar enough — *dropa*, liver disease, shortness of breath — that the unified-modern-diagnosis "gout" cannot cover all three.)

## Part II — Per-chapter "C = …" footnotes

These ~180 numbered footnotes appear after the translation. Van Arsdall flags every place where her translation differs appreciably from Cockayne's, and every place where the modern plant identification is contested. The pattern is consistent with the prefatory choices, but several specific cases bear directly on the harvest-times project.

### 2A. Lexical disagreements with Cockayne (a sampling)

| OE | Cockayne | Van Arsdall | Tradeoff |
|---|---|---|---|
| *gewyrc to duste* (note 5) | "reduce it to dust" | "make into powder" | "Reduce it" is opaque early-modern phrasing. "Make into powder" is what the practitioner actually does. Cockayne is more literal; Van Arsdall is more usable. |
| *scearfian* (note 6) | "scrape" | "shred" | Both lexically defensible. Van Arsdall: "shred" makes more sense before powdering a (possibly dried) plant. The case shows philology supplemented by material plausibility. |
| *seoð on wætere to þriddan dæle* (note 8) | "boil it down to the third part" | "simmer down to one third" | Van Arsdall: "Modern usage is to reduce liquid by so much, usually by simmering (not boiling) a long time." Same *wyllan* logic — match lexicon to actual practice. |
| *Wiþ sidan sare* (note 10) | "for sore of side" | "for a pain in the side" | Cockayne's word order is OE-mimicking; modern English wants *the* and *a*. |
| *twega* "two" (note 11) | Cockayne emends to "three" "to agree with the Latin original" | "two" — keeps the OE | Cockayne suppresses the OE/Latin discrepancy to harmonize with the source text. ### 2B. Plant-identification disputes (the cases that affect the harvest-times analysis)

These are the chapters where multiple authorities (C = Cockayne, DeV = DeVriend, D'A = D'Aronco/Cameron, Bierbaumer, DOEPN, DOE) disagree, and Van Arsdall has to pick one. Almost all of them flag *which* plant the AS practitioner was actually working with — directly relevant to whether modern phytochemistry can be applied to the remedy.

#### Note 35 — "Bee Plant" / *ueneria* / *beowyrt*

First edition: "sweet flag" (*Acorus calamus*). Second edition: literal "bee plant" because "newer studies are calling that identification into question."

DOEPN identifies it as *Melissa officinalis* L. (lemon balm) on the bee-association grounds. DeVriend MS illustration evidence is more compatible with *Acorus calamus*. M.A. D'Aronco (private communication 2022) leans toward yellow iris. Conclusion: "A definitive identification may one day be made."

**Tradeoff:** Van Arsdall's retreat from a specific identification ("sweet flag") to a place-holder literal name ("bee plant") parallels her *fotadle/gout* retreat. She sacrifices the convenience of a single Linnaean identification in order not to lock in a reading the philological evidence cannot support. This matters enormously for the harvest-times project: if the plant is *Melissa officinalis* it joins the Lamiaceae cluster with August-flowering pharmacology supporting the harvest instruction; if it is *Acorus calamus* (not introduced to Britain until ~1596) the AS practitioner was working with something else entirely. ([analysis/deep_research_findings.md §IV](../analysis/deep_research_findings.md))

#### Note 49 — Dragonwort vs. snakeweed (chs. 6 and 15)

Cockayne, DeVriend, D'Aronco identify ch. 15 as *Dracunculus vulgaris* (dragonwort) and ch. 6 as *Polygonum bistorta* (bistort/snakeweed). Hunt identifies *both* as bistort. Van Arsdall accepts the C/DeV/D'A split.

**Tradeoff:** for the harvest-times analysis, the bistort April-root finding hinges on this being *P. bistorta* — a fact this note makes contingent on which authority you follow.

#### Note 68 — "Ashwagandha" (ch. 23)

First edition: no modern name supplied. C, DeV, Bierbaumer = lily of the valley. D'Aronco (citing Maggiulli and André) = *Withania somnifera* (ashwagandha). DOEPN agrees with D'Aronco.

Van Arsdall: changed her mind, accepts ashwagandha. The note acknowledges the only English name found ("poison gooseberry") is rarely used medicinally.

**Tradeoff:** this is a case where philological humility (no name in ed. 1) gave way to evidence (DOEPN + D'Aronco's citation chain). The choice illustrates the iterative character of the work — between editions the field has moved, and Van Arsdall updates rather than defending her earlier reading.

#### Note 74 — Madder (ch. 29)

DeV, Bierbaumer = *Viburnum lantana* (wayfaring tree, "never used for medicinal purposes" per Grieve). C = *Sambucus ebulus*. DeV: "the plant is difficult to identify."

**Tradeoff:** the madder counterexample in the harvest-times analysis ([analysis/expanded_candidates.md:46-65](../analysis/expanded_candidates.md#L46-L65)) is *already* contingent on a contested identification. Van Arsdall flags this. If the plant is *Viburnum lantana* the whole pharmacological evaluation collapses (no medicinal use). If it is *Rubia tinctorum* (madder proper), the harvest-time misalignment is real but the plant has known anthraquinone activity.

#### Note 100 — Aster vs. Chickweed (ch. 61)

D'A (with André) = aster. C and DeV = chickweed (*Stellaria media*). D'A's argument: chickweed does not grow on stony ground. Counterargument: Grieve says chickweed grows virtually everywhere and notes its flowers open ~9 a.m. and remain open 12 hours — i.e., into the night. Van Arsdall: "The final word is not in."

**Tradeoff:** for any harvest-time analysis of this plant, the lunar/Virgo timing instruction in the manuscript means very different things depending on which species is meant. The note flags the uncertainty; it does not resolve it.

#### Note 102 — Pomegranate vs. Cockle (ch. 66, peony)

*Maligranati* = pomegranate, but whether pomegranate would have been known in England at the time is uncertain, "and may be why it is left in Latin." DOE identification = cockle (a grain-field weed).

**Tradeoff:** an example of a *non-translated* OE term being itself diagnostic — the OE translator's decision to leave a name in Latin may signal that the OE practitioner did not have a vernacular plant for it, which is evidence about the practical availability of the plant. Van Arsdall extracts a methodological observation from a translator's apparent gap.

#### Note 103 — *monaðlic adl* / "month-sickness" → "lunacy" (ch. 66)

The OE word for the condition treated by peony is literally "month-sickness." Modern English: insanity. But the medieval concept depended on the moon causing the disorder; "lunacy" preserves the moon-connection that "insanity" loses.

**Tradeoff:** literal modernization ("monthly sickness," "menstrual condition," "insanity") would lose the etymological link between the OE *monaðlic*, Latin *lunaticus*, and the moon-causation belief that explains the prescribed nighttime harvest of peony. Van Arsdall picks the older English word ("lunacy") because it carries the medieval cosmological frame that the modern equivalents have stripped out. This is the inverse of her usual modernizing move: here, an archaic English word is *more* faithful to the OE conceptual world than any modern alternative.

(For Madeleine: this footnote alone justifies why the peony case in the harvest-times analysis has to be discussed with the moon-cause-of-madness etiology in view — the etymology is the evidence.)

#### Note 110 — *Centaurea calcitrapa* / star thistle / ironwort (ch. 74, herba heraclea)

C: no name, calls illustration "fantastic." DeV = star thistle, on a manuscript gloss C had rejected. D'A (and André): ironwort. Van Arsdall accepts D'A.

The kicker, almost in passing: *"Sideritis is believed to heal wounds caused by anything iron."*

**Tradeoff (and signal for the harvest-times project):** if "ironwort" is the right identification, this is a separate AS-text site where iron is functionally implicated in plant medicine — *as the wound-cause* rather than *as the harvest-tool prohibition* (betony, mandrake). Worth flagging for the iron-and-phenolic-chemistry argument in [analysis/deep_research_findings.md §VI](../analysis/deep_research_findings.md). The thematic association between this plant's name, its illustrated identity, and iron-wound remedies could be philological evidence that AS practitioners had a *categorical* concept around iron in plant medicine — one that includes both the iron-tool prohibition and iron-wound healing.

#### Note 124 — Pennyroyal etymology: *dweorgedwosle* (dwarf-...) (ch. 94)

DeV: the *dweorge* (dwarf) element relates to medieval beliefs about dwarfs causing illness. DOEPN: it just means "smaller variety of the plant." Van Arsdall flags both readings.

**Tradeoff:** "whether one sees a connection to dwarfs or to an ancient diminutive form of the name could be important to many studies, especially where magic is alleged to be involved." This is exactly Madeleine's territory. Van Arsdall is showing that *every* plant-name etymology can be read either as evidence-of-magical-thinking or as evidence-of-ordinary-naming-convention, and the call has to be made philologically, not ideologically.

#### Note 178 — Peony / sunflower (ch. 171)

C: *Paeonia officinalis*. DeV: this chapter is not in any Latin source he consulted, and identifies it as *Helianthus annuus* (sunflower).

**Tradeoff:** sunflower (*Helianthus annuus*) was unknown in pre-Columbian Europe — so a 10th-c. AS identification of sunflower would be impossible. DeV's identification therefore implies either (a) the OE chapter was added later from a non-classical source, or (b) DeV is wrong. The harvest-times analysis tags this case as one of the most genuinely uncertain in the corpus; Van Arsdall's footnote shows why the philology is unstable.

### 2C. Sociological / register notes about Cockayne

A class of footnotes simply records *what Cockayne did in places where his choices look ideologically loaded* — not lexical disputes per se, but observations about translator-as-mediator. Examples:

- **Note 70 (chamomile, ch. 24):** Cockayne supplies the Greek letters Χαμαίμηλον for the plant name although they are not in the manuscript. — Cockayne adds classical apparatus the OE doesn't have. (This note matters for Madeleine's argument: Cockayne's *supplied Greek* is the kind of classicizing gesture that visibly imposes a Greco-Roman frame onto an OE source, exactly the operation Madeleine traces in the rational/magical historiography.)
- **Note 159 (fleabane, ch. 143):** Cockayne gives an entire entry in Latin and translates OE *cennan* as *parere* (to give birth); the underlying Latin original has *abortionem praegnantibus facit* — i.e., the OE has been Bowdlerized via Latin. 
- **Note 164 (shepherd's purse, ch. 150):** Cockayne translates the phrase about stimulating women's periods *into Greek*. (Cockayne shifting to Greek for sexually charged content.)
- **Note 166 (St. John's Wort, ch. 152):** Cockayne writes "menses" in Greek and calls genitals "naturalia."
- **Note 167 (globe thistle, ch. 153):** "The translation of this passage is a particularly good example of Cockayne's style: For stirring of the mie or urine, take this same wort, so oozy, pounded, give to drink; it forth leadeth the mie." — Van Arsdall uses Cockayne's prose against him: this is an unreadable sentence in modern English.
- **Notes 171, 181, 182 (iris, yarrow, castor-oil):** Cockayne shifts to Latin or Greek at points where the medical content is sexual or culturally charged. **Pattern:** Cockayne's literalism breaks at exactly the points where Victorian propriety asserts itself. Translation as social act, with the translator's discomfort visible in the lexical record.

**Tradeoff at the meta-level:** Van Arsdall reads Cockayne as a person, working in a specific decade, with specific Victorian conventions, whose translation choices are *evidence* about him as much as about the OE. A modern translator who reproduces Cockayne's choices reproduces those Victorian conventions — including the gender bias, the Latinizing of unpleasant content, the importing of classical apparatus, and the dismissive register toward "barbarous" material. Cockayne's Greek alphabet for *Χαμαίμηλον*, his "naturalia" for genitals, his Latin substitutions for menstruation — these are Victorian evidence dressed as philology. Van Arsdall's translation is a deliberate undressing.

## Part III — what unifies the choices

A short summary of the philological logic behind Van Arsdall's choices:

1. **Modernize lexically; preserve the underlying ambiguity of the OE.** Use modern English that a non-Latinist can read, but don't smooth over places where the OE does *not* commit to a specific meaning. (*cnucian* stays "pound," *teter* stays "skin condition," *fotadle* stays "foot disease," *flewsa* stays "flux.")

2. **Match register to the OE, not to modern medical convention.** The OE was vernacular and colloquial; translate it that way, even if modern medicine prefers Latinate vocabulary. ("Pass water," "private parts," "very runny stools.")

3. **Translate to the practice, not just to the surface lexicon.** *wyllan* = "gently boil" because that is what a competent practitioner did. *bewrit þriwa* describes a horticultural action (loosening soil) more than a magical one. This is the move that quietly licenses Madeleine's whole project: surface-ritual instructions can be re-read as material practice, with no philological violation.

4. **Don't retro-diagnose.** "Foot disease" not "gout"; "skin condition" not "leprosy"; "month-sickness" not "menstrual disorder" but "lunacy" (preserving the moon-etiology). Translation should not import modern biomedical categories that the OE text does not actually make.

5. **When identifications are contested, mark the contest.** Van Arsdall changes her mind between editions when evidence shifts (ashwagandha, ribwort plantain, croton, gorse, eryngo). She does not pretend to certainty she does not have ("Bee Plant," "Aster or Chickweed," peony / sunflower).

6. **Cockayne is evidence — about Cockayne.** Treat his translation choices as historically-situated artifacts. Where his literalism imports nineteenth-century conventions (gendered pronouns, Latin or Greek substitutions for sexual content, classical apparatus the OE doesn't have), his "literalism" is itself an interpretation. Modern translation should make these gestures visible rather than reproduce them.

## Implications for the harvest-times project

Three places where Van Arsdall's choices matter directly for Madeleine's argument:

**a. The "translate to the practice" move (point 3 above) is the same move the harvest-times project makes at the level of whole instructions.** *wyllan* → "gently boil" is, structurally, the same kind of inference as "harvest betony in August without iron" → "preserve phenolic acids by avoiding Fenton-catalyzed oxidation." Both move from surface linguistic / surface-ritual content to underlying material practice. Van Arsdall's translation methodology is therefore a precedent in the same monograph she is being cited from. This could be used in [drafts/paper2_intellectual_history.md](../drafts/paper2_intellectual_history.md) — Van Arsdall as a *translator* is already doing the kind of recovery work the *paper* claims is needed at the conceptual level. The argument becomes: *what Van Arsdall did at the verb-level for cnucian and wyllan, this paper does at the level of the harvest instruction.*

**b. Several plant identifications central to the alignment count are contested.** Bee Plant (Melissa vs. Acorus vs. Iris), madder (Rubia vs. Viburnum lantana), aster vs. chickweed (61), peony/sunflower (171), and several others are *not securely identified* — and Van Arsdall says so. The "9 of 11 testable candidates" framing in the empirical paper draft already excludes the most uncertain identifications (per [drafts/paper1_ethnopharmacology.md §2.2](../drafts/paper1_ethnopharmacology.md)), but the per-chapter notes show how much the corpus depends on contested IDs. A reviewer who reads Van Arsdall closely will know this. The paper could pre-empt by making explicit how identification uncertainty was handled, with citations to specific Van Arsdall footnotes.

**c. Note 110 (*herba heraclea* / ironwort, "heals wounds caused by anything iron") is a previously unflagged piece of evidence for an AS conceptual cluster around iron in plant medicine.** It pairs with the iron-tool prohibition for betony and mandrake in [analysis/deep_research_findings.md §VI](../analysis/deep_research_findings.md) — but from the *opposite* direction (iron as wound-causer, plant as iron-counteragent, vs. iron as harvest-contaminant, prohibited from contact).
