# Theoretical Framework: Ritual as Algorithm, Harvest as Empiricism

*The epistemological argument for reading Anglo-Saxon harvest-time instructions as encoded pharmacological knowledge*

---

## I. THE PROBLEM: A DICHOTOMY THAT WON'T DIE

The study of Anglo-Saxon medicine has been organized, since Cockayne (1864-66) and concretized by Cameron (1993), around a dichotomy between "rational" remedies and "magical" ones. The rational is identified with classical learning — Dioscorides, Pliny, Hippocrates — and the magical with insular, vernacular, pagan or syncretic practice. Remedies are sorted into bins: this one is rational because it uses a known pharmaceutical; that one is superstitious because it prescribes a lunar phase or an incantation.

This dichotomy has been challenged but never dissolved. Scholars like Jolly (1996), Banham, Horden, and Van Arsdall have pushed back against its crudest forms, arguing that Anglo-Saxon medicine had more Latinity than Cockayne allowed, more rationality than Singer and Grattan claimed. But as Madeleine's Review of Scholarship demonstrates, these defenses often *reinforce* the dichotomy by its own terms: defending AS medicine by pointing to its classical sources merely reaffirms that rationality originates in Greece and Rome, and that insular knowledge is only legitimate insofar as it can be traced back to Mediterranean authority.

The harvest-time evidence offers a different kind of challenge — not a defense of Anglo-Saxon medicine within the dichotomy, but a **dissolution of the dichotomy itself**. When the instruction to "gather betony in August without iron and dry in shade" is shown to optimize phenolic compound preservation by modern pharmacological standards, the categories of "rational" and "magical" collapse. The instruction is simultaneously:

- **Ritual** (it prescribes a month, prohibits a tool, and is embedded in a text that calls the plant "very holy")
- **Empirical** (it reflects accumulated observation about when the plant is most potent)
- **Algorithmic** (it encodes a step-by-step procedure for quality control of raw pharmaceutical material)
- **Pharmacologically correct** (it aligns with modern evidence for peak bioactive compound concentration)

No single category — rational, magical, empirical, ritual — captures what is happening. The instruction is all of them at once.

## II. PASQUINELLI'S ALGORITHMIC RITUAL

Matteo Pasquinelli's "Three Thousand Years of Algorithmic Rituals: The Emergence of AI from the Computation of Space" (e-flux Journal #101, 2019) provides the theoretical apparatus for thinking past the dichotomy.

### The argument

Pasquinelli reads the Vedic Agnicayana ritual — constructing a falcon-shaped fire altar from thousands of precisely-numbered bricks, each placed with its dedicated mantra — as a **primordial example of algorithmic culture**. The ritual transmitted techniques of geometric approximation comparable to modern calculus, encoded in procedural form and transmitted through repetition across millennia. His definition of "algorithm":

> (1) an algorithm is an abstract diagram that emerges from the **repetition of a process** ... it is not a rule that is invented from above but **emerges from below**; (2) an algorithm is the **division of this process into finite steps** in order to perform and control it efficiently; (3) an algorithm is a solution to a problem, an invention that bootstraps beyond the constraints of the situation: any algorithm is a **trick**; (4) most importantly, an algorithm is an **economic** process, as it must employ the least amount of resources.

### Application to harvest-time instructions

The Old English Herbarium's harvest instructions satisfy all four criteria:

**Emergent from below.** These instructions were not deduced from theory. No Anglo-Saxon text presents a theory of volatile oil chemistry or phenolic oxidation. The instructions encode **observation accumulated through practice** — generations of healers noticing that betony harvested in August works better than betony harvested in March, that herbs cut with iron tools lose potency faster, that plants dried in the sun are weaker than those dried in shade. The knowledge emerged from below, from the repeated practice of making medicine, not from above, from philosophical first principles.

**Divided into finite steps.** "Gather in August / without iron / dry in shade" is a three-step quality control protocol. "Before sunrise / say what you are picking it for" is a two-step protocol (timing + verbal declaration of intent). These are finite, reproducible, transmittable procedures — the definition of an algorithm.

**A trick.** The instruction to avoid iron tools is a *trick* in Pasquinelli's sense — a non-obvious solution to a problem the practitioner may not even have been able to articulate theoretically. The practitioner did not need to know that iron catalyzes oxidation of phenolic compounds via Fenton chemistry; they needed only to know that *it works better this way*. The trick bootstraps beyond the constraints of the practitioner's theoretical knowledge.

**Economic.** Pre-sunrise harvest is the *minimum intervention* needed to preserve volatile compounds. No equipment, no reagents, no processing — just timing. Iron avoidance is cheaper than any post-harvest stabilization treatment. Shade-drying costs nothing beyond patience. These are remarkably economical solutions to the problem of pharmaceutical quality control.

### The epistemic colonialism move

Pasquinelli's strongest polemical contribution is this:

> "Claiming that abstract techniques of knowledge and artificial metalanguages belong uniquely to the modern industrial West is not only historically inaccurate but also an act ... of implicit **epistemic colonialism** towards cultures of other places and other times."

He quotes Jean-Luc Chabert:

> "Algorithms are not confined to mathematics ... The Babylonians used them for deciding points of law, Latin teachers used them to get the grammar right, and they have been used in **all cultures** for predicting the future, for **deciding medical treatment**, or for preparing food."

The historiographical trajectory Madeleine traces — from Cockayne's "barbarian hordes" through Singer and Grattan's "final pathological disintegration of Greek medical thought" to Cameron's dichotomy — is **precisely** the epistemic colonialism Pasquinelli names. The assumption that empirical knowledge must look like post-Enlightenment propositional science in order to count as rational is not a neutral analytical framework; it is a historically situated judgment that privileges one epistemological tradition over all others.

The harvest-time evidence demonstrates that **empiricism can take non-propositional form**. The Anglo-Saxon practitioner did not write: "Volatile phenolic compounds in Stachys officinalis reach maximum concentration during the flowering stage in late summer, and are susceptible to oxidative degradation catalyzed by transition metals such as iron(II)." They wrote: "Gather it in August without iron." The knowledge is the same. The encoding is different.

## III. BALSAMIC TIME AND CHRONOPHARMACOGNOSY

The emerging field of chronopharmacognosy provides the modern scientific framework within which the Anglo-Saxon instructions become legible.

### Balsamic time

Hazrati, Mousavi & Nicola (2024) introduce the concept of **"balsamic time"** — the optimal harvest moment when secondary metabolites in a medicinal plant reach peak levels. The term itself is borrowed from traditional herbalism (the "balsamic period" is the harvest window), now formalized with phytochemical data. They review genetics, plant age, seasonal/daily variation, and developmental stage effects on compound quantity and quality.

The Anglo-Saxon instructions are, in effect, **prescriptions for balsamic time** — encoded in the idiom of months, lunar phases, and times of day rather than in the idiom of phenological stages and HPLC chromatograms, but encoding the same empirical observation: *when you harvest matters for what you get*.

### The matter of time

Liebelt et al. (2019) — "Only a matter of time: the impact of daily and seasonal rhythms on phytochemicals" — provide the mechanistic review. Phytochemical composition is under complex temporal control involving:

1. **Circadian clocks** — endogenous oscillators regulating gene expression in ~24-hour cycles
2. **Environmental cues** — light, temperature, humidity as zeitgebers (time-givers)
3. **Developmental programs** — ontogenetic changes across the plant's life cycle
4. **Stress responses** — biotic and abiotic stressors that trigger secondary metabolite production

Their warning is directly relevant: failing to account for harvest timing "can result in misleading estimations of the potency and composition of phytochemical extractions." This means that **modern pharmacological studies that do not control for harvest timing are themselves epistemologically incomplete** — a point with delicious irony for a thesis arguing that Anglo-Saxon practitioners controlled for timing while modern researchers often do not.

## IV. THE CHAMOMILE CASE: A PARABLE

The chamomile finding condenses the entire argument into a single data point.

The Old English Herbarium (24:1) prescribes: "pick the plant... **before sunrise**" for eye pain.

Modern research (Journal of Spices and Aromatic Crops) finds:
- **Total essential oil yield** is *lowest* at 6 AM (0.15%) and highest at noon (0.18%)
- But **alpha-bisabolol oxide-A** — the primary anti-inflammatory sesquiterpene, the compound most relevant to treating eye inflammation — **peaks at 6 AM**

A modern researcher measuring total oil yield would conclude that the Anglo-Saxon instruction is wrong. A researcher measuring the therapeutically relevant compound would conclude that it is right. The difference is in what you measure — and what you measure depends on what you think you're looking for.

The Anglo-Saxon practitioner was not optimizing for total oil yield. They were optimizing for **therapeutic efficacy against the stated condition** — eye pain and inflammation. By that measure, their instruction is correct, and more precisely targeted than a naive "harvest when oil yield is highest" approach would be.

This is not a trivial point. It suggests that the empirical observation embedded in the harvest instruction was calibrated to *clinical outcome*, not to any proxy measure like aromatic intensity or oil volume. The practitioners were observing what worked for patients, not what smelled strongest. This is, in a meaningful sense, an empirical methodology — outcome-oriented, iteratively refined through practice, and encoded in a transmittable procedure.

## V. WHAT RITUAL DOES THAT THEORY CANNOT

Why encode pharmacological knowledge in ritual form at all? Why not simply write: "August is the best month for betony"?

Several reasons emerge from the literature:

### 1. Ritual is mnemonic

In an oral or semi-oral culture, ritual form aids memorization. The instruction "gather it in August without iron" is more memorable embedded in a passage that calls the plant "very holy" and prescribes specific gestures than it would be as a bare empirical claim. The ritual context provides the **memory palace** (to use Pasquinelli's framing from his Section 5 on the Method of Loci) within which the practical knowledge is stored.

### 2. Ritual enforces compliance

A practitioner who believes the plant is holy and the harvest procedure sacred is more likely to follow the instructions precisely than one who knows only that "August is somewhat better than July." The ritual frame converts a soft recommendation into a hard constraint. This is functionally equivalent to a modern standard operating procedure (SOP) in pharmaceutical manufacturing — the ritual creates **procedural discipline**.

### 3. Ritual transmits without requiring understanding

The practitioner does not need to know *why* iron degrades phenolics in order to avoid iron. The ritual transmits the practice without requiring the theory. This is enormously efficient: it means the knowledge can survive even when the theoretical understanding that generated it is lost, forgotten, or never articulated. As Pasquinelli argues, the algorithm "emerges from below" — it is the codification of an observed regularity, not the application of a theory. Ritual preserves the codification even when the observation is no longer accessible.

### 4. Ritual compounds efficacy

As Madeleine argues in her Review of Scholarship, the placebo effect and efficacy phrases (EPs) in Anglo-Saxon medicine may **compound** the pharmacological effect. A remedy that combines a pharmacologically active plant, harvested at peak potency, with an efficacy phrase ("this is a special leechdom") and a ritual context (faith, intention-setting, practitioner authority) is attacking the illness from multiple vectors simultaneously. The ritual does not contaminate the pharmacology; it *supplements* it.

Kaptchuk's research on placebo and ritual theory supports this: "routine biomedical pharmacological and procedural interventions contain significant ritual dimensions," and intention-setting within contexts of faith healing "can elicit powerful medical benefits, even over and above conventional medications."

## VI. THE COUNTEREXAMPLES AND INTELLECTUAL HONESTY

The thesis does not claim that every ritualized instruction in the Old English Herbarium encodes empirical pharmacological knowledge. Two clear counterexamples exist in our corpus:

### Peony (Ch. 66): Mythological harvest, genuine efficacy

The instruction to harvest peony at night because "it shines like a lantern" derives from classical mythology (Pliny's *aglaophotis*), not from empirical observation. There is no pharmacological basis for nighttime harvest of peony roots. Yet the plant's use for "lunacy" (epilepsy) is clinically validated — a modern trial showed 62.5% seizure reduction in intractable childhood epilepsy.

This case shows that **Anglo-Saxon practitioners could identify the right plant for the right condition** even when the harvest instruction was inherited mythology rather than empirical observation. It suggests that plant-condition matching and harvest-time knowledge may have operated on different epistemic tracks — the former grounded in clinical observation, the latter sometimes in classical inheritance.

### Madder (Ch. 29): Ritual timing, wrong season

The instruction to harvest madder roots "before sunrise in July" with a purity requirement does not align with modern knowledge — madder root anthraquinone content peaks in **autumn** of the 2nd-3rd year, not midsummer. The "before sunrise" and purity elements are ritual overlays without pharmacological basis. (This case is further complicated by uncertain plant identification.)

These counterexamples are not embarrassments to the thesis — they are essential to it. They demonstrate that the claim is not "every AS instruction is secretly rational" (which would be naive) but rather: **the systematic pattern of alignment across the corpus is too consistent to be coincidental, and the aligned cases show a level of pharmacological sophistication that cannot be explained by chance alone.**

The score — 9 of 11 testable candidates showing alignment (82%) — is the strongest version of this argument. It is a pattern, not a universal law.

## VII. TOWARD A NON-DICHOTOMOUS READING

The traditional reading sorts remedies into bins:

```
RATIONAL                          MAGICAL
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Classical origins                 Insular origins
Pharmacologically active          Pharmacologically inert
No ritual elements                Ritual/religious elements
"Scientific"                      "Superstitious"
```

The harvest-time evidence suggests a different model:

```
          ┌──────────────────────────────┐
          │     ALGORITHMIC RITUAL       │
          │                              │
          │  Empirical observation        │
          │  encoded in ritual form       │
          │  transmitted as procedure     │
          │  refined through practice     │
          │                              │
          │  ┌────────────────────────┐  │
          │  │ Pharmacological layer  │  │
          │  │ (plant + timing +     │  │
          │  │  preparation)         │  │
          │  └────────────────────────┘  │
          │  ┌────────────────────────┐  │
          │  │ Placebo/ritual layer   │  │
          │  │ (EPs + faith +        │  │
          │  │  practitioner)        │  │
          │  └────────────────────────┘  │
          │  ┌────────────────────────┐  │
          │  │ Mnemonic layer        │  │
          │  │ (ritual form as       │  │
          │  │  knowledge carrier)   │  │
          │  └────────────────────────┘  │
          │                              │
          └──────────────────────────────┘
```

In this model, the layers are not in competition. They are **concentric** — the pharmacological knowledge is at the center, the placebo/ritual effect supplements it, and the mnemonic/ritual form preserves and transmits both. No layer contaminates or negates another. The remedy works because *all the layers work*, each in its own register.

This is what Madeleine's Review of Scholarship was already reaching toward with the argument that "the assimilation of 'rational' and faith-driven approaches in AS medicine may actually have increased overall medical viability." The harvest-time evidence provides the empirical ground for that claim.

## VIII. CONNECTIONS AND LINEAGES

### Pasquinelli → Staal → Vedic computation → AS harvest ritual

Pasquinelli's reading of the Agnicayana builds on Frits Staal's documentation of the ritual (Kerala, 1975) and Paolo Zellini's discovery that it encoded techniques of geometric approximation. The structural parallel to AS harvest instructions is direct: in both cases, a ritual procedure encodes empirical/technical knowledge that was not articulated in propositional form, was transmitted through practice and repetition, and remained operative for centuries without requiring its practitioners to understand the underlying mathematics or chemistry.

### Kaptchuk → placebo → EPs → ritual as medicine

Ted Kaptchuk's work on placebo and ritual theory (2011) — which Madeleine's Review already engages — establishes that "routine biomedical pharmacological and procedural interventions contain significant ritual dimensions." The harvest-time evidence extends this: not only does ritual *supplement* pharmacology through placebo, but ritual *encodes* pharmacology through procedural form.

### Jolly → hybrid epistemology → beyond the dichotomy

Karen Jolly's *Popular Religion in Late Saxon England* (1996) argued that pagan and Christian elements in OE texts were not survivals but "conscious decisions by local monastics and the Church at large to be adaptive." The harvest-time evidence adds a third layer: the hybridization is not only theological (pagan + Christian) but also epistemic (empirical + ritual). The monks who wrote these texts were operating within a knowledge system where observation, faith, classical inheritance, and insular practice were not separate compartments but **facets of a single integrated practice**.

### Elshakry → postcolonial STS → who gets to count as scientific

Marwa Elshakry's "When Science Became Western" (Isis, 2010) — cited in Madeleine's bibliography — traces how the identification of "science" with European modernity was itself a historical construction. The harvest-time evidence is a small case study within this larger argument: the Anglo-Saxon instructions were dismissed as unscientific not because they failed empirically but because they did not *look* like post-Enlightenment science. The epistemological form was wrong, even when the empirical content was right.

## IX. THE SCIENTIFIC METHOD AS RITUAL: HISTORY OF SCIENCE PERSPECTIVES

The dichotomy that has organized Anglo-Saxon medical scholarship depends on an unexamined assumption: that "scientific" practice has a stable, transhistorical identity, and that pre-modern practices can be evaluated against it. The history of science has spent forty years dismantling this assumption. Drawing on that literature does not merely defend AS medicine — it dissolves the ground on which the dichotomy stands.

### What counted as "experience" before the experiment

Peter Dear's *Discipline and Experience* (1995) traces a fundamental shift in what the word "experience" meant in natural philosophy. In the Aristotelian framework dominant through the medieval and early modern periods, *experientia* meant **habitual, general, universal** knowledge — the kind of thing that *anyone* would know, encoded in maxims and common observations accumulated by many observers over time, not by a single person on a single occasion. "Fire burns." "Herbs gathered before the dew lifts are stronger." These are experiences in the Aristotelian sense: the distilled residue of generations of practice, transmittable without reference to any specific witnessing event.

The shift to the **individual, dated, witnessed experimental event** — what we now identify as scientific observation — happened gradually across the seventeenth century and was only complete with the Royal Society's formal protocols for receiving reports. What Boyle witnessed in his laboratory on a specific day, with named witnesses present, constituted a new kind of epistemological object: the singular experimental fact, as opposed to the universal experiential maxim.

The AS harvest instructions are *experientia* in Dear's sense. "Gather betony in August without iron" is not the record of a specific observation; it is the distilled residue of generations of practice, encoded in the form that made it transmittable and memorable. To evaluate this instruction against the criteria of the post-Baconian experiment — controlled conditions, singular observation, named witness — is to apply an anachronistic epistemological standard. The instruction was never trying to be a laboratory result. It was trying to be a maxim. As a maxim, it succeeds.

### Artisanal empiricism and the knowledge of practitioners

Pamela Smith's *The Body of the Artisan* (2004) recovers a form of empiricism that existed alongside and often in tension with learned natural philosophy: the **knowledge of practitioners**. Apothecaries, surgeons, metallurgists, and herbalists knew things about their materials that no philosophical text recorded — because they worked with their hands, because they handled materials over decades, because they observed outcomes and adjusted practice. Their knowledge was embodied and tacit, not propositional. But it was rigorously empirical.

Smith traces how this artisanal knowledge became a resource for the "new philosophy" of the Scientific Revolution, as natural philosophers like Bacon and Boyle turned to craftsmen for knowledge of materials and processes. The experimental method was *partly constructed from* artisanal practice — from knowledge embedded in workshop procedure rather than philosophical text.

The AS leech is closer to Smith's artisan than to either the classical philosopher or the modern pharmacologist. Their knowledge was embodied, accumulated through observation, and encoded in procedural form. "Pick it before sunrise" is not a deduction from theory; it is knowledge of the kind a skilled practitioner accumulates over years of noticing which treatments work and when. This is **artisanal empiricism**, and it meets Smith's definition of empirical knowledge as rigorously as any laboratory result.

### Bacon's method as structured ritual

The irony of the rational/magical dichotomy becomes sharpest when placed against Francis Bacon's *Novum Organum* (1620). Bacon's contribution is often described as the formulation of inductive method — the systematic collection of instances, the construction of Tables of Presence, Absence, and Degrees, the induction of principles from particulars. But Bacon's method is itself a **structured procedure**: a finite series of steps, each with specific rules, designed to produce reliable knowledge about nature. It is, in Pasquinelli's terms, an algorithm.

The structural parallel to the harvest instructions is not superficial. Both are:

- **Finite step procedures** for extracting reliable information from nature
- **Economical** — specifying the minimum intervention needed to isolate the relevant variable
- **Transmittable without requiring theoretical understanding** — Bacon's Rules can be followed without knowing why they work
- **Designed to correct for systematic error** — Bacon's Idols of the Tribe, Cave, Marketplace, and Theatre are exactly analogous to the biases that harvest timing corrects for (measuring the wrong compound, at the wrong time, with a contaminating tool)

The harvest instruction that specifies "before sunrise" for chamomile is controlling for a variable — time of day — that affects compound composition. Bacon's Table of Degrees records how much of a phenomenon is present under varying conditions. Both are algorithms for holding variables constant in order to isolate the relevant effect. Bacon provides a metalanguage for what he is doing; the AS practitioners did not need to. The knowledge is structurally the same. The *rhetoric* of epistemological authority is different.

### The ritual structure of modern science

Shapin and Schaffer's *Leviathan and the Air-Pump* (1985) argued that the production of scientific knowledge in the seventeenth century was a social and performative practice — that "matters of fact" were produced through specific rituals of witnessing, staging, and credentialing. Boyle's air-pump experiments required not just apparatus but *performance*: who could be present, what counted as adequate witnessing, how results would be reported, which voices would be credited. The scientific experiment is **a ritual for producing authorized knowledge**.

This argument extends forward to the present. The modern randomized controlled trial — the gold standard of pharmaceutical evidence — has an explicit ritual structure: randomization ceremony, blinding protocol, pre-registration of hypotheses, statistical thresholds for significance, peer review. None of these elements is purely technical; all involve social conventions about who counts as an authoritative witness, what constitutes adequate evidence, and which deviations from protocol invalidate a result. The RCT is, in Pasquinelli's terms, an algorithm — and it is, in Shapin and Schaffer's terms, also a ritual.

The harvest instruction is an algorithm, and also a ritual. The difference between them is not the presence or absence of ritual structure — both have it. The difference is **which ritual has been granted epistemological authority** by the dominant scientific culture of the twentieth century. This is Elshakry's point extended into methodology: the AS harvest instruction was dismissed not because it lacked ritual structure but because it performed the *wrong* ritual — the wrong social conventions, the wrong authorizing community, the wrong textual genre.

### Tacit knowledge and the limits of explicit science

Michael Polanyi's *The Tacit Dimension* (1966) provides a final theoretical resource. Polanyi's argument — "we know more than we can tell" — describes a genuine epistemological phenomenon: expert practitioners possess knowledge that exceeds what they can articulate in explicit propositions. A skilled diagnostician who "just knows" a patient is seriously ill before any test confirms it is deploying tacit knowledge accumulated through years of pattern recognition. This knowledge is real, is often superior to propositional knowledge, and is not accessible to introspection or explicit formulation.

The AS harvest instruction encodes tacit knowledge in explicit procedural form. The practitioner who developed the instruction through generations of practice knew that betony harvested in August without iron worked better — but could not have articulated the phenolic chemistry underlying that knowledge. What the instruction preserves is the *procedure* extracted from the *tacit knowledge*, translated into transmittable form. This is not a degradation of knowledge; it is an achievement. It is the same achievement Bacon was trying to accomplish with his tables of induction: making explicit what practitioners knew implicitly. The AS leech got there first, by a different route, in a different genre — and the knowledge survived.
